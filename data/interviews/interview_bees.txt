Interviewer 1: How do you survey for native bees? Like, what's the methodology?

Beekeeper: A few methods. We use bee bowls — small colored bowls filled with soapy water — to passively trap bees during peak activity. We do net sweeps in standardized transects. Beekeeper: It is. And it's what distinguishes what we do from a lot of pollinator outreach work. Interviewer 1: Let's keep walking — what's this section?

Beekeeper: This is what I call the rough corner. It's intentionally messier than the meadow. There's deadwood here, brush piles, leaf litter that I leave undisturbed, patches of bare ground. Ground-nesting bees — which is actually the majority of native species — need bare or sparsely vegetated soil. So if you want a full community of native bees, you need all of those features. Most pollinator gardens get the flower part right and forget the nesting part. The result is bees pass through to feed but they don't establish populations because there's nowhere to live. Interviewer 2: That's a great way to put it. Critical infrastructure.

Beekeeper: It is critical infrastructure. The dead trees, the leaf piles, the bare patches — these are the housing stock for native pollinators. Take them away and the bees have nowhere to live. Interviewer 2: What do those partnerships look like in practice?

Beekeeper: Several forms. One — spray notification. This was the first big breakthrough. Most farmers around here spray multiple times during the growing season. Some of those sprays are toxic to bees. When farmers spray without warning, my hives suffer. Bees fly out, collect contaminated pollen and nectar, bring it back to the hive, and you lose entire colonies. It's devastating. So we set up a notification system. I gave the farmers in this radius my phone number. They text me when they're going to spray. I close my hives — literally cover the entrances — during and after the spray until it's safe. The system isn't perfect. Sometimes they forget to text. Sometimes the spray happens with less notice than they planned. But mostly, it works. And it's saved my operation more than once.

Interviewer 1: They actually text you?

Beekeeper: They do. Now. It took years to build that practice. But yes — I get texts. Sometimes the texts are very practical and short. "Spraying tomorrow morning, you should close up." Sometimes they're a little more conversational. I appreciate every one of them. Each text represents a relationship that had to be built and trust that had to be earned.

Interviewer 2: That's extraordinary, honestly.

Beekeeper: It's the part of the work I'm most proud of, actually. More than the bee species count, more than the nonprofit revenue, more than the publications. The fact that grown men who run thousand-acre conventional operations text a single woman with a PhD before they spray pesticides — that's a real social shift. That's people changing their behavior to accommodate ecological reality. And once they're doing it for me, the conversation can extend. We can talk about other things they could do. Field margins. Reduced spraying. Habitat installation. The texting is the gateway.

Interviewer 1: What about field margins?

Beekeeper: This was the second breakthrough. So conventional row crop farms in this area typically have very tidy field margins. The crop goes right up to a narrow strip of mowed grass, then the road or the fence. Any plants in the margins that aren't grass get mowed down. The reasoning — looks neat, doesn't harbor pests, doesn't compete with the crop. But those margins, left to grow, are some of the best potential pollinator habitat available on these farms. There's no irrigation cost, no input cost, and the farmer isn't losing crop because they're not growing crop there anyway. So the proposition was — what if you mowed your field margins less aggressively? Let some of the wildflowers and forbs grow. Maybe even seed in some native species. You'd be adding pollinator habitat at almost zero cost to your operation.

Interviewer 2: And they went for it?

Beekeeper: Some did. I started with the most receptive ones. The two farmers closest to me agreed to a trial — they stopped mowing a half-mile of field margin for one growing season. We monitored the bee activity. The increase was substantial. I shared the data with them. And then they extended the practice to more of their fields. Other farmers in the area heard about it. We had a couple of farm tours where I walked farmers through what was happening on the trial fields. And gradually adoption spread. Right now I'd estimate we have over 40 miles of conserved field margin across farms in this region. Forty miles. Of habitat that didn't exist five years ago.

Interviewer 1: Wow.

Beekeeper: I know. It's been amazing to watch. And that's just the visible part. The less visible part is that the farmers are now thinking about pollinators when they make decisions. They're aware of bloom timing. They're noticing pollinators on their crops. They're modifying their practices in small ways they wouldn't have before. The cultural shift is bigger than the acreage of margins. Once you start seeing pollinators, you can't unsee them.

Interviewer 2: Are there other things you've done with farmers?

Beekeeper: A few. We've helped a couple of farms install cover crop mixes that bloom — adding pollinator-supporting species to their winter cover crops. We've installed several hedgerow plantings on field edges where farmers wanted something more substantial than just unmowed margin. We've helped one farm transition a low-productivity corner field to permanent pollinator habitat and apply for cost-share through NRCS. So a range of things, depending on what each farmer wants and can do. The key is — I don't come in with a checklist. Every farm is different. Beekeeper: Mostly. There's still some condescension from pure academics toward applied work — the sense that we're not doing real science if we're working on real-world problems. Interviewer 1: Let's walk down to that section over there — what is it?

Beekeeper: Yeah, this is — this is one of my favorite spots. It's the pond. Or the wet area, really. It was a low spot when I bought the place that held water seasonally. I let it become a true wetland over the years. Pulled out a couple of small drainage tiles that the previous owner had installed. Let it fill. Planted native wetland species around the edges. Now it's a thriving wetland habitat — about a quarter acre. Frogs. Dragonflies. Lots of bird species use it for water. And specific pollinators that depend on wetland flowering species — there's a beautiful native sunflower that grows here that I almost never see elsewhere. The variety of habitats on this small property is part of what makes it so productive ecologically. You don't need a lot of land. You need diverse land.

Interviewer 2: That's a powerful idea — diverse land instead of a lot of land.

Beekeeper: It really is. Most landscape ecology assumes that you need vast acreage to support biodiversity. And for some species, you do. Large carnivores. Migratory birds that need huge stopover habitats. But for most insects and many bird species, what matters is the diversity within a smaller area, not the total size. A nine-acre property with eight different habitat types — wetland, meadow, hedgerow, forest edge, brush pile, garden, orchard, lawn — can support more biodiversity than 90 acres of single-habitat monoculture. So the message I bring to homeowners and small landowners is — your land matters. You don't need to own a forest. You need to think about your land as a series of small habitats and design accordingly.

Interviewer 1: Are there orchard trees here too?

Beekeeper: A small orchard, yes. Mostly fruit trees that support pollinators. Some apples, some plums, a couple of pear trees, some pawpaws which I love. They flower in early spring when the bees are coming out of winter. The orchard gives me some fruit and gives the bees an early-season food source. Combined, the property has been carefully designed so that something is blooming from late February through November. That's a long blooming season, especially for our area. Most landscapes have a few weeks of major bloom and then nothing. Designing for continuous bloom is a major intervention.

Interviewer 2: Let's circle back to the spray notification story for a second — that one really stands out. Has anyone ever messed it up badly?

Beekeeper: A few times, yes. I lost an entire hive once because a farmer forgot to text me. He felt terrible. Interviewer 1: Have you ever lost bees to the point where you thought about stopping?

Beekeeper: Not really. Even with the bad years, the overall trend has been positive. I've gotten better at managing risk. I've redistributed hives away from the most vulnerable spots. I've worked with farmers to time spray applications when fewer pollinators are active. So the losses haven't accumulated to the point of breaking the operation. It can spiral.

Interviewer 2: What about climate change — how is that affecting things?

Beekeeper: It's affecting things significantly. The bloom times are shifting. I have native plants flowering two or three weeks earlier than I would have predicted ten years ago. Which can create mismatches — a bee species that emerged on a certain timeline may now emerge when its host plant has already finished blooming. Or vice versa. So the synchronization that evolved over thousands of years is breaking down. We see fewer of some species that depended on tight synchrony with specific plants. We see more of generalist species that can use whatever's blooming when they're active. So the bee community composition is shifting toward generalists and away from specialists. Which is concerning because specialists are often the most ecologically important species — they do work that no other species does.

Interviewer 1: That's sobering.

Beekeeper: It is. But it's also part of why this work matters. The more habitat we maintain — diverse habitat — the more resilience the system has. Generalist habitats with high diversity buffer the impacts of climate-driven mismatches. So building habitat across landscapes isn't just nice-to-have anymore. It's adaptive infrastructure. We're building the system that will let pollinators persist through the climate disruptions coming. That's how I frame it for farmers who are starting to feel climate effects in their own operations. The habitat work isn't separate from climate adaptation. It is climate adaptation.

Interviewer 2: Are there policy changes that would make your work easier?

Beekeeper: Several. Stronger pollinator protection in pesticide labeling — the current labels are inadequate for protecting native bees, even when they protect honeybees somewhat. Better cost-share programs for habitat installation on farms. Expanded support for state-level pollinator programs. And — this is more controversial — restrictions on the use of neonicotinoid pesticides, which are extremely toxic to pollinators and persist in soil and plants for long periods. The EU has banned several neonics for outdoor agricultural use. We haven't. We should. But I don't see that happening in the current political environment.

Interviewer 1: What about technology and pollinator monitoring — is that helping?

Beekeeper: It's improving. There are new acoustic monitoring techniques that can detect bee species from their sound signatures. There are camera trap systems that can record visitation patterns. There are eDNA techniques that can detect species from environmental samples. So the science is advancing rapidly. We're going to know more about pollinators in the next ten years than we've known in the previous fifty. Which means we'll be able to design conservation interventions much more precisely. So I'm hopeful about the trajectory of the science as well as the practice.

Interviewer 2: What's your closing message to a young person thinking about doing what you do?

Beekeeper: Several things. One — you don't have to choose between science and activism. You can do both. You can do rigorous research and you can apply it to real-world problems. The distinction between pure and applied science is mostly artificial and increasingly outdated. Two — local matters. You don't need to save the world. You need to take care of the place you're in. Pick a region. Get to know it deeply. Build relationships with the people who live there. That depth of local knowledge is more valuable than broad shallow exposure. Three — be patient. Real change is slow. Especially relationship-based change. The farmer who took two years to talk to me was worth those two years. You can't speed up trust. So do the work, show up consistently, and let time do its part. And four — find joy in the work. There's so much hard news in conservation. You can't sustain decades of work on grim determination alone. You need the joy of watching the bees emerge in spring. The joy of holding a queen bee in your hand. The joy of seeing a meadow you planted come back fuller each year. Cultivate the joy. It's the fuel.

Interviewer 1: That's beautiful.

Beekeeper: It's what I try to live by. Okay — we should head back. The light's changing and I want to give you a jar of honey to take with you. The wildflower honey from this property is — I won't say it's the best honey you'll ever have, but it'll be in the conversation.

Interviewer 2: We can't wait.

Beekeeper: Come on, then. Watch your footing on the way back. The path narrows here.

[They walk back toward the house.]

Beekeeper: One more thing I want to say. People sometimes ask me if all this matters in the big picture. With ecosystems collapsing globally, with climate change accelerating, with biodiversity crashing — does my nine acres of pollinator habitat actually matter? And my answer is yes. It matters because every place that's well-cared-for is a place that's not damaged. It matters because the practices we develop here can be replicated elsewhere. It matters because the farmers I work with are changing how they manage thousands of acres collectively. It matters because the young people who learn here go on to do their own work. And it matters because — even if the global picture is bad — the existence of healthy ecosystems is good in itself. Beauty matters. Life matters. So even if everything else is going badly, the bees on my property today are alive and well, and that has value, intrinsically. That's enough reason to do the work. Don't ever let anyone tell you that local work doesn't matter. It does. It always does.

Interviewer 1: Thank you. That's perfect.

Beekeeper: You're welcome. Let me get that honey.
