Interviewer 1: Thanks so much for letting us come out today.

Farmer: Yeah, no problem. Come on in, or — actually, you want to walk first while the light's still good? It looks like it's gonna rain later. Forecast keeps changing but I don't trust it.

Interviewer 2: Yeah, let's walk first.

Farmer: Okay. Let me grab my boots. The ground out back is still soft from last week. Hold on. [Pause] Okay. So you want me to just — start? Or you want to ask me stuff?

Interviewer 1: Maybe just tell us where we are and what we're looking at.

Farmer: Alright. So we're in Robeson County, North Carolina. Right outside Pembroke. About 30 minutes from Lumberton. We're in the heart of the Lumbee community here — I'm Lumbee, my family's Lumbee going back generations. The land we're standing on right now is what's left of what used to be — what used to be a bigger operation. I had 64 acres at the peak. Right now I've got 28. I'll explain that as we walk. Come this way, watch your step around the gate, the latch don't catch right anymore and I haven't fixed it.

Interviewer 2: How long have you been here?

Farmer: This land's been in my family since the 1920s. So a hundred years roughly. My great-grandfather bought the original parcel — he was a tenant farmer for most of his life and then he got enough together to buy his own piece. Which was huge for a Lumbee family at that time. The Lumbee community has been on this land since long before that, but ownership specifically — the formal title — that's been hard for our people for a long time. So that purchase mattered. My grandfather expanded it. My father held onto it. And then I took it over when my dad got sick in 2012. I was 35 then. I'm 48 now.

Interviewer 1: What did you grow up doing on the farm?

Farmer: Everything. Tobacco, mostly, when I was little. My dad was tobacco. That was the cash crop for this whole region for generations. Then the tobacco buyouts happened in the early 2000s and a lot of folks got out. My dad transitioned partly into vegetables, partly into hay, kept a few cows. Smaller scale. And that's roughly what I was running when I took over. Vegetables for market, hay for sale, a small beef operation maybe 20 head at any given time. We had it. It was working.

Interviewer 2: And what happened?

Farmer: [Long pause] A lot of things. None of them by themselves would have done it. All of them together did. Where do you want me to start.

Interviewer 1: Wherever makes sense.

Farmer: Okay. So — Matthew. Hurricane Matthew in 2016. That was the first major hit. Robeson County got hammered. The Lumber River flooded historically. We lost a lot of crop that fall, lost some hay, lost about half my fencing. Insurance covered some of it but not most. And we were still digging out from that when Florence hit in 2018. Two years apart. Florence was worse than Matthew. The water came up onto land that had never flooded before. I lost everything I had in the ground that year. The cows had to be moved to higher ground and one of them broke a leg in transport and had to be put down. The barn lost half its roof. I spent the next year rebuilding with whatever I had.

Interviewer 2: That's — I can't imagine.

Farmer: Yeah. And it wasn't just my farm. The whole county was reeling. The Lumbee community especially — we got hit really hard, both storms. A lot of homes lost. A lot of family farms hurt the way mine was. And the federal response was — it was inadequate, I'll just say that. The disaster relief that did come through took years. Some of it never came. I'm still waiting on some paperwork from Florence that's never gonna get processed.

Interviewer 1: How were you funding things while you waited?

Farmer: I went into debt. I took out a USDA emergency loan after Florence. I put farm equipment on credit cards because I needed to keep operating and I couldn't wait for the loan to clear. And then COVID hit in 2020 and the markets I was selling into shifted — restaurants were my biggest buyers for the vegetables, and the restaurants closed. So I lost that income stream right when I was trying to climb back. Some of those restaurants didn't reopen. The ones that did were buying less. So my customer base contracted.

Interviewer 2: Did you find other markets?

Farmer: I tried. I started doing farmers markets in Lumberton and Fayetteville. Did a little CSA. Some direct sales. But you can't replace the volume from restaurants overnight, especially when you're not set up for direct retail. I'd been a wholesale operation, mostly. So the equipment, the storage, the marketing — none of it was right for direct. I had to learn a whole different business. Which I did. But it took years and meanwhile the debt kept growing.

Interviewer 1: When did you start having to sell land?

Farmer: 2022. I'd been holding on, holding on. And then my dad — my dad's still alive, he's 78 now, lives with my brother in Lumberton — but his medical bills were getting bad and Medicare wasn't covering everything and I was helping cover the gaps. And on top of the farm debt, that was just — it was too much. I sold the first 12 acres that fall. To a developer who'd been calling for years. Used the money to pay down farm debt and help with dad. And then I sold another 24 in early 2024. Same buyer. He's been buying up land all through this area. He's building houses on some of it, holding some of it.

Interviewer 2: How did that feel?

Farmer: [Long pause] You don't want to know how it felt. There's no language for it. That land was — it was family. It had been worked by my grandparents, my parents. My kids played on it when they were little. I learned to drive a tractor on it. And then I had to sign it over to some developer from Charlotte who'd never set foot on it before he showed up with the check. And once it's gone, it's gone. That land's a subdivision now. Or it will be. They're clearing it this year.

Interviewer 1: Have you driven by since?

Farmer: I drive by every day. There's no way around it without adding twenty minutes to every trip. So I see it. I see the equipment they brought in. I see the trees coming down. I see them grading what used to be a pasture. And every time I see it I have to make peace with it all over again. Because the alternative — the alternative was losing all of it. Losing everything. So selling 36 acres to save the other 28 — that was the math. That was the only math that worked.

Interviewer 2: Was there ever a moment you thought about selling everything?

Farmer: Yes. There was a stretch in 2023 when I was — I was in a bad place. Mentally. I'd just sold the first parcel, the debt was still bad, I was exhausted, my marriage was struggling. My husband and I were not in a good place. And there was a week where I sat down with the developer's offer for the whole property and I came real close to signing. I could see the math working — pay off everything, walk away clean, start over somewhere with nothing weighing on me. And — I couldn't do it. I couldn't do it. Because once I'd signed that, there'd be nothing of my family on this land. A hundred years of my people, and I'd be the one who let it go. I couldn't be that person. So I tore up the offer and called him and told him no.

Interviewer 1: That must have been a hard call.

Farmer: It was the hardest call I've ever made. And then I had to figure out how to make it work without selling. Which is what we've been doing since. Selling the second 24 acres in 2024 was part of that. Diversifying the income was part of that. My husband going back to working in town was part of that. So a lot of pieces.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about your husband.

Farmer: His name is — let's just say his name's not important. He's not Lumbee, he's white, from up near Asheville originally. We met in college at UNC Pembroke. He's been farming with me since we got married in 2003. He's a good man. But the last few years have been hard on us both, in different ways. He's working at the Lowes in Lumberton now. Has been since 2023. Brings in steady income, which we needed. But it means he's not here on the farm during the day, so I'm running it myself most of the time. Which adds pressure. And it changes the marriage in ways neither of us expected. We've gone to counseling. We're better than we were a year ago. We're not where we want to be. We're working on it.

Interviewer 1: I appreciate you sharing that.

Farmer: Yeah. It's part of the story. I think folks reading about farms in trouble don't always realize what the trouble does to a household. It's not just a financial thing. It's everything. It's the way you eat dinner. It's the way you talk to each other. It's the way you sleep. So when I tell you we've been struggling, I mean — we've been struggling in every way you can struggle.

Interviewer 2: Do you have kids?

Farmer: Three. Two girls and a boy. They're 21, 18, and 15. The 21-year-old is at East Carolina now, studying nursing. The 18-year-old just started at UNC Pembroke this fall, hasn't picked a major yet. And the youngest is still in high school here in Pembroke. They all grew up on this farm and they're — I think they have complicated feelings about it. They saw the hard years. They saw me crying in the kitchen more than I want to admit. They saw their dad come home from Lowes exhausted. So the farm isn't romanticized for them the way it might be for kids in healthier farm situations. They love it but they're also a little wary of it.

Interviewer 1: Do you think any of them will come back?

Farmer: I don't know. Maybe my youngest. She's the one who actually likes the work. The older two are pretty clear that they want different lives. And I support that. I support them all. The land doesn't have to define them. I want them to be free. If one of them comes back, that's a gift. If none of them do — that's their right. I'd rather they be happy elsewhere than be miserable here trying to live my dream.

Interviewer 2: Let's walk back to what you're growing — what's the operation look like now, on the 28 acres?

Farmer: It's smaller and more focused than it used to be. I have — see those rows running back toward the tree line? That's our vegetable production. Mostly summer crops right now — tomatoes, peppers, squash, cucumbers, okra, sweet potatoes coming up later. I do all of it myself most days. I've got one part-time helper who comes Tuesdays and Saturdays during the growing season. She's a college student at UNC Pembroke who needed a job. She's good — she's learning fast.

Interviewer 1: How many acres of vegetables?

Farmer: About three, intensively. Which doesn't sound like much but it produces a lot. The thing about vegetables is they pack tight. Three acres of vegetables done well can outproduce ten acres done poorly. And the income per acre is higher. Which is part of why I shifted toward more vegetables and less hay over the years. The hay business doesn't pay enough per acre to justify keeping it going at small scale. So I cut my hay production. I still cut some for our own animals. But not for sale anymore.

Interviewer 2: And the cattle?

Farmer: I've got six head right now. Down from the 20 I used to have. That's been a big shift. The cattle were — they were both an income source and a way of managing land that wasn't in row production. Now I'm running just a few for our own beef and to keep the pastures from going to brush. It's not really commercial anymore. It's homestead-scale.

Interviewer 1: How do you sell what you do produce?

Farmer: Farmers markets, mostly. I do two markets a week — Lumberton on Wednesdays, Fayetteville on Saturdays. Direct retail at the farm a few days a week — I've got a little stand at the road that I open up Thursday and Friday afternoons. And I do a small subscription — about 15 families that get a vegetable box every week from June through October. That's it. Pretty modest. But the margins are better than they were when I was doing wholesale. I make more money per pound of tomatoes now than I used to.

Interviewer 2: That's interesting.

Farmer: It is. And it's part of what's let us keep going. When I was selling wholesale to restaurants, I was selling at maybe a third of what I get at farmers market. So the rebuild after COVID forced me into a more profitable model, even though the total volume is way down. I'd never have made that change voluntarily because the wholesale model was what I knew. But once the wholesale collapsed I had to rebuild differently. And the differently has actually been better in some ways. Not in every way. But in some.

Interviewer 1: Can you walk us through what your week looks like?

Farmer: Sure. Monday is recovery from the weekend market. I sleep a little later. I clean up the stand, process whatever didn't sell, do bookkeeping. Tuesday is field work — planting, weeding, harvesting for the road stand. My helper comes that day. Wednesday morning I'm setting up at Lumberton market. Spend the day there. Come home, decompress. Thursday and Friday are field work and the road stand. Saturday is Fayetteville market — that's the big one, I'm up before five getting loaded. I get home around three, exhausted. Sunday is church and family and one of the only days I try not to do anything farm. Though I usually end up doing something. The animals don't take Sundays off.

Interviewer 2: That sounds relentless.

Farmer: It is relentless. And I'm 48. I'm not 25. My body feels every hour of it. The mornings are hardest. Getting my back to work after a night of sleep is — yeah. Every morning is a project. And I think about that sometimes. Like, how many more years can I do this body? Five? Ten? Maybe ten if I'm lucky. And then what? That question keeps me up at night.

Interviewer 1: Do you have a plan for that?

Farmer: Not a real one. I think about training someone up. I think about leasing part of the land. I think about converting to something less physically demanding — maybe more permanent crops, fruit trees, something that doesn't require the daily intensive labor of vegetables. But I haven't committed to anything. The day-to-day takes all my energy. The long-term planning happens in the cracks. And there aren't many cracks.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the Lumbee community aspect of all this.

Farmer: Yeah, that's important. So the Lumbee community here is — we're family, basically. Whether we're related by blood or not, we know each other. Three generations of relationships, four, sometimes more. Everyone knows everyone. And that's been both a blessing and a complicated thing through all this. The blessing is — community support is real. When Florence hit, my Lumbee neighbors showed up. Without being asked. Brought food, brought tools, helped clear debris, helped me fix the barn. That's not abstract community, that's people in your driveway. I would not be standing on this land right now without that support.

Interviewer 1: What's the complicated part?

Farmer: The complicated part is that the same community has been losing land for generations. So when I sold parcels — when other folks sell parcels — it's not just an individual financial decision. It's part of a bigger pattern. The Lumbee community has been losing acreage steadily through development, through tax pressures, through inheritance complications. The Pembroke area has been growing fast. Property values are going up. Development is closing in. And every time a Lumbee family sells, the community loses a piece of itself. I felt that when I signed those papers. I felt like I was failing my people, even though my people understood why I had to do it. That weight is hard to describe.

Interviewer 2: Did people react to your sales?

Farmer: Most people knew what I'd been through. Most understood. There were a few — there were a few harder reactions. Family members who thought I should have held on, even if it meant losing everything. Or other Lumbee folks who saw the developer building subdivisions on what had been our land and felt the larger loss. I get it. I understand the feeling. I share the feeling. But I also had three kids to think about and a mortgage to pay. Sometimes the family interest and the community interest don't align perfectly. I had to choose family.

Interviewer 1: Has the federal recognition issue affected any of this for you?

Farmer: The Lumbee recognition fight has been going on my whole life and my parents' lives. We're recognized by the state of North Carolina but not fully recognized by the federal government in the way other tribes are. Which has consequences for everything — for healthcare, for education, for access to certain agricultural programs that are available to federally recognized tribes. So we've been excluded from a lot of resources that could have helped during the hard years. That's been a structural disadvantage. Not the only reason I had to sell land — but a piece of why my margin for error was thinner than other communities' might have been. The Lumbee Recognition Act has been in Congress for decades. It comes close to passing sometimes and then doesn't. I'd love to see it actually pass in my lifetime. It would help. Not just me. The whole community.

Interviewer 2: Let's walk over to the vegetable beds. What are we looking at here?

Farmer: This is mostly summer stuff right now. So these rows are tomatoes — I've got — let me count — eight varieties this year. Some heirlooms, some hybrids, some that I've been saving seed from for years. The yellow ones over there are Brandywines, those have done really well for me. The dark ones are Cherokee Purples — those are popular with the market customers. Then peppers — I've got sweet bells, banana peppers, hot peppers, some specialty ones I grow for restaurants that came back after COVID. Then this whole section is squash and zucchini. The cucumbers are on those trellises further down. And then I've got the okra coming in on the back side which we'll walk to.

Interviewer 1: How do you manage all of this by yourself?

Farmer: Carefully. And imperfectly. I prioritize. The tomatoes get the most attention because they're the biggest seller. Squash and zucchini almost manage themselves once they're going. Cucumbers need consistent harvest or they get oversize fast. So I time my walks through the field around what needs attention most. Some things slip. There are weeks where weeds get ahead of me. Years where pest pressure was higher than I could keep up with. The intensive vegetable model assumes a certain amount of labor, and I'm one person. So I'm always running short of what the system really wants. But I do what I can.

Interviewer 2: What practices do you use? Like, are you organic, are you spraying?

Farmer: I'm not certified organic. I use organic-approved methods on probably 80% of what I do. I'll spray when I have to. I had a really bad blight year on tomatoes a few summers back and I had to use a fungicide to save the crop. I'd rather not, but losing the whole crop wasn't an option for me financially. So I make those calls case by case. I tell customers exactly what I do. They appreciate the honesty more than they'd appreciate a certification label.

Interviewer 1: How's the soil here?

Farmer: It's good. It's coastal plain soil — sandy loam mostly. Drains well, holds nutrients okay. Tobacco country soil, traditionally. I've been working on it since I took over — adding organic matter, cover cropping when I can, rotating crops. The years of flooding from Matthew and Florence actually deposited some sediment that improved certain areas. Silver linings, I guess. Mostly the land has been here, and the land has been farmed, for so long that the basics are pretty established. The land knows what it's doing. I'm just managing it.

Interviewer 2: That's a nice way to put it.

Farmer: It's how I think about it. The land doesn't need me to be brilliant. It needs me to pay attention and not screw it up. Most years I manage that. Some years I don't.

Interviewer 1: Have any of the regenerative agriculture practices made it into your operation?

Farmer: Some. I do cover crops in winter on a lot of the vegetable ground. I rotate aggressively — I don't put the same family of vegetables in the same bed two years in a row. I use compost instead of synthetic fertilizer where I can. I leave wild zones around the field edges for pollinators and beneficial insects. So yes, regenerative practices are present in what I do. But I don't call myself a regenerative farmer because — honestly, that label has gotten so commercialized I don't want to be associated with it. It used to mean something specific. Now it means whatever the person using it wants it to mean. So I just say I'm a small farmer who tries to do right by the land.

Interviewer 2: That's a fair critique of the term.

Farmer: I think so. I've watched the term "regenerative" get used by everyone from people doing genuine ecological work to massive corporations slapping it on packaging. It's worn out. The good practices haven't worn out — the practices are still good — but the label is tired. So I focus on practices and let other people fight about labels.

Interviewer 1: Let's walk to the back. What's out here?

Farmer: This is the orchard area. I've been slowly putting in fruit trees over the last six years. The idea was — I knew I couldn't keep doing vegetables this intensively forever. So I started planting things that would eventually require less labor. Peaches, plums, figs, a few apples though apples don't love it here, some pears. They're not really producing yet, most of them. Another two, three years before I'm getting meaningful fruit from them. But that's the plan. As I age out of intensive vegetables, the orchard becomes a bigger piece of the operation. Lower labor, higher value per pound, and it ages into productivity instead of out.

Interviewer 2: Is that a regional fit, all these fruits?

Farmer: Mostly. The peaches and figs do really well here. Plums do okay. Pears are good. Apples are tougher because we don't get enough cold most winters anymore — climate's shifting. So I'm mostly working with what fits the climate. Some of the trees I picked specifically for heat and humidity tolerance. I'll know more in five years. Right now they're young enough that I'm still figuring it out.

Interviewer 1: How did you fund the orchard with everything else going on?

Farmer: Small steps. I planted a few trees a year, whatever I could afford. Used some grant money I got from a small farms program one year. Took advantage of some discount stock from a nursery that was clearing inventory. So it's been gradual. The orchard isn't the result of one big plan. It's the result of many small decisions over years. Which is mostly how everything has happened on this farm, actually. Big plans don't survive contact with reality. Small decisions accumulate.

Interviewer 2: Have you had access to any grants or programs that helped?

Farmer: Some. Not as much as I would have liked. I've gotten a couple of small NRCS cost-share contracts over the years. I got a small farms grant from the state ag department once. I qualified for some socially disadvantaged farmer programs because of my Lumbee heritage, though navigating those required more paperwork than the money was worth in some cases. And I got the USDA emergency loan after Florence, which kept me alive but also added debt that's still on the books. So the programs have helped marginally. They haven't been transformative. They haven't been enough to prevent the land sales.

Interviewer 1: What would help — if you could design support for farmers in your situation?

Farmer: Real disaster recovery that actually arrives quickly. Programs that account for compound impact — when you've had two hurricanes and a pandemic in four years, the standard disaster response isn't enough. Long-term low-interest credit for land retention specifically — programs designed to help farmers keep land when they're under economic pressure. Better recognition for Lumbee and other state-recognized tribes so we have access to the federal Indian Country programs. Healthcare costs that don't bankrupt farm families. And honestly — protection from the development pressure. There are land trust models that work for keeping farmland in production. We need more of those, more accessible, more affordable. Right now my only realistic exit options are sell to a developer or sell to a neighboring farmer. Neither one keeps the land in my family. A land trust option would change that calculation.

Interviewer 2: That's really substantive.

Farmer: It's what I've been thinking about for years. Nobody asks. So most of the policy conversation happens without input from the farmers actually living through this. We have ideas. We have things that would help. We just don't have the time or the access to push them.

Interviewer 1: Let's circle back to the personal side a little. How are you doing — really?

Farmer: Better than I was. Honestly. The lowest point was 2023. Since then we've stabilized, financially mostly. My husband's job helps. The smaller farm is more manageable than the bigger one was. I'm sleeping better. The marriage is recovering. So I'm — I'm functional. I'm okay. I'm not thriving the way I'd want to be. But I'm not falling apart anymore. Which after a few years of falling apart, feels like a lot.

Interviewer 2: Where do you get strength from?

Farmer: My community. My family. The land itself. My faith — I'm Methodist, that's been important. The Lumbee Methodist tradition is — it's a particular thing. Worship in our community is a real source of strength. I have prayer warriors who have carried me through more than they know. Beyond that — I get strength from work. Honest work in front of you, that you can see done at the end of the day. Even when the work is exhausting, doing it feels better than not doing it. The body wants the work even when the mind is tired.

Interviewer 1: That's beautiful.

Farmer: That's how I've been raised. Lumbee culture is — there's a deep value placed on work and on land and on family. Those values have sustained generations of us through everything. Through poverty, through discrimination, through disasters. We work and we love each other and we hold on to the land. And when those three things are alive, we make it through. So I lean on what my people have always leaned on.

Interviewer 2: What do you want people to understand about your situation that they might not?

Farmer: That land loss isn't just an economic story. It's a cultural one. When a Lumbee family loses land in this county, something irreplaceable is gone. The connection to ancestors, the memory of generations, the physical place where your people lived and farmed and raised children — none of that gets recovered. The developer buys the land and clears the trees and builds houses and the new residents have no idea what was there before. They don't know the names of the people whose hands worked that soil. They don't know the stories. They don't know what was lost. So the loss is invisible to them and visible to us. And that asymmetry — that's part of what makes it hard.

Interviewer 1: That's powerful.

Farmer: I think it's true for any community that's been losing land. Black farmers, Indigenous farmers, immigrant farmers — anywhere a community has been displaced. The land changes hands and the cultural memory dies with it unless someone fights to keep it alive. That's part of why I'm willing to do interviews like this. Not because I want my own story told. But because — these stories accumulate. They build a record. They make it harder for the loss to be invisible.

Interviewer 2: That's why we appreciate you doing it.

Farmer: I appreciate you coming. And — listen, I want to say something specifically. You're from a university. The university has a relationship with this community that's complicated. UNC Pembroke is — that's our institution. It started as the Indian Normal School in 1887 to train Lumbee teachers. So the school is ours. But research from outside institutions has not always served this community well. Researchers have come, taken what they wanted, written what they wrote, and gone. So I'm watching how you handle this. I'm trusting you. I'd ask you to honor that trust. Don't write about us like we're a tragic curiosity. Write about us like we're people doing what we can in a hard situation.

Interviewer 1: That's a really important thing to hear. We'll do our best.

Farmer: I appreciate that. Okay — should we walk back? It's getting hot.

Interviewer 2: Yeah, let's head back.

[On the walk back.]

Farmer: One more thing I want to say. I've been pretty heavy in this conversation. I want to leave you with something else too. Even with everything I've described — even with the losses and the debt and the body wearing down — I love this. I love this work. I love walking my field in the morning. I love the smell of tomato leaves on my hands. I love that I'm still here, that my family is still on this land, that my children grew up knowing where they came from. So please don't write me up as just a sad story. The sadness is real. But so is the love. Both things are true at the same time.

Interviewer 1: That's beautifully said.

Farmer: It's how I really feel. The hard and the beautiful at once. That's farming. That's life out here. That's being Lumbee on land that's been ours for longer than the country has been a country. So when you tell this story — tell both halves.

Interviewer 2: We will.

Farmer: Alright. Come on inside, I made tea. And I want to send you with some tomatoes. We've got more than we can sell this week.

Interviewer 1: That'd be wonderful. Thank you.

Farmer: Of course. Come on.
