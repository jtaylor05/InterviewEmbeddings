Interviewer 1: Can you describe what a food forest actually is, for people who don't know?

Horticulturist: Sure. So a food forest is a designed agricultural system that mimics the structure of a natural forest, but composed of plants that are useful to humans — food, medicine, fiber, that kind of thing. You have multiple layers. You have a canopy layer of tall trees — usually fruit and nut trees. Below that you have a sub-canopy of smaller trees. Below that shrubs. Below that herbaceous plants. Below that ground covers and root crops. And then vines climbing through. So instead of a single crop on a flat plane, you have stacked production across multiple vertical layers. The whole system is perennial, mostly. Once it's established, it requires much less labor than annual agriculture. And it builds ecosystem function — soil health, biodiversity, water retention — while producing food. So it's a hybrid of forestry and food production.

Interviewer 2: How does it compare to traditional farming?

Horticulturist: Well — the productivity per acre is lower than industrial agriculture if you're measuring calories. That's just true. We're not going to feed America with food forests. But the productivity is high in other ways. The diversity is way higher. The labor required is way lower once established. The ecosystem services — carbon sequestration, water filtration, pollinator habitat, wildlife — are vastly greater. And the food is — it's a different kind of food. It's not commodity calories. It's whole foods with deep nutritional density, picked at peak ripeness, eaten in season. That's a different category of food than what comes from industrial monoculture.

Interviewer 1: How do you decide what to plant in something like this?

Horticulturist: A lot of factors. We start with site — what does the land actually want to grow? What's the soil, the moisture, the sun exposure, the microclimates? Then we look at the layers — what do we need in canopy, sub-canopy, shrub, herb, and ground cover layers? Then we think about ecological function — what plants will support each other, what supports pollinators, what fixes nitrogen, what brings nutrients up from deep soil. Then we layer in human use — what produces food that people want to eat? What produces medicine? What produces useful materials? And there are constraints — what's actually available for us to source, what's affordable, what's tested for this climate. So the design is really layered. The original master plan for this site took the founding team almost two years to develop. They worked with a permaculture designer. They consulted with extension. They visited other established food forests. And then we've been refining it for seven years.

Interviewer 2: Do you give the food away? You said earlier you don't sell.

Horticulturist: We give all of it away. That's been our model from the start and we've stuck with it. About 60% of what we harvest goes to local food pantries. Interviewer 1: Why give it away?

Horticulturist: Several reasons. One — the church believes that food is a gift. That's their theological framing. Food is meant to be shared, not transacted. Two — selling food at this scale and variety would be a logistical nightmare. The processing, the licensing, the packaging, the marketing. None of us want to be doing that. We want to grow food. Three — there's something about food given freely that changes its meaning. Interviewer 2: Do you think food forests could ever scale up to be a major part of food production?

Horticulturist: I don't, honestly. And I want to be careful how I say this because I love this work and I believe in it. But the math of feeding 330 million Americans with food forests is — it's not possible. The land area, the labor, the consumer behavior changes required would all be enormous. And the productivity per acre for staple calories — grains, beans, that kind of thing — is much lower than industrial monoculture. So I don't think the answer to feeding the world is more food forests.

Interviewer 1: What is the answer then?

Horticulturist: I don't know if there's a single answer. I think industrial agriculture is here to stay in some form. We'll be eating commodity grains and proteins for the foreseeable future. The question is whether we can shift the proportion. Whether we can have more food forests, more diversified farms, more community gardens, more orchards, more pasture-based meat — alongside industrial agriculture, gradually displacing some of its volume. So it's not food forests replacing industrial ag. It's food forests as part of a more diverse food landscape. And I think they have value even at small scale. They show people what's possible. They teach. They feed people who otherwise wouldn't have access to fresh food. They build community. They support biodiversity. They sequester carbon. Even if they're not feeding the world, they're doing important work.

Interviewer 2: That's a really honest assessment.

Horticulturist: I think it has to be honest. Overselling food forests as the answer to global food challenges does the movement a disservice because it makes us look unserious. We have to acknowledge what these systems can and can't do. And what they can do — provide real food to local communities, model ecological relationships, teach people about the natural world, build resilience in regional food systems — is plenty. We don't need to claim more than that.

We're using all the modern knowledge we have — ecological science, soil biology, plant breeding, climate research, design principles. We're integrating that with traditional knowledge and with what the land teaches us. So it's actually one of the most cutting-edge fields, intellectually. It's just slow. It looks like old practice because it involves trees and dirt. But the thinking behind it is current and rigorous. So I'd want people to take it seriously as a practice and not dismiss it as romantic.

Interviewer 2: What would you say to a young person thinking about doing what you do?

Horticulturist: Come work somewhere first. Don't romanticize it. Spend a season at a food forest or a farm. See if you actually like the work, because the work is the work. Most days you're not having epiphanies. You're pulling weeds and lifting buckets. So you need to actually enjoy the physical work. Second — get used to making less money than your peers. Or at least less money than your training would otherwise have you make. This work doesn't pay well, generally. So you have to be okay with simplicity. Third — find a community. This is not solo work, even when you're working alone. The community of practitioners is small and generous and you need to be in it. And fourth — be patient. With the plants, with the land, with yourself. Real change takes time. The food forest you start today will be transforming the year you die. That's the timeline. Get comfortable with it.

Interviewer 1: This has been incredible. Thank you so much.

Horticulturist: Yeah, thank you. I enjoyed it. And — wait, before you go, I want to feed you. I'll pull some persimmons. They're still a little astringent but you'll get a taste. And take some pears with you. We had a good year for pears.

Interviewer 2: That's so kind of you.

Horticulturist: It's how we do things. You came to the land, the land has things to give. That's the rule.

Interviewer 1: Can you tell us more about pawpaws? You've mentioned them a few times.

Horticulturist: Yeah, I'm honestly obsessed with pawpaws. So pawpaw is the largest native fruit in North America. It grows wild across the eastern half of the country, from the Gulf Coast up into Ontario. And almost nobody knows what they are. They were a staple food for Indigenous peoples in this region for thousands of years. They were eaten by colonists. They were sold in markets through the 1800s. And then they basically disappeared from American culinary culture in the 20th century because they don't ship well and they have a short shelf life. So commercial agriculture couldn't figure out how to monetize them and they were dropped. But the tree is here. The fruit is here. It just got forgotten.

Interviewer 2: What does it taste like?

Horticulturist: People describe it differently. The most common description is — a mix of mango, banana, and custard. It's tropical-tasting, which surprises people because it's growing in Maryland. It has a creamy texture, almost like a custard once it's fully ripe. And it has this lingering aftertaste that's hard to describe — almost floral. It's a remarkable fruit. And the trees are easy to grow once they're established. They want shade when young. They're naturally understory. So they fit perfectly into a food forest design — you plant them under the canopy and they fruit beautifully.

Interviewer 1: Why aren't more people growing them?

Horticulturist: Because the commercial system doesn't reward it. You can't ship them — they bruise easily and they're ripe for maybe a week. You can't store them well — they last a few days off the tree. So a commercial farm can't sell them through normal supply chains. Which makes them perfect for local, direct-to-consumer models. The only way to eat a pawpaw is to be near where it grew. Which actually argues for more local food systems, because they're the only systems that can deliver foods like this. Pawpaws are kind of a poster child for what we lose when we structure agriculture entirely around shipping and shelf life.

Interviewer 2: Are there other native species you're working with?

Horticulturist: Lots. We have a real focus on native species across the food forest. We've got American persimmon, which I mentioned. We've got American hazelnut. American beech, which is technically a food tree — the nuts are edible. American plum. Black walnut. Eastern red cedar — not a food but an important ecological species we keep around. Mayapple. Wild ginger. Ramps. Native pawpaws as we just discussed. Native blueberries. The whole eastern temperate forest has an incredible roster of food species that have been forgotten. Part of our work is recovering that knowledge and demonstrating it.

Interviewer 1: Is there an ecological argument for native species specifically?

Horticulturist: Yeah, a strong one. Native species support native ecosystems in ways that non-native species don't. The insects that evolved alongside them eat them. The birds that eat those insects depend on them. Pull out the native plants and the food web collapses upstream. Doug Tallamy at the University of Delaware has done foundational research on this — he's shown that native oaks, for example, support hundreds of species of caterpillars, while non-native ornamentals support only a handful. That difference matters enormously for bird populations. So when we use native species in the food forest, we're not just feeding humans. We're feeding the whole ecosystem.

Interviewer 2: But you also have non-native species, right? Asian persimmons and chestnut crosses.

Horticulturist: Right. We're not native purists. We use what works. The reality is that some of our most useful food species are non-native — apple, pear, peach, plum, most of the orchard standards. Almost everything humans eat has been moved around the world. So we use a mix. We weight toward natives where we can. We use non-natives where they fill important roles. We avoid invasive species entirely. And we're thoughtful about what we plant. It's not native versus non-native as a binary. It's a careful integration.

Interviewer 1: Do you propagate your own plants?

Horticulturist: Some. We have a small nursery area where we propagate trees from seed and from cuttings. We graft. The pawpaw seedlings here in this patch — most of them were grown from seed by volunteers from fruit we harvested in previous years. So we're closing the loop. We're not just consuming nursery stock. We're producing our own. And the genetics matter — the trees we propagate come from individuals that have proven themselves on this land. We're selecting for what works here. Over generations that becomes its own little localized lineage. The shorifa I mentioned that I have at my apartment — I'd love to be able to do that here too with truly tropical things. But we're outside the climate for that. So we focus on what fits.

Interviewer 2: How do you handle pests and diseases? You said earlier you don't really use chemicals.

Horticulturist: We don't use synthetic pesticides at all. We've used some organic-approved sprays in small ways for specific problems, but very rarely. Mostly we rely on diversity. The diversity itself is the pest management. When you have a polyculture with dozens of species mixed together, pest populations don't build up the same way they do in monocultures. The bugs that eat plant A get eaten by predators that thrive because of plant B, and so on. The system stays in balance. Not always — we have outbreaks sometimes. We've had years when the deer were a real problem. We had a Japanese beetle infestation a few years back. But the system mostly self-regulates. And we tolerate losses that a commercial operation couldn't. If we lose 20% of our peach crop to brown rot in a wet year, we shrug and move on. A commercial farm can't shrug. We can.

Interviewer 1: That seems like a luxury.

Horticulturist: It is a luxury. It's a luxury enabled by our funding model. We don't need every tree to produce maximum yield. We're not trying to hit revenue targets. So we can let ecology be messy and accept that. Which is one reason I'm careful not to oversell what we do as a model for commercial agriculture. Commercial farms don't have the freedom we have. Our model only works because we have alternative funding sources. So when farmers visit and ask how we manage to avoid spraying — I always have to caveat that.

Interviewer 2: Let's go back to volunteers. What's the demographic of the people who come?

Horticulturist: It's surprisingly diverse. Age-wise, we have everyone from kids — we have some homeschool families where kids come weekly with their parents — to retirees in their 70s and 80s. The bulk is probably 30s through 60s. Background-wise — a lot of folks who work in offices and need physical work to balance their lives. A lot of retirees. Some teachers. Some healthcare workers. A few people who are between jobs or in transition. Politically — we have a mix. The food forest pulls from both progressive and conservative communities. There's a homesteading-curious crowd that leans more conservative. There's an environmental justice crowd that leans more progressive. They all show up on Saturdays and work side by side. And the work itself unites them. It's hard to argue politics when you're weeding the same bed.

Interviewer 1: That's a beautiful thing in this political moment.

Horticulturist: It really is. And it's something we're intentional about. We don't talk politics from the front. We don't post political content on our social media. We've had to navigate some tensions — there have been moments where a volunteer made a comment that made other volunteers uncomfortable, and we've had to have private conversations to set norms. But mostly people self-regulate. They come here for something other than politics, and they leave that other stuff at the gate. Which I think is meaningful. It's a piece of evidence that political division isn't inevitable. People can be in real community across political lines if there's something else holding them together. The land holds us together here.

Interviewer 2: Do you have any stories about volunteer relationships that formed unexpectedly?

Horticulturist: Many. We had two volunteers — one was a retired Army officer, pretty conservative, very straightforward person. The other was a graduate student in environmental studies, very progressive, theoretical, intellectual. They couldn't have been more different on paper. And they ended up partnering on a permaculture design project together for our pollinator section. Spent months working together. They've become close friends. They still text. They have lunch monthly. They've completely changed each other's thinking on things. Neither converted the other politically — they still vote differently — but they understand each other deeply now. And that wouldn't have happened anywhere else in their normal lives. The land made that friendship possible. So yes, there are stories like that.

Interviewer 1: That feels rare in 2026.

Horticulturist: It is rare. And I think that's part of why this kind of work matters beyond food. We're so structurally segregated now — politically, economically, culturally. People don't have many spaces where they encounter folks unlike themselves. The food forest is one of the few places I can think of where that's actually happening. Whether it's reproducible at scale, I don't know. But it's a model. It's an existence proof that it's still possible.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the events you host with food pantries. How do those relationships work?

Horticulturist: So we work with five food pantries across the region. Two are church-affiliated, three are independent nonprofits. Each one has different needs and different capacities. Some can take large quantities of produce on a regular schedule. Others want smaller deliveries but more frequently. We've worked out a system over the years where we know what each pantry can handle and we route harvest accordingly. The pantries handle distribution to their clients. They've also been good partners on education — sometimes their clients have never seen the fruits we grow. So a few pantries have started doing little orientations — here's what this is, here's how you eat it, here's a simple recipe. That's been beautiful to hear about.

Interviewer 1: Are you ever in direct contact with the people who eat your food?

Horticulturist: Occasionally. We hosted a community dinner two years ago that brought together volunteers and food pantry clients and pantry staff. About 60 people came. We ate food cooked from our harvest. People shared stories. That was — that was meaningful in a way I'm still processing. Most of our food production doesn't have that kind of feedback loop. You harvest, you deliver, you don't see the people who eat it. So having those moments where the whole chain is in the same room — they remind us why we do it. We've talked about doing them more often. Capacity is the constraint.

Interviewer 2: Are there things you wish you could do that you don't have the resources for?

Horticulturist: A lot. We'd love more staff. With one more full-time person, we could double our capacity. Even a part-time person dedicated to volunteer coordination would change things significantly. We'd love better infrastructure for processing — a proper kitchen for the preservation workshops, better cold storage for harvest, better tool storage. We'd love to do more outreach to communities that don't typically engage with this kind of work — particularly in immigrant communities and lower-income neighborhoods. The barriers to those communities visiting us aren't logistical. They're cultural and informational. And bridging that requires dedicated outreach work that we don't have time for. So a lot of capacity gaps.

Interviewer 1: What about technology? Do you use much of it?

Horticulturist: Some, more than people might expect. We have a planting database that tracks every tree on the property — species, variety, planting date, location, phenology, observations. We use that constantly. We have a basic weather station that gives us microclimate data. I use mapping software to plan plantings. We have a simple online volunteer signup system. Compared to a commercial operation we're low-tech. But we're not anti-technology. We use what's useful. The data we've collected over seven years is one of our most valuable assets. It lets us see patterns that wouldn't be visible otherwise. Which varieties are producing well, which are struggling. Which microclimates are warmer or wetter. Whether bloom dates are shifting. All of that.

Interviewer 2: Are bloom dates shifting?

Horticulturist: Yes. Measurably. Over the seven years we've been tracking, our average spring bloom dates have moved earlier by roughly five or six days. And we've had more extreme weather events — late frosts that hit plants after they've already bloomed, dry stretches, heavy rain events. The climate is changing in real time and we're seeing it in our records. Which has implications for what we plant going forward. We're starting to phase in some species that might have struggled here 20 years ago and might thrive now. We're also being more careful with species that need consistent cold for fruit set — apples, especially. We may eventually lose some apple varieties from our site because we're not getting reliable chill hours anymore.

Interviewer 1: That's sobering.

Horticulturist: It is. And it's not just climate. It's pollinator decline. We've documented fewer native pollinators visiting the site over time. Bumblebee populations are way down. We see fewer butterflies. The wild bees are stressed. And that affects production. The fruits we grow rely on pollinators. If they go, we go. So we're doing what we can — planting pollinator-supporting species, leaving wild zones, advocating for broader changes. But the trajectory at the regional and national level isn't great. So the food forest is also functioning as a small refuge. Whether that's enough to matter at scale, I don't know. But it's what we can do.

Interviewer 2: Let me ask one more question and then we'll let you go. What's something you hope people take away from this kind of work?

Horticulturist: A few things. One — that food can be free. That sounds simple but it's radical in our economy. The idea that something this essential to human life doesn't have to be commodified. The food pantry clients who get pawpaws from us — they're getting something that money can't easily buy. And the gift of it matters as much as the nutrition. Two — that beauty matters. We could grow food in efficient rows. But we don't. We grow it in patterns that feel like sanctuaries. Because beauty is part of nourishment. Three — that complexity is okay. Modern life pretends everything should be simple, optimized, fast. The land isn't like that. The land is endlessly complex and slow. Spending time with that teaches you something. Four — that community is still possible. The people who come here have built real friendships across real differences. That's evidence that the social fragmentation we live with isn't inevitable. And five — that simple practices are healing. Eating real food, working with your hands, being outside, being with others — these aren't fancy interventions. They're old practices. And they still work. So that's what I hope people take. The food forest is one small place. But the principles it embodies are universal and accessible.

Interviewer 1: That's a beautiful place to end. Thank you so much.

Horticulturist: My pleasure. Let me grab those persimmons before you go. And — keep in touch. I want to hear how the project goes.

Interviewer 2: We will.

Horticulturist: Alright. Walk this way, the ripest ones are over by the path.
