Developing the institutional knowledge about this specific site — how it drains, how it dries out, where the cold air pools, which blocks need extra attention in which weather — that takes years of standing there and watching. Or I might have just talked myself out of it entirely, which would have been a mistake. So maybe not knowing was the right thing.

Interviewer 2: How are you thinking about sustainability in the financial sense going forward — what's the horizon look like?

Farmer: The honest answer is we've been on a growth curve and the growth has been real and I believe it continues. Our wine club membership has grown every year. Our distribution footprint has grown every year. The tasting room revenue has grown every year. And we're getting better at what we do, so the quality is going up as our site knowledge deepens. I don't see a reason that trajectory doesn't continue, assuming we don't get hit with a catastrophic disease year or some macroeconomic shock that collapses the premium beverage market, which — I can't insure against that. But barring something like that, I think we're in a genuinely sustainable position for the first time. Whether that sustainable position is the right foundation for the life changes we may be considering — that's a different question.

Interviewer 1: The cidery idea — if that comes together, what does it add to the picture?

Farmer: Diversification, mainly. Wine is seasonal and weather-dependent in ways that keep me up at night. Cider, using a range of apple varieties that ripen at different times, potentially gives us a more distributed harvest and processing calendar. It also opens up a price tier — we can offer cider in the tasting room and at markets at a lower price point than our wines, which expands our customer base. And thematically, it fits. Apples and wine grapes both have this deep connection to European agricultural tradition, to place-specific character, to the idea of terroir. I can tell a coherent story that puts our wines and our ciders in the same conversation. And I think the heirloom apple angle — sourcing from these old orchards in the mountains around us — that story is genuinely exciting. These varieties have been growing in these mountains for generations. Some of them you can't find anywhere else. Getting to be the people who turn that into something people can drink is — yeah, I'm into it.

Interviewer 2: What does the landscape look like to you five or ten years from now — your vineyard, and also the region?

Farmer: For us — I hope we're producing somewhere between 2,000 and 3,000 cases, the cidery is up and running, we're hosting more educational programming, maybe a formal apprenticeship or internship program for people who want to learn mountain viticulture. And I hope we've figured out the life stuff. I hope Caleb is happy. That matters most.

For the region — I think Southwest Virginia wine is going to be a real thing in a way it isn't quite yet. The elevation, the character you can get from these sites, the fact that land is still accessible to people who aren't already wealthy — that combination means there's room for more operations to come in and do interesting work. I'd love to see a small cluster of committed producers within, say, a 30-mile radius of us. Something people can visit as a destination. The wine trail model has worked in other parts of Virginia and there's no reason it can't work here if the product is good. And I think the product will be good. This place wants to grow wine. The land is telling us something and we're just trying to listen to it correctly.

Interviewer 1: That's a beautiful way to put it. Is there anything we haven't asked about that you think is important?

Farmer: [Thinks] The wildness of it. The way living with this land changes you over time. I don't know how to explain this to someone who hasn't done it, but you develop an almost bodily relationship with a piece of ground that you tend every day. I know where the vines are weakest, I know which rows are always a little ahead of the others in spring. I can walk through the vineyard in the dark and know exactly where I am. And that relationship — the fact that this place knows me and I know it — that's not something I can recreate somewhere else in a year or two. That accumulates slowly and you don't know how deep it goes until you think about leaving it. That's part of what makes the conversations Caleb and I are having hard. It's not just a business to me. It's a place that I'm in a relationship with. And that sounds a little strange, maybe. But I think you probably understand it.
