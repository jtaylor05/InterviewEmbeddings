Interviewer 2: How do you decide what to breed?

I'm reading constantly — old breeding books from the 1920s, 1930s. Heritage Breeds Conservancy materials. Talking to other breeders. You're selecting for a lot of things at once. Temperament — really important, because aggressive sows or boars are dangerous and they pass it on. Mothering ability — does she take care of her piglets, does she crush them, does she let them nurse properly. Growth rate on pasture, not in confinement. That matters because a lot of breed data is from confinement operations and it doesn't translate. Feed conversion on what they're actually eating out here — mast, forage, supplemented grain. You make a breeding decision and you don't really know if it was right for two years.

Interviewer 1: That's a long planning horizon.

Everything's about speed and turnover. I don't operate that way. Can't. The animals don't operate that way.

Farmer: Hmm. I'd say — mentally? Most of it. Like, I'm thinking about it constantly. Interviewer 2: So we're coming up on — is this the woods?

Each paddock is maybe two and a half acres. We rotate the pigs through them. They stay in one paddock for two to three weeks depending on the season and the impact, and then we move them to the next one. And the paddock they came out of gets to rest. Recover. Sometimes for a full year before they come back to it.

Interviewer 1: Why so long?

Farmer: Because pigs are intense. They root. They dig. They disturb the soil. Which is great in moderation because they break up the duff, they bury seeds, they cycle nutrients. But if you leave them in one place too long they create a wasteland. They'll just demolish it. So the management is all about — give them enough time in a spot to do beneficial work, then move them before they do damage. And then give the land enough rest to actually recover. The mistake a lot of small pig operations make is too small a footprint, too little rotation, and they end up with pigs in a mudlot. Which is just industrial farming with prettier marketing.

Interviewer 2: That's a really harsh critique.

Farmer: It's true though. I — I'll talk about that more. But I see a lot of small farms that call themselves regenerative or pasture-raised and the animals are essentially in a sacrifice area. Interviewer 1: [Laughs] Noted.

They'll finish out around 250 to 280, which is small by commercial standards. Commercial pigs go to 280-300 in about six months. Ours take 10 to 12 months to get there. So we're growing them about twice as slow. And that matters for the meat, big time. The slower growth, the more intramuscular fat, the more flavor development. You can't rush it.

Interviewer 2: They look so different from what I think of when I think of pigs.

Farmer: Yeah, these aren't the pigs you see in pictures. These are darker, they're hairier, they've got — look at that snout, see how long it is? That's a foraging snout. That pig is built to dig in the dirt and find food. A modern commercial pig doesn't look like this. They've been bred for confinement. Short legs, short snouts, white skin because they're indoors and don't need pigment for sun protection. They've been bred away from being a real animal in a lot of ways. These — these are still pigs. The way pigs are supposed to be.

Interviewer 1: How do they handle the heat? It gets hot down here.

Farmer: They handle it pretty well, honestly. Better than commercial breeds would. The Ossabaws especially — that breed comes from a barrier island off Georgia, so they evolved in heat and humidity. The Red Wattles are okay too. We make sure they've got wallows — those muddy areas where they can cool off — and lots of shade, which the woods provides naturally. The biggest heat issue is actually the farrowing sows in summer. A sow in late pregnancy in 95-degree weather is uncomfortable. So we time our farrowings to avoid the peak of summer when we can.

Interviewer 2: How many litters do you get a year?

Farmer: We aim for about 20 litters a year total across our sows. Each sow has maybe 1.5 to 2 litters a year, not the 2.4 you get in industrial. We let our sows rest. We don't push them. And the litter sizes are smaller too — maybe 6 to 8 piglets per litter versus 12 to 14 in industrial. Which sounds bad on paper but the survival rate is way higher because the sows can actually mother them. In confinement they crush piglets because they don't have room to move, they don't bond with them, they're not behaving like real animals. So we lose fewer than the industry does even though we start with fewer.

Farmer: Shelters. We've got these little A-frame structures back at the barn area — I'll show you on the way back. Piglets do really well in the woods once they're old enough. They learn from mom. They start rooting around at like three weeks old. It's amazing to watch.

Interviewer 2: Can we get closer?

Interviewer 1: How do you decide which ones become breeders versus which ones get sold for meat?

Farmer: A lot of observation. Temperament first. We watch them from birth. How do they react to us, how do they interact with their littermates, how curious are they without being aggressive. The temperament is — that's the foundation. Then conformation — body structure, leg quality, length, depth of body. And then we watch how they develop on pasture. Are they good foragers, do they grow at the rate we want, do they stay sound. If a pig has structural issues, leg issues, anything that's going to be a problem for breeding or for passing on to offspring, they go in the feeder group. We're really strict about that. Some breeders aren't. They breed weak animals because they need the numbers. We won't do that. We'd rather have fewer good breeders than more mediocre ones.

Interviewer 2: How long do you keep a sow in the breeding program?

Farmer: As long as she's productive and healthy. We've got one sow, Esther, who's almost eight years old. She's had nine litters, she's still producing, she's still mothering. We don't have that pressure because we're not optimizing for maximum throughput. We're optimizing for quality and for the genetics.

Interviewer 1: Tell us about the woods themselves. Like, what's growing here?

Farmer: So this is mostly oak and hickory, which is exactly what you want for pigs. Mast crop — acorns, hickory nuts — that's a huge part of their diet in fall and early winter. The pigs go from grazing and rooting in summer to eating acorns and nuts in fall and the difference in the meat is dramatic. The fat changes. The flavor changes. People who buy from us in winter specifically request "mast finished" because they know the difference. We've also got some pine and some understory — saplings, vines, ferns. The pigs eat a little of all of it. They're omnivores, so they're getting protein from grubs and small things they dig up too. We supplement with non-GMO grain — we have to, especially in winter when forage is limited. But the grain is maybe 40% of their total diet annually, less in summer, more in late winter. The forest is doing a lot of the feeding.

Interviewer 2: That sounds like a really different animal nutritionally.

Farmer: It is. There's actually research on this — pasture-raised and forest-raised pork has different fatty acid profiles, higher in omega-3s, different micronutrients. The fat is yellower, which is a sign of better nutrition. Industrial pork is white because the pigs have no access to forage. Our pork is — I mean, you've seen the color of our fat? It's almost golden in some cuts. That's the diet. That's the life they led. And that ends up on a plate and people taste the difference.

Interviewer 1: Let's keep walking — what's over here?

Farmer: This is one of the older paddocks — we haven't had pigs in here for about ten months. Look at the regrowth. See how much understory has come back? We're trying to find that balance between disturbance and recovery that mimics what would have happened naturally — a herd of feral pigs or any disturbance species would have moved through, done its thing, and moved on. The land evolved with that pattern of disturbance and rest. What it didn't evolve with is constant pressure in one place, which is what most pig operations do.

Interviewer 2: Are there native pigs around here that you'd be mimicking?

Farmer: Not native exactly — there are feral hogs in South Carolina but they're descendants of European pigs that got loose. The point is, the land is built to handle disturbance — what it's not built to handle is constant disturbance. So you give it pulses. Pulse and rest. That's the rhythm.

Interviewer 1: Let's circle back to something you said earlier — about small farms calling themselves regenerative but not actually doing it. What did you mean by that?

Farmer: Oh, here we go. [Laughs] Okay. So — I'm going to be honest with you. I'm probably going to say some things that aren't going to make me friends. But this is what I see.

Interviewer 1: Go for it.

Farmer: There's a lot of small farms right now that are using the language of regenerative agriculture as marketing without actually doing the work of regenerative agriculture. And I see it especially with pigs because pigs are hard. Pigs require management. They require infrastructure. They require thinking ahead. And a lot of operations — they get some pigs, they put up some fence, they call it pasture-raised, and they just leave them in the same spot until the spot is destroyed. The pig's life isn't that much better, the land is being damaged, and the marketing is fooling the customer.

Interviewer 2: That's a strong critique.

Farmer: It is. And I want to be clear — I'm not saying this about every small operation. There are people out there doing it really well. But the bar to call yourself "pasture-raised" or "regenerative" right now is way too low. And it's hurting the people who are actually doing the work because customers can't tell the difference. They see "pasture-raised pork" at two different prices and they don't know that one is from a real rotational system and the other is from a mudlot. And the cheaper one is undercutting the real producer.

Interviewer 1: What do you think can be done about that?

Farmer: Education. Transparency. Get customers on the farm. That's what we do. We host farm dinners twice a year. Anyone who wants to can come out, see the land, walk the woods, see the pigs in their actual environment. And if you've done that, you know what real pasture-raised looks like. You can spot the fake versions immediately. But it requires the customer to put in some effort. And it requires the farmer to be open to scrutiny.

Interviewer 2: How do you handle restaurants? You sell to a lot of restaurants, right?

Farmer: BBQ specifically, yeah. South Carolina BBQ tradition is whole hog, mustard sauce — that's the regional style. And a heritage hog is what BBQ used to mean before commercial pork took over. You go back 80, 100 years, the hog that was being cooked for BBQ in South Carolina was a hog that was raised on a small farm, foraging, with that intramuscular fat — that's where the flavor of traditional BBQ comes from. When we sell to a real pitmaster, we're connecting them back to that tradition. And they recognize it immediately. They taste it and they go — okay, this is what my grandfather was cooking. This is the real thing.

Interviewer 2: That must feel meaningful — the cultural side of it.

Farmer: It is. Yeah, it really is. Farmer: It's a mix. Some are Black-owned BBQ places — and that side of South Carolina BBQ history is really specific and important to us. Interviewer 2: How do you handle the relationship with restaurants — like, do they buy whole animals, half animals?

Farmer: Whole. Almost always whole. Which is part of what makes our model work with BBQ — they want whole hog. So we deliver a whole hog, processed, and they use everything. We don't have to deal with the headache of selling specific cuts at retail to balance the carcass, which is the killer for a lot of small pork operations. If you can only sell pork chops and pork belly and the rest sits in your freezer, you're losing money. Selling whole hog to restaurants means we move the whole animal at full value every time.

Interviewer 1: How does processing work?

Farmer: We have a relationship with a small USDA processor about an hour from here. They've been doing it for decades. They know our animals, they know how we want things done. The processor relationship is — it's a critical thing. There's not a lot of small USDA processors left and if they go away, it's very hard to do what we do. I know other farmers who've had to drive three, four hours to processing because their local processor closed. That's not sustainable.

Interviewer 2: Is that processor specifically equipped for whole hog?

Farmer: Yeah, that's actually their specialty. A lot of processors aren't really set up for whole hog because most of the market is for cuts. But this place has been doing whole hog for the BBQ tradition for a long time. Which is part of why we ended up there.

Interviewer 1: How do you think about pricing?

Farmer: Honestly we're priced as low as we can be while still being viable. Which is still more than commercial pork. A whole hog from us, depending on size, is going to run a restaurant anywhere from $900 to $1,500. Which sounds like a lot until you realize what's in there. That's a year of an animal's life, our labor, infrastructure, breeding work, feed, processing, delivery. And it's also a product that the restaurant can charge a premium for because of what it is. So the math works for them and it works for us. Barely. We're not getting rich.

Interviewer 2: Are you breaking even? Profitable?

Farmer: We're profitable. We're modestly profitable. Marcus and I both pay ourselves a salary that — it's not what we could make doing other things. I know that for a fact. But it's enough. So I think about it as wealth-building over income generation. Long-term thinking. Which is — that's how farms used to work, right? Generational. You weren't trying to maximize your year-over-year revenue. You were building something that lasted.

Interviewer 2: That's a beautiful way to think about it.

Farmer: It's just how my grandparents thought about it. I'm not original. [Laughs] I'm just trying to remember what's been forgotten.

Interviewer 1: Let's circle back to factory pig farming for a second — what do you want people to understand about that, that they might not understand?

Farmer: Man. Okay. So I haven't worked in industrial pork, just to be clear. Everything I know is from reading and from talking to people who have. But I've read a lot. I've read the journalism, I've read the research, I've watched the documentaries. And the picture is pretty grim. So in industrial pig farming, sows are in gestation crates for most of their adult life. A gestation crate is a metal stall that's so narrow the sow can't turn around. Can't turn around. For her whole pregnancy. And then she goes into a farrowing crate for the birth and the nursing period, which is also a crate she can't really move in. She gets out for a week between weaning her piglets and being bred again, and then she's back in the gestation crate. That's her life. Until she's culled at 3 or 4 years old for declining productivity.

Interviewer 2: That's hard to hear.

Farmer: It's hard to say. And the piglets — they're weaned at 17 to 21 days, which is way too early. Their mothers in nature would nurse them for months. They're put into nursery rooms that are crowded and confined. They never see sunlight or grass. They're fed grain only, often with subtherapeutic antibiotics to keep them growing fast in the conditions they're in. And the meat is the meat that's in your grocery store. That's where most American pork comes from. And the customer doesn't know. Because the marketing doesn't tell you. The package says "all-natural" or some other meaningless phrase, and that's what people see. They don't see the gestation crate. They don't see the antibiotics. They don't see the early weaning. They just see a pork chop.

Interviewer 1: Why do you think people don't know more about this?

Farmer: A bunch of reasons. The industry has worked really hard to keep it out of public view — there are actually ag-gag laws in some states that make it illegal to film inside these facilities. The economic incentives push toward consolidation and cheap food, and the consumer gets cheap food in exchange for not asking questions. And honestly, who wants to know? It's easier not to know. I don't fault customers for that necessarily. But once you do know, you have a responsibility. To at least ask the question. To at least look for something different.

Interviewer 2: And our role is partly to spread that awareness. Like, why we're doing this research.

Farmer: I appreciate that. I really do. Because the more people know, the more pressure there is to change. And honestly — change has been happening. It's slow. But more states are banning gestation crates. More retailers are sourcing from operations that don't use them. Consumer awareness is growing. It's not fast enough. But it's moving.

Interviewer 1: You mentioned earlier that you wanted to come back to something about farmers — let me see if I can find what it was you said. Something about farmers not wanting to change?

Farmer: Yeah. Okay. I'm going to — alright. I'm going to say some things. And I want to be clear that I love farmers and I have a lot of respect for the work. But I'm going to be honest with you.

Interviewer 2: Please.

Farmer: A lot of farmers don't want to change. They don't. Sometimes a farmer is genuinely stretched too thin and trying to survive and there's no bandwidth for anything else. I understand that. But a lot of times — a lot of times — what's being called "too busy" is actually "I don't want to." It's a choice. And it's dressed up as a constraint.

Interviewer 1: That's a strong statement.

Farmer: It is. And I've been thinking about it a lot. Because I see — okay. I see programs. I see grants. I see free workshops. There is so much support available now, more than there has ever been, for farmers who want to do things differently. Interviewer 2: Do you think there's something specific in farming culture that resists change?

Farmer: I think there's pride. And I respect the pride. But the pride sometimes blocks the learning. Like, a 60-year-old farmer who's been doing it one way his whole life — going to a workshop run by a 30-year-old who's going to tell him about cover crops or rotational grazing or whatever — that's hard. That's a hit to the ego. So instead of going, he stays home. Says he's too busy. And then complains five years later that the land isn't producing what it used to and the prices are bad and the kids don't want to take over the farm. And it's like — okay. There were paths you could have taken. You didn't take them. That's a choice.

Interviewer 1: Has there been a moment where you saw that clearly with someone you knew?

Farmer: Yeah. There's an old farmer down the road from us. Conventional row crop and cattle. Good guy. I like him a lot. And his land has been declining for years — anyone can see it. The soil is depleted, the pastures are overgrazed, his yields are dropping. And I've talked to him. Marcus has talked to him. We've offered, multiple times, just to walk the land together. To share what we know. Or to introduce him to people we know who could help. And he always — there's always a reason. He's too busy this week. The weather's not right. He's got family coming. He'll do it next month. Next month becomes next year. He's still saying it. And meanwhile his operation gets harder every year. And he calls it bad luck or bad markets or the weather. And — I don't know what to do with that. I really don't.

Interviewer 2: That sounds frustrating.

Farmer: It's heartbreaking, honestly. Because I think — I think he could turn things around. The land could come back. He's got knowledge and skill. He just won't make the leap. And it's not about money. It's not about time. It's about something else. Pride, maybe. Fear. The discomfort of admitting that what you've been doing might not be working. And I have a lot of compassion for that. But I also want to call it what it is. It's a choice. And the consequences of the choice are real.

Interviewer 1: Do you think your relationship to that — being someone who left, who came back, who didn't inherit a way of doing things — does that help you see it differently?

Farmer: I think it does. I didn't have a system to defend. I came into this with no idea what I was doing, which meant I had to learn from scratch. And I think that openness is actually a competitive advantage. New farmers who didn't grow up in farming culture can sometimes be better at adopting new methods because they don't have the baggage. They don't have a way of doing things that they're emotionally attached to.

Interviewer 2: Do you think there are ways to get past that resistance with established farmers? Like, what's worked when something has worked?

Farmer: When it's worked, it's been one-on-one. Quiet. Not in a workshop. Not in a group setting. It's been a farmer they trust coming to them and saying, hey, I'm trying this thing, come see it. Personal. Not institutional. The institutional outreach doesn't work most of the time. The peer-to-peer stuff works. Slowly. The other thing that works is profit. If you can show a farmer that another farmer like him is making more money doing something different, that gets attention. Money talks. It really does. So if we can keep building examples — small, profitable, sustainable operations that look like something a traditional farmer can imagine doing — that's the most effective advocacy. Don't argue with him. Just show him that it works.

Interviewer 1: We've heard some of that from other farmers too — that the only thing that moves the needle is profitability.

Farmer: Yeah. And I think that's just realism. Farmers are businesspeople. They have to be. They've got mortgages and equipment loans and families to feed. If you can't show them that change is going to make their financial situation better, you can't expect them to take the risk. So the burden is on the people doing things differently to prove that it works financially. We have to be — Marcus and I — we have to be a working example. Our books have to add up. We have to be paying our bills doing it the right way. Because if we can't, then everyone gets to say, see, that's why we don't do that. So there's a lot of pressure on us to make this actually work. Not just philosophically. Economically.

Interviewer 2: Let's talk a little bit about the homestead side of things — the cows, the garden, the chickens. How does that fit?

Farmer: That's our food. Mostly. The garden feeds our family and Marcus's family. The chickens give us eggs. The cows give us milk — we have two Jersey cows, they're sweet animals. We make butter, we make some cheese, we drink the milk. So we're producing a lot of what we eat right here. And the pigs eat some of the surplus — milk surplus, vegetable scraps, all of that goes to them. The whole system feeds itself. Which I love. I really love that. The closed loop.

Interviewer 1: Did you grow up with that?

Farmer: A little bit. My grandparents had it. My parents — less so. By the time I was a kid we were more on the conventional grocery store track. So bringing it back is partly a reconnection to my grandparents' way of life. They had a kitchen garden. They had a cow. They had hogs. And then a generation slipped and we lost a lot of that knowledge. I've been picking it back up the slow way. Reading old books, asking older people in the community, just trying things.

Interviewer 2: Do you teach your kids any of it?

Farmer: As much as they'll let me. [Laughs] My son's 11 and my daughter's 8. They help with the chickens. They've helped with the garden. My son's helped me with some of the pig stuff but he's still a little intimidated by the bigger animals, which I think is healthy — they should be intimidated by a 500-pound sow. They'll get more confident as they get older. But the goal isn't to make them farmers. The goal is to make them know where food comes from. Even if they go off and become accountants or whatever, they'll know. And that knowledge will affect every choice they make about food for the rest of their lives.

Interviewer 1: Have you noticed any change in how people perceive farming as a profession? Like, is it cool now?

Farmer: It's — yeah, weirdly. [Laughs] I think small-scale farming has gotten more cultural attention than it had 20 years ago. The whole farm-to-table movement, the rise of food media, social media accounts of farmers, all of that has put farming back into the cultural conversation in a way it hadn't been. Whether that translates to more people actually doing it — I'm less sure. There's still huge structural barriers. Land access. Capital. Knowledge. Networks. So you can romanticize farming all you want but if you can't afford to buy land, you can't farm. So the cultural shift has been positive but it hasn't necessarily translated to more new farmers at scale.

Interviewer 2: What would it take to change that?

Farmer: Land access programs. There are some — there are some land link programs, farmer training programs, things like that. They're underfunded. They need to be bigger and more numerous. And we need cultural support for farming as a serious career, not as a hobby or a lifestyle. Farming is a real profession. It requires skill, knowledge, capital, business acumen. It's not just throwing some seeds in the ground and hoping. So treating it with that level of seriousness — in our education system, in our economic system, in our cultural narrative — that would help.

Interviewer 1: How do you feel about the way Black farmers are represented in this conversation? Like, do you see yourself in the broader "regenerative agriculture" community?

Farmer: That's a good question. And — honestly? Less than I'd like to. The regenerative agriculture movement, at least the visible part of it, the conferences and the books and the media — it's pretty white. Which makes sense given who has access to land in this country and the history of how Black farmers got pushed off their land in the 20th century. There's been a massive loss of Black-owned farmland over the past hundred years. We're a tiny percentage of farmers now. And the people who get to be the public face of regenerative agriculture are mostly people who could afford to either inherit or buy significant amounts of land. Which has its own racial pattern. So I see myself in the work. I don't always see myself in the movement.

Interviewer 2: Has that affected how you approach what you do?

Farmer: A little. I think Marcus and I are very aware that we represent something — not by choice, just by being. Black brothers running a regenerative pig operation in South Carolina is not nothing. It's a statement even when we're not trying to make one. And we've had Black farmers and aspiring farmers reach out to us, just to talk, to ask questions, to feel like they're not the only one. We've been to a few events specifically for Black farmers and those have been meaningful. There's a network growing. It's smaller than the mainstream regenerative network but it's there. And it has its own character — there's a lot of history in those rooms. Loss. Resilience. People recovering family land. Stories that don't get told as much in the broader movement.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything specific about your operation that connects to that history?

Farmer: The pigs themselves, in a way. Heritage hog breeds — many of them have history that's tied to specific communities and regions. The Ossabaw came to America through Spanish colonization. There's a deep history there of pigs being part of the Southern food economy across racial lines, with Black foodways being absolutely central to what we now think of as Southern cuisine. BBQ tradition, hog butchering traditions, charcuterie traditions — a lot of that came from Black labor and Black knowledge that didn't get attributed properly. So when we raise these animals and sell them to BBQ pitmasters — including Black pitmasters who are preserving that tradition — there's a connection there that's bigger than just business. It's cultural preservation. It's reclaiming.

Interviewer 2: That's a powerful way to frame it.

Farmer: It's how it feels to me. I don't go around talking about it like that all the time because — I don't know, I'm a private person. But you asked. And that's the honest answer.

Interviewer 1: Let's talk about the future a little bit. What do you see for the farm in ten years?

Farmer: I want the breeding program to be — I want it to be something that other people are seeking out. Like, I want our genetics to be recognized as some of the best heritage pork genetics in the Southeast. That's a long-term goal. We're maybe halfway there. Another decade of careful selection, and I think we'll be in that conversation. I want to maintain the same scale, roughly. I don't want to grow this into something that requires a bigger team and more management. Marcus and I can manage what we have. If we grew it, we'd lose the intimacy of the operation, which is part of what makes it work. So we're not chasing growth. We're chasing quality.

Interviewer 2: What about additions to the operation?

Farmer: I'd like to do more educational stuff. Host more farm dinners. Maybe a workshop series for new pig farmers who want to learn how to do this right. Marcus has been talking about a podcast for a while — telling the story, interviewing other farmers we respect, putting the conversation out into the world more. I'm — I'm skeptical of the podcast because that's more public-facing stuff that I don't love. But he's good at it. So if he wants to do it, I'll support him.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything you want to add — anything we didn't ask about that you'd want people to know?

Farmer: [Pause] I want people to know that this is hard. It is hard, hard work. And it requires patience that most modern life doesn't train us for. The breeding cycle is years. The land healing is years. The customer relationships are years. If you want to do this, you have to accept that you're committing to a long timeline, with no guarantees, in a culture that rewards speed and shortcuts. You're going against the grain of basically everything. And it has to mean something to you to make that worth it. The pay isn't going to make it worth it. The status isn't going to make it worth it. It has to mean something. For us — Marcus and me — it means continuity. It means honoring our grandparents. It means doing right by these animals. It means feeding people food that's worthy of them. That meaning is what gets us up before sunrise every day. If you don't have that, you can't sustain it.

Interviewer 2: That's really beautifully put.

Farmer: I don't usually talk like this. [Laughs] You got me going.

Interviewer 1: We really appreciate it. Honestly.

Farmer: No, I — thank you. This was — it was actually good. Talking it through. Sometimes when you're just doing the work every day you forget why. And going back over it like this is — yeah. It reminds me.

Interviewer 2: One last thing — what would you say to someone our age who was thinking about getting into this?

Farmer: Don't romanticize it. Come work on a farm. Spend a season. Get your hands dirty. See if you actually want this life or if you just want the idea of it. And if after a season you still want it — then start as small as you can, somewhere you can afford to be, with people who'll teach you. Don't try to buy land in year one. Don't try to start a brand in year one. Just learn the work first. Everything else comes after. The land will come, the customers will come, the brand will come. But the work has to be solid first. That's the foundation.

Interviewer 1: Thank you so much.

Farmer: Yeah. Yeah, thank y'all. Come back anytime. And — when y'all are writing this up, if you need to check anything with me, just call. I'm easier on the phone than I am in front of a microphone. [Laughs]

Interviewer 2: We'll do that.

Farmer: Alright. Y'all drive safe getting back.
