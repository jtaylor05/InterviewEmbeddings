Interviewer 1: Thanks so much for having us out — this place is gorgeous.

Farmer: Oh, thank you! Yeah, we — we worked really hard on it. My husband and I built the house ourselves. Well, mostly ourselves. We had help on some of the bigger stuff. But yeah, this whole property — five years ago this was just trees and brush. So everything you see, we did. The landscaping, the beds, all of it. Come on in, or do you want to start outside?

Interviewer 2: Maybe outside? It's such a beautiful day.

Farmer: Oh yeah, let's. Let me just grab my coffee. You want any? I just made a pot.

Interviewer 1: I'm okay, thank you.

Interviewer 2: I'd actually love some, if that's okay.

Farmer: Yeah, totally, give me one second. [Pause] Okay. So — let's walk back this way. The flower beds are the main attraction, that's where I spend most of my time. And the bees are back there too. We've got eight hives now, which is more than I planned for but it just kind of happened. Bees do that.

Interviewer 1: How does that happen?

Farmer: [Laughs] So you start with two. That's what everybody says — start with two. And then one of them swarms and you catch the swarm, and now you have three. And then a friend has a hive she can't manage anymore and gives it to you. And then you split a hive in spring because it's getting too big. And suddenly you have eight hives and you're like, when did this happen? But I love them. They're — they're actually one of my favorite parts of the farm. They're so calming. I'll just go sit by them sometimes. It sounds weird but you can sit a few feet away from a hive on a warm afternoon and just listen to them and it's the most peaceful thing in the world.

Interviewer 2: We're in — this is Dorchester County, right?

Farmer: Yes — Dorchester. About 35 minutes from downtown Charleston depending on traffic, which honestly some days is a lot. Charleston has gotten so much busier in the last few years. We're far enough out that we have land and quiet, but close enough that I can be at a Charleston market in under an hour. Which is the whole reason we picked this spot. The Charleston market is huge for what I do. Flowers are not a thing you can ship cheaply. They have to move locally. So being near a major city was non-negotiable.

Interviewer 1: How big is the property?

Farmer: Six acres. Which sounds tiny to a lot of farmers — and it is. But for cut flowers, you can do a lot with six acres. Honestly we use maybe two and a half acres actively. The rest is woods and the house lot and some pasture for the sheep. The flower production happens on what would be a really big backyard. People always underestimate that. They think you need 50 acres to farm and you really don't, depending on what you're growing.

Interviewer 2: Let's start with — how did you end up doing this? Like, what's the path?

Farmer: Oh god. Okay. So I am — I am first generation farmer. Like, fully. My family has no farming background. My dad worked for the city of Charleston for 30 years, my mom was a teacher. We grew up in North Charleston. I went to college at College of Charleston, studied marketing, worked in marketing for almost ten years after graduating. I was working for a hospitality group doing their digital marketing. Living downtown. Married, no kids yet, doing the thing.

Interviewer 1: And then?

Farmer: And then COVID. [Laughs] That's the whole story for so many farmers my age. COVID happened, the world stopped, my husband and I were in a small condo downtown going kind of insane. We started looking at land just to have somewhere to escape to. And the place we ended up buying was this property. It was raw land, no house, no power, nothing. We bought it in October 2020 with the idea that we'd build something. And I was already obsessed with gardening at that point — I'd been growing flowers in containers on our condo balcony, and then in raised beds at my parents' house when we'd visit. I had this whole flower thing happening that was just hobby-level. And once we had the land, I started turning it into something serious. By 2022 I'd quit my marketing job and was doing this full-time. So coming up on four years now.

Interviewer 2: That's a fast transition.

Farmer: It was fast. And I want to be honest — it was scary. I went from a salary and benefits and stability to — to this. Which is not stable. And we made it work because my husband still has a steady job. He works remote, he's in IT, and his income is what keeps the lights on while I build this. I want to be really clear about that because I think a lot of people see flower farmers on Instagram and think, oh, she just started a business and it's thriving. And the truth is, most of us are operating with a partner's income covering the household expenses. Or savings. Or both. The "I quit my job to farm flowers full-time" story almost always has a support structure behind it that doesn't get talked about.

Interviewer 1: That's a really important thing to say.

Farmer: It is, and it's something I try to talk about more on my own social media because I think the unrealistic picture is doing real damage. I see women — and it is mostly women, in this part of farming — who quit jobs, who go into debt to start flower farms, who think they're going to be profitable in year one. And then year two hits and they're broke and traumatized and they think it's because they did something wrong. And it's not because they did something wrong. It's because the business is hard and the timeline is long and Instagram doesn't show you that.

Interviewer 2: Yeah, that comes up a lot in conversations about small farming generally.

Farmer: Right, but I think with flower farming specifically there's a particular aesthetic to it. The pictures are so beautiful. The buckets of cosmos in the morning light. The dahlia field in October. Women in linen overalls cutting zinnias. It's gorgeous and it's seductive and it makes the work look easy. And then you actually try to do it and you realize you're working 14 hours a day in 95 degree heat with mosquitoes eating you alive, and the deer ate your tulip bulbs, and the chickens you bought to eat the bugs got picked off by a hawk, and you're losing money in your first three years and nobody on Instagram shows that part.

Interviewer 1: Are you on Instagram yourself?

Farmer: I am. And I try to show the real version. I post the bad weeks. I post the failures. I post when I'm crying in the field because something I worked on for three months bolted in a heat wave. Because I think — I think we owe each other honesty about what this actually is. And I have an okay following, maybe 18,000 right now. Which is small compared to the big flower farm accounts but it's the people I actually want to be talking to. Customers, other flower farmers, people who care.

Interviewer 2: Can we talk about what you're growing? Just so we have a sense of it?

Farmer: Yes! Okay, so let me give you the rough breakdown. I'm focused on cut flowers for bouquets — that's the main product. I do everything seasonal. So spring is anemones, ranunculus, tulips, sweet peas, snapdragons. Summer is zinnias, cosmos, sunflowers, lisianthus, celosia, dahlias starting in late summer. Fall is more dahlias, more celosia, marigolds, amaranth, ornamental grasses. Winter is when I'm doing nothing — I take November through January mostly off, which is one of the gifts of being in this climate, that I get a real off-season. I do some forced bulbs for Valentine's but otherwise winter is for planning and rest.

Interviewer 1: That's a lot of variety.

Farmer: It is. And the variety matters because bouquet sales depend on having texture and color variation. You can't sell a bouquet of just zinnias. Well, you can, but it's not as compelling. So I'm always trying to have at least 8 to 10 things in production at any given time so I can put together a really beautiful mixed bouquet.

Interviewer 2: Where do you sell mostly?

Farmer: Three main channels. Farmers market — I do the Charleston downtown market on Saturdays, that's my biggest day of the week. Florists — I have wholesale relationships with maybe four florists in Charleston who buy specific things from me weekly when they're in season. And then a small subscription — I do a flower CSA basically. Twelve weeks of bouquets, May through July, customers get a bouquet a week. I keep that small, maybe 30 subscribers, because anything bigger and I can't deliver them all.

Interviewer 1: How do you do the subscription delivery?

Farmer: I drive them. [Laughs] One day a week, every week of the season. I'm in my car for like six hours doing drop-offs around Charleston and the surrounding areas. Which sounds inefficient and it is. But the customers love it. I see them every week. I know their dogs' names. They're not just buyers, they're like — they're part of my farm in a way. And the personal connection is the whole thing. They're not paying me for flowers. They're paying me for a relationship with a place and a person.

Interviewer 2: Can we talk a little bit about the bigger flower farming industry? Like, where do most of the flowers in this country come from?

Farmer: Oh, this is one of my favorite things to rant about. So — about 80% of cut flowers sold in the US are imported. Mostly from Colombia and Ecuador. The flower industry in those countries is huge and it's been built around supplying the US market. And there are real problems with it. Labor conditions, pesticide exposure for workers, environmental impact, the carbon cost of flying flowers thousands of miles. When you buy a bouquet at a grocery store, you're almost certainly buying something that was grown on another continent, sprayed with chemicals that are banned here, picked by workers who weren't paid well, and flown to a wholesale market and then trucked to your store. And it's been refrigerated the whole way. So by the time you get it, it looks fresh but it's actually been cut for like ten days.

Interviewer 1: Versus what you do?

Farmer: Versus a flower I cut at 6 AM on Saturday morning and you buy at the market at 9 AM Saturday morning. The freshness alone — people who try local flowers for the first time can't believe how long they last in a vase. Two weeks versus four days. Because they haven't been in transit for ten days. And then there's the variety. The imported flowers are all bred for shipping — they're durable, they look the same, they have no scent. Most commercial roses have no smell. Did you know that?

Interviewer 2: I didn't, actually.

Farmer: It blows people's minds when they realize. They've grown up smelling no smell from cut roses and they think that's just how roses are. And then they smell a garden rose and they're floored. So part of what I'm doing is reconnecting people to what flowers actually are. They're sensory, they're alive, they're seasonal. And that's a totally different experience from the grocery store bouquet.

Interviewer 1: Is the market for local flowers growing?

Farmer: It is, slowly. There's an organization called the Association of Specialty Cut Flower Growers — ASCFG — that's been growing membership year over year. There's a "buy local flowers" movement that's small but real. And in cities like Charleston where there's a strong farm-to-table restaurant culture, that mentality is starting to extend to florists too. People are asking — where did these flowers come from? Are they local? But it's slow. Most people still buy whatever Trader Joe's has, and that's fine, I'm not judging. I just want people to know there's a choice.

Interviewer 2: Let's keep walking — what are we coming up on now?

Farmer: This is the main production area. So these are my main field rows. I do raised beds, 30 inches wide, with permanent paths between them. This is the no-till market garden style — I learned a lot of this from Floret Flowers and from Erin Benzakein's books. She's kind of the figure who put cut flower farming on the map for a lot of people my generation. And she's also been really honest about how hard it is, which I appreciate. So this is — let's see. This row is zinnias. The next row is celosia. The next two rows are cosmos. Over there is the dahlia patch, which is starting to come on — they'll really pop in another few weeks.

Interviewer 1: Dahlias are popular right now, right?

Farmer: Dahlias are huge right now. They're the it-flower of the past five years. Wedding florists want them, brides want them, Instagram wants them. And they're beautiful, they really are. But they're also fussy. The tubers don't store easily, they're susceptible to viruses, deer love them, and they don't bloom until late summer. So they're a lot of work for a fairly short season. I love them but I'm not over-invested in them. I do a few hundred plants. I know flower farmers doing thousands. I don't want to be that farm.

Interviewer 2: Why not?

Farmer: Because I don't want to be a monoculture. And because I don't want my whole business to depend on one trendy crop. Trends change. Five years ago everybody wanted ranunculus. Ten years ago it was peonies. Dahlias are right now. In five years it'll be something else. I'd rather build a business that has a wide base of beautiful seasonal flowers and isn't dependent on whatever's currently viral on Pinterest.

Interviewer 1: That's smart. Tell us about your soil management — like, what are you doing for the beds?

Farmer: So we built these beds from scratch when we started. The soil here was — okay. It's sandy. It's coastal sandy soil that drains too fast and doesn't hold nutrients well. So I've been adding organic matter constantly. Compost, leaf mold, mulch, the works. I have a relationship with a horse farm about ten miles from here and I get composted horse manure from them by the truckload. That stuff has transformed my beds. The first year I was here, my flowers were okay but not great. Now they're — they're really vigorous. The difference is the soil. And I'm not tilling, I'm just adding to the top. The biology builds up underneath and the plants respond.

Interviewer 2: Are you using any sprays or anything?

Farmer: I'm not. I'm chemical-free. I don't use synthetic fertilizers, I don't use pesticides, I don't use fungicides. Which has costs — I lose more to pests than someone who sprays would. But I have the bees, and I have my kid running around out here, and I just don't want any of that on my property. So I deal with the losses. And honestly, the more I build the soil, the more I notice my plants being naturally more resistant. Healthy plants don't get hit as hard by pests. The pest problems are usually a sign that something else is off.

Interviewer 1: Are you certified organic?

Farmer: No. And I don't plan to be. The certification process is expensive and it's geared toward food, which isn't really my market. My customers can come here and see what I do. The Charleston market — they trust the farmers there. The florists I work with come visit. I don't need a piece of paper. And honestly the organic certification has been corrupted enough at scale that it's not the trust signal it used to be. So I just describe my practices to people and they can decide.

Interviewer 2: This is something we've heard from a lot of farmers — that organic doesn't mean what it used to.

Farmer: Right, and I think that connects to a bigger conversation about — okay, so I'm going to bring up something a little controversial. I'm pretty into the Make America Healthy Again stuff. The MAHA conversation around food and health and pesticides. And I know that's politically charged for some people. But I think there's something real there. Like, the chemicals in our food system, the way we've moved away from real whole foods, the disconnect between what we eat and how it's produced — those are real concerns. And I think small farmers like me are doing the work that aligns with what MAHA is saying matters. We're growing things without poison. We're keeping animals on pasture. We're connecting people back to where food comes from.

Interviewer 1: Do you see MAHA and the regenerative agriculture movement as aligned?

Farmer: Mostly yes, but it's complicated. They both care about soil, food quality, less reliance on synthetic inputs, more local food systems. There's a lot of overlap. But the regenerative ag world is broader and more — it has a longer history. Regenerative practices were being developed and named for decades before MAHA came along, by people across the political spectrum. So there's tension. Some regenerative farmers don't want their work tied to a political movement. And some MAHA folks are skeptical of the academic-leaning regenerative agriculture establishment. So they should be allies but sometimes they're not really talking to each other.

Interviewer 2: Where do you sit in that?

Farmer: I sit in the overlap. I drink raw milk. I think pasteurization is overused. I think a lot of the chronic disease in this country is connected to industrial food. I'm skeptical of seed oils. And I also think regenerative practices are essential for ecological health, climate resilience, biodiversity. So I'm pulling from both worlds. And honestly, I think the future of small farming is going to depend on people who can pull from both worlds without being purist about either one. There's wisdom in a lot of places.

Interviewer 1: Raw milk is a big topic in MAHA circles, right?

Farmer: Huge. And we drink it. We milk our sheep — I'll show you the sheep in a minute — and we drink the milk raw. It's incredible. The flavor is so different from store milk. And there's nothing wrong with us — we've been drinking it for years and we've never gotten sick. The pasteurization rules are based on industrial dairy practices where you have animals in confinement under conditions that produce contaminated milk. From a small herd, on pasture, with clean equipment, raw milk is fine. It's how people drank milk for thousands of years. The blanket regulations against it are — I think they're overcautious and they're hurting small dairies that can't afford pasteurization equipment.

Interviewer 2: We have to ask — do you ever get pushback for any of these views? Like, in the farming community here?

Farmer: A little. I have friends in the regenerative ag space who think the raw milk stuff is fringe. And I have friends in the MAHA space who think I'm too liberal on some things. So I get a little of both. But honestly the Charleston community has been so welcoming to me. The market crowd is — it's a real community. The other farmers I see every Saturday, the customers I see every week, we're a chosen family. There's a lot of love in that market. Most weeks I leave feeling so full of connection and good conversation. People care about what I do and I care about what they do. So the bigger political stuff doesn't dominate. We're all just trying to do good work and feed people good food.

Interviewer 1: That's really nice to hear. Let's actually walk over to the sheep — and you mentioned bees, we should see those too.

Farmer: Yeah, let's go this way. The sheep are back by the woods. We've got two right now — well, three if you count the lamb. We've got two ewes, Daisy and Wren, and Daisy had a lamb in March. Her name's Pickle. [Laughs] My daughter named her. We milk Daisy and Wren both. Not a lot of milk — sheep don't produce a ton — but enough for our family. We make cheese sometimes too. Sheep cheese is amazing.

Interviewer 2: That sounds wonderful. What breed?

Farmer: They're East Friesian crosses. East Friesian is the classic dairy sheep. They're really sweet — like, they're more like dogs than sheep. They come when you call them. Pickle especially is a complete love. She'll fall asleep in your lap if you let her.

Interviewer 1: How old is your daughter?

Farmer: She's six. She's at her grandparents' today — my mom takes her on Wednesdays so I can have a focused work day. Which is — that's been a lifesaver, honestly. I homeschool her, so I'm with her most of the time. Having one full day a week where I can just go hard on farm work is the difference between functional and not functional.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about homeschooling. That's a big undertaking on top of the farm.

Farmer: It is. And I want to be honest, it's hard. It's a lot. But it's also been incredible. She's outside all the time. She knows things — she knows where milk comes from, she knows what a bee waggle dance is, she knows the names of every flower we grow. She's six and she can identify like 40 different cut flowers. She comes to the market with me some Saturdays and helps make bouquets. So the homeschooling and the farming aren't really separate things for us. They're integrated. She's learning by being part of what we do.

Interviewer 1: What made you decide to homeschool?

Farmer: A few things. After COVID, both of us had this realization that we wanted a different kind of life for her. Not — not anti-school exactly. But we wanted something more flexible, more nature-connected, more aligned with our values. And the schools near us — there's nothing wrong with them but they're conventional and we wanted something different. So we joined a homeschool co-op that meets twice a week, where she does activities with other homeschooled kids, and we do the academic stuff at home. It's working for us. We'll see how it goes as she gets older. We're not committed to homeschooling all the way through — we're taking it year by year.

Interviewer 2: Is there a homeschool community in this area?

Farmer: A surprisingly strong one. Charleston has a real homeschool community across pretty different demographics — there's the religious homeschool community, there's the secular alternative-ed community, there's the unschoolers, there's the classical educators. We're in the secular nature-focused world mostly. And it's been wonderful. Our co-op has families that span — like, politically, religiously, racially — it's a really diverse group of people who all believe their kids learn better outside conventional school. So my daughter is growing up with friends from a lot of different backgrounds and that matters to us.

Interviewer 1: That's beautiful. Let's keep walking — are these the bees?

Farmer: Yes! Okay, these are the hives. So we've got eight here in this little apiary area. I keep them on the edge of the wood line so they have good forage from the wild flowers and the trees and they're a little protected from wind. I do mostly hands-off beekeeping. I'm a treatment-free beekeeper, which is controversial — some beekeepers think I'm reckless, others think it's the only way to do it. My take is — I want bees that can survive without me dosing them with chemicals. I want strong genetics. So I take losses some years. I've lost hives. But the ones that survive are the ones whose offspring will be stronger. It's a long-term thinking versus short-term thinking thing.

Interviewer 2: Do you sell honey?

Farmer: A little. I sell honey at the market in the fall mostly. It's a small side product. The bees are honestly more about pollination for the flowers and just — having them around. They're calming. I love them. And the honey is delicious.

Interviewer 1: How do you think about pollinators more broadly?

Farmer: I think about them all the time. My whole production system is pollinator-friendly because I don't spray. And I keep a lot of flowering things around even outside my production area — there are sections of the property that I've kept wild specifically as pollinator habitat. Native wildflowers, the wood edge, the wet area down by the back of the property where I let the buttonbush grow. And I've added native species deliberately — milkweed for monarchs, swamp sunflower, partridge pea. So the farm is functioning as a pollinator refuge, which is really important to me. The bigger the agricultural landscape gets, the fewer places there are for pollinators to live. Even my small contribution feels meaningful.

Interviewer 2: Were you always interested in this kind of thing? Or did it grow as you started farming?

Farmer: It grew. I was not a nature kid. I grew up in a city. I didn't go camping. I didn't garden as a child. All of this is recent for me. Which I think is actually a good story to tell because it means anyone can come to this. You don't have to be raised on a farm. You don't have to have a green thumb your whole life. You can be a 30-year-old marketing professional who buys some land and learns. The information is out there. The community will welcome you. You just have to start.

Interviewer 1: Can I ask about the chickens? You said you don't have them anymore.

Farmer: Yeah, that's a sore subject. [Laughs] So we had a flock when we first moved out here. We started with maybe 15 hens, all different breeds — we did the rainbow egg thing where you have different colored eggs. We loved them. They were so much fun. The egg quality was incredible. And then we started losing them. Hawks first. Then a fox got into the coop one night and killed seven in one go. Then we had a raccoon issue. We had a great pyrenees for a while, my parents' dog who they couldn't keep anymore, and that helped — but he died of old age about a year ago and we haven't gotten another dog. And without a dog, the chickens just — we couldn't keep them safe.

Interviewer 2: What kind of solutions did you look into?

Farmer: We looked at everything. A proper enclosed run with hardware cloth dug into the ground, an electric fence around the coop, better night-time security, automatic doors that close at dusk. And all of it added up to thousands of dollars that we just don't have right now. The farm isn't profitable yet — I'm still in build-up mode. So spending three or four thousand dollars on chicken infrastructure when I'm trying to keep the flower business growing — it didn't make sense. So we made the hard call. We rehomed our last few hens to a friend who has a more secure setup. And we buy eggs from our neighbor now. Which is fine. She has a beautiful flock, her eggs are great, we pay her $6 a dozen and feel good about it. But I miss having chickens. I really do. And I want to bring them back when I can afford to do it right.

Interviewer 1: How much would it cost to set it up properly, do you think?

Farmer: To do it really right? With a fully enclosed run, electrified perimeter, secure coop, automatic doors — I'd guess four to five thousand dollars for a small flock. Which I know sounds like a lot for chickens. But predator pressure here is intense. We're on the edge of woods. We have foxes, hawks, owls, raccoons, possums, sometimes bobcats. So any chickens we keep have to be fortified or they're going to disappear. And I don't want to keep them in a chicken tractor full-time — that's not the life I want for them. So we wait until we can do it right. Maybe in two years if the business keeps growing.

Interviewer 2: That's a really good case study in small farm economics actually. Like, the gap between what you can imagine doing and what you can afford to do.

Farmer: Yeah, it's huge. And I think that gap is invisible to a lot of people. They see what I'm doing and think — oh, she's living the dream. And in lots of ways I am. But every decision is also a financial decision and there are things I'd love to do that I can't yet. The chickens are one. A second hoop house is another — I want one for season extension but it'd be five grand minimum. A proper walk-in cooler. Better wash station for the flowers. The list is long. It all costs money. And the flower business is profitable — I want to be clear — but it's not very profitable. Not yet. So we make tradeoffs every year.

Interviewer 1: Let's pivot a little — what's your day actually like?

Farmer: Okay. So spring through fall, my mornings start early. I'm up around 5:30. The first thing I do — before I even shower, before coffee — is go out and check the flowers and cut what needs cutting. Because flowers cut in the cool of the morning hold up so much better than flowers cut later in the day. So 5:30 to 7 I'm in the field with my pruners. Then I come back in, get cleaned up, my daughter is usually waking up around then, my husband makes breakfast. He's been amazing about that — he takes mornings with her so I can be in the field. We do schoolwork from like 9 to 11. Then I take her out with me for the late morning, she helps with planting or weeding or — usually she ends up just playing in the field, which is fine. We have lunch around noon. Naptime for her, which is now more quiet time since she's six, but she still rests for an hour. That hour is when I do my desk work — invoicing, social media, ordering supplies. Then afternoon is more field work or processing flowers, getting ready for market or a delivery. Evening she helps me check the bees if it's a bee day. Dinner together. Bed. Repeat.

Interviewer 2: That's intense.

Farmer: It is. And I'm not going to lie, some days are too much. I've had breakdowns. There were weeks last summer where I was just — I was so tired and there was so much to do and I felt like I was failing as a mom and as a farmer at the same time. Which I think a lot of women in this space feel. We're trying to do all the things and some weeks all the things is too many things. But then there are days where I'm cutting flowers in the morning and the light is gorgeous and my daughter is making me a crown out of weeds and I'm just — I'm in love with this life. So it's both. It's both, all the time.

Interviewer 1: How does your husband fit into it?

Farmer: He works a regular job remote and he handles a lot of the household stuff so I can farm. He's incredibly supportive. He'll come out and help on weekends with heavy lifting or building. But the farm is really my thing. He doesn't want to be a farmer. He's happy with his work and he's happy that I'm doing what I love. We're a team, but our roles are different. And I think that's something else that's not always visible — successful small farms often have a partner with off-farm income, even if that partner isn't farming. The farm doesn't fund the household yet. His job funds the household and the farm income gets reinvested into the farm.

Interviewer 2: That's such a common pattern across the conversations we've had.

Farmer: I'd bet. Because small farming is brutal economically in the first five to ten years. You're building infrastructure, building soil, building customer base, building knowledge — and you're paying for all of that while making barely any money. The farms that survive that period usually have either a partner's income, savings, family money, or off-farm work by one of the principals. Pure full-time first-generation farming with no outside income — almost nobody is doing that successfully. The ones who claim to be are usually leaving something out of the story.

Interviewer 1: What do you see for the future of the farm?

Farmer: I want to grow it carefully. More production from this same footprint. Better infrastructure — the hoop house, the cooler, the wash station. I want to get the chickens back. I want to expand the bee operation a little. And I want to bring in an apprentice or a part-time helper eventually. Right now it's just me out there and that limits how much I can do. If I had one good person two days a week, I could probably double my production. But I'm not ready for that yet because I want to make sure I can pay someone fairly. I'm not going to bring on labor I can't compensate well.

Interviewer 2: What about the long term — 10 years out?

Farmer: I want to be doing this in ten years, absolutely. I want my daughter to grow up with this being part of her life, whether she goes off and does something else as an adult or whether she stays involved. I want to be a known flower farm in the Charleston area. I want to be teaching — I'd love to host workshops eventually, teach beginning flower farmers, share what I've learned. And I want to be financially stable. That's the big one. I want this to be a real business that pays its own way, not just a passion project supported by my husband's income.

Interviewer 1: Anything you wish you'd known when you started?

Farmer: How long it takes. I thought I'd be profitable by year three. I'm in year four and I'm still not where I thought I'd be. So patience — way more patience than I came in with. And honestly, I wish I'd known how lonely the work can be. I'm an extrovert. I worked in offices for ten years. I miss having coworkers some days. Farming alone all week is not what I thought it would be. The market on Saturdays is where I get my people fix and I need it desperately by the time Saturday comes around.

Interviewer 2: Have you found community with other farmers?

Farmer: Yes, gradually. There's a small group of flower farmers around Charleston that I'm close with. We text. We help each other when someone needs a specific flower for a wedding they can't supply. We're not really competing because the demand is bigger than what any of us can produce. So we collaborate, which has been wonderful. And the broader market community at Charleston is — I mentioned this — really tight. We're a family. So I have my people. But it took a couple years to find them. The first year I was really isolated.

Interviewer 1: What would you say to a young woman thinking about getting into flower farming?

Farmer: Go work on a flower farm for a season first. Please. Before you spend any money. Find a farmer who'll let you apprentice for a season, work with them, see if you actually love the work. Because the work is the thing. Not the Instagram aesthetic. The work — the bending, the cutting, the heat, the bug bites, the planting in cold mud in February. If you love the work, you can figure the rest out. If you don't love the work, no amount of beautiful Pinterest dreams will get you through the hard parts. And then — start small. So much smaller than you think. A quarter acre. Honestly, a tenth of an acre. Get good at that first. Don't buy 20 acres and order $10,000 of bulbs in year one. Start where you can succeed and scale from there.

Interviewer 2: Anything else?

Farmer: I'd say — don't take on debt. Or take on as little as you can. The debt is what kills people. I've watched farmers go under because they took out big loans to build infrastructure they couldn't service with their income. Bootstrap if you can. Build slowly. Pay cash for everything. It will take you longer to get where you're going but you'll get there with a business that can survive.

Interviewer 1: That's great advice. We're going to wrap up soon — anything we missed that you want to say?

Farmer: I want to say something about being a Black flower farmer specifically. Because it doesn't come up a lot in this kind of conversation but it matters to me. There aren't many Black farmers, period, in this country anymore. There are even fewer in the flower farming world. Most of the flower farms you see on Instagram are white women on farms in Washington or Maine or upstate New York. There's nothing wrong with that. But I'm here. And I want to be visible. Because there are young Black women out there who could love this work and don't know it's an option for them. So I try to be in the spaces where my picture being there matters. The flower farming conferences. The social media. The Charleston market. I show up. And I think that matters even when I'm not explicitly talking about it. The presence is the message.

Interviewer 2: That's powerful.

Farmer: I think so. And the Black farming community is small but it's growing. There are some incredible Black farmers in South Carolina doing all kinds of things — rice, vegetables, pork, you name it. And there's a sense of — of reclaiming. Black families lost so much land in this country. Being a Black landowner farming her own land — that's not nothing, historically. So I carry that. Not as a burden, but as a privilege. I get to do something a lot of my ancestors didn't get to do.
