Interviewer 1: Can you explain what that is for people who don't know the term?

Scholar: Sure. So the land sparing versus land sharing debate is one of the longest-running debates in conservation biology and agroecology. The simple version goes like this — if we want to produce food and also protect biodiversity and ecosystem function, what's the better strategy? Option A — land sparing — says: farm as intensively as possible on the smallest possible footprint, get the maximum yield per acre, and use the land you don't need for wild habitat. Option B — land sharing — says: farm in a way that integrates biodiversity directly into agricultural land. Lower yields per acre, but wildlife and ecological function are embedded throughout the landscape. There's no separate spared land. The farmed land is also habitat.

Interviewer 2: Which side are you on?

Scholar: [Laughs] I knew that was coming. The honest answer is — neither, fully. I'm increasingly convinced that the framing itself is the problem. The dichotomy doesn't capture how real landscapes work. There's a researcher named Claire Kremen — she's at UBC now, was at Berkeley for a long time — who's done some of the best work on this. She argues we need a "both-and" framing. Large protected areas surrounded by wildlife-friendly farmed matrices, working together synergistically rather than as competing strategies. I think she's mostly right.

Interviewer 1: So they're not mutually exclusive?

Scholar: Right. The original debate framed them as if you had to choose. And there are real empirical studies on both sides. There was a major paper by Phalan and colleagues in 2011 that found land sparing performed better for biodiversity in two specific case studies. There've been other studies pushing back, especially from researchers working in temperate mixed-use landscapes who find that diversified farming systems can support significant biodiversity. A meta-analysis from earlier this year actually suggested that for over half of the cases reviewed, a combination of sharing and sparing at the landscape level performed best. So the empirical picture is genuinely mixed. And I think that mixed picture is itself the answer — context matters enormously.

Interviewer 2: So what's the implication of that for farmers and policymakers?

Scholar: It means simple answers don't work. The "right" approach depends on where you are, what species you're trying to protect, what production systems make sense for the climate and culture, what the history of the land is, what alternative land uses exist. In some tropical contexts, sparing might genuinely be better — concentrating production on small, high-yield footprints and protecting large remaining habitats. In other contexts, especially in temperate mosaic landscapes like much of the eastern US, sharing through agroecology might do more for both biodiversity and food production. There's no global answer.

Interviewer 1: And this connects to your own farming, right? You have an orchard?

I'm experimenting with different approaches in different sections of the orchard so I can compare them directly over time.

Interviewer 2: What about technology? Like, where does precision agriculture and AI fit into the conversation?

Scholar: Complicated. There are technologies that genuinely support regenerative practices — precision soil testing, drone imagery for pasture monitoring, GPS-guided rotational systems. The tools themselves aren't inherently good or bad. It's how they're deployed and who controls them. The technology question always reduces to a power question for me. Who owns the data? Who profits? Interviewer 1: How has your thinking on land sparing versus land sharing evolved?

Scholar: I've gotten less dogmatic. Five years ago I was more squarely in the land sharing camp — basically, agroecology as the answer. Now I think the picture is more nuanced. There are contexts where intensification on a small footprint genuinely is the better outcome — if it actually leads to protected habitat being conserved rather than just farmed by someone else. The "leakage" problem is real. You spare land here and someone else clears land elsewhere because demand is global. So the sparing logic only works if there's actual protection paired with the intensification. Which is a big if. In practice, I think most landscapes need a mosaic — protected core areas, agriculturally intensive zones that produce a lot, and a wildlife-friendly farming matrix that connects them. The pure either-or framing doesn't match the complexity of real landscapes.

Interviewer 2: How does that inform what you tell individual farmers?

Scholar: I usually ask them: what do you want your farm to be in 30 years? And then we work backward from there. If they want a fully integrated wildlife-friendly farm where every acre is doing multiple things — that's a sharing approach. We can talk about diversification and biodiversity-supporting practices. Both can be done well. Both can be done badly. The values question comes first. The technical question comes after.
