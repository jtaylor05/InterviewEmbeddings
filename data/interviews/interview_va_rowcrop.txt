Interviewer 1: Can you say more about that distinction?

Farmer: Yeah, this is something I think about constantly. So in commodity agriculture, the dominant mindset has been yield maximization for decades. The whole industry — the seed companies, the chemical companies, the equipment dealers, the universities — has been organized around helping farmers produce more bushels per acre. And there was a time when that was the right goal. Yields were the constraint. If you could grow more, you'd make more. Simple. But that hasn't been true for a long time. The constraint now is input costs. Seed is expensive. Fertilizer is expensive. Chemicals are expensive. Equipment is expensive. Land taxes are expensive. So the question for any farmer is not — how do I produce more? It's — how do I produce the right amount with the lowest possible input cost? Net profit is the goal. Not gross yield. Most farmers I know cannot answer that question precisely because they've never thought about it.

Interviewer 2: You can?

Farmer: I can. Because I'm a businessperson first and a farmer second. I keep detailed records. I know my cost per bushel for every crop I grow. I know my breakeven on every field. I know which practices add to my bottom line and which subtract. And those records are why I do what I do. Reducing nitrogen by 40% saved me thousands of dollars a year. Going to no-till saved me equipment costs and fuel and labor. Cover crops cost me money in seed but they saved me more in fertility and erosion. The decisions only make sense once you can see them on a spreadsheet. Most of my neighbors don't keep that kind of record. They farm by tradition. Tradition is expensive.

Interviewer 1: That's a pretty harsh assessment of your neighbors.

Farmer: It is, and I'd appreciate you being careful with it. I love these people. I've known some of them my whole life. They're hard workers. They're decent neighbors. They show up when there's trouble. I'm not putting them down as people. I'm describing a pattern I've observed in commercial agriculture broadly, not just here. Most farmers — most farmers across the country — are not financially sophisticated. They're great at producing crops. They're not as great at running businesses. And the financial sophistication is the difference between farms that thrive and farms that go under. That's not a moral judgment. It's an observation about skill sets.

Interviewer 2: Why do you think farmers as a group are like that?

Farmer: A few reasons. One — the culture of farming privileges production over business. You're a real farmer if you grow good crops. You're not really evaluated as a businessperson. So farmers don't develop those skills. Two — financial information in agriculture is opaque. The companies selling you stuff don't want you to do the math, because the math doesn't favor them. Seed companies, chemical companies — they're great at making you feel like more is better. They're not great at helping you figure out what you actually need. Three — there's a kind of identity attachment to maximum production that goes deep. Farmers measure themselves against their neighbors by yield. Nobody asks at the co-op what your net income was. They ask what you got per acre. So the incentive structure is wrong, basically. You optimize for what gets measured, and the wrong thing has been getting measured for a long time.

Interviewer 1: Has that started to shift at all?

Farmer: Slowly. I've seen more discussion of profitability and cost per bushel in trade publications in the last few years. The big ag economics extension folks are more focused on it. But the cultural change is slow. The farmers who change are usually the ones who've been forced to — they've come close to going under and they had to rethink. The ones who are still solvent see no reason to change. The system keeps reinforcing itself.

The bankruptcies come a little sooner. And the rural economy hollows out a little more. It's not visible day to day but the trajectory is real. So I'd love to see more investment in this stuff, not less. And I want to be careful about that statement because I work for the state. Interviewer 2: Are you ever frustrated with how those events are run?

Farmer: Sometimes. There's a culture in some agricultural conferences that I find off-putting. Too much vendor presence pushing products. Too much consultant-speak. Too much overstating the certainty of practices that haven't been adequately tested. And I think the regenerative ag world specifically has gotten — there's a religious quality to some of it. Some practitioners talk about it like they've found the one true way. And that turns me off. Because it's not the one true way. It's a set of practices that work in some contexts and don't work as well in others. And the certainty makes the movement less credible to farmers who are pragmatic. Interviewer 1: That's a really thoughtful position.

Farmer: I've earned it the hard way. I tried things that didn't work. I overreached early on. I had years where I cut inputs too aggressively and saw yield declines that hurt my bottom line. So I know that this stuff has to be done carefully and gradually. There's a learning curve. And the regenerative ag movement at its evangelistic edge sometimes doesn't acknowledge that. Which is doing a disservice to farmers who try to follow that advice and lose their farms.

Interviewer 2: Can you say more about that?

Farmer: Yeah. So I've seen — I can't tell you specific names, but I've seen farmers go to a workshop or read a book and come back so fired up about regenerative practices that they make big changes to their operation in a single year. And the first year of major change in a row crop system is rough. You lose yield, often substantially. Your costs change in ways you didn't expect. Your equipment requirements shift. And if you don't have the financial cushion to absorb a bad year or three — you're in trouble. So the regenerative narrative that this stuff is straightforward and works immediately is dangerous. The reality is that it takes years and requires capital reserves to absorb the transition. Most farmers don't have those reserves. So they can't make the change even if they want to.

Interviewer 1: How did you have the reserves?

Farmer: Several factors. One — my family land is paid off, going back to my grandfather's purchase. Two — my father saved aggressively and didn't expand beyond what he could fund himself. Five — my wife works as a nurse in town, which adds household income. So the farm doesn't have to cover everything. I have multiple financial buffers. And those buffers are what let me experiment. Without them, I couldn't take the risks I've taken. Which is — that's a structural problem in American agriculture. The farmers who can do the most experimentation are the ones who least need to, financially speaking. The farmers who need to change the most can't afford to. It's perverse.

Interviewer 2: That's a great point.

Farmer: It's one of the reasons I'm careful about how I present my work. I don't want to make it sound like any farmer can do this. They can't. Farmer: Stronger cost-share programs that buffer farmers through transition periods. More accessible technical assistance. Interviewer 1: That sounds healthy.

Farmer: It is. And it's something that's helped sustain the marriage through some tough patches. Farmers' marriages are hard. The work is consuming. The stress is constant. She doesn't need to care about cover crop ratios. I don't need to care about her garden tomato varieties. We let each other have our own things.

Interviewer 2: Let's talk about the bigger picture for a minute. You said earlier you think a lot about scaling regen ag. What are the central problems?

Farmer: Okay, this is something I could talk about for a long time. So the central problem, I think, is that regenerative agriculture as currently practiced is a set of techniques developed and refined on relatively small operations, mostly by passionate practitioners with strong personal commitments to it. And the question is whether those techniques can scale up — whether they can be adopted by mainstream commercial agriculture, which is largely large-scale, mechanized, focused on commodity crops, and motivated by margins not values. That's a big jump.

Interviewer 1: What makes it hard?

Farmer: Several things. One — many regenerative practices were developed for diversified small farms with mixed enterprises. Cover cropping is one of the few that scales well to row crop systems. Most other practices don't translate directly. Integrated livestock grazing is great in theory but tough to implement at 1,000-acre row crop scale because it requires complex management and infrastructure that conflicts with mechanized cropping. So you can't just import small-farm regenerative practices into large operations wholesale.

Interviewer 2: Two?

Farmer: Two — the economics get worse at scale in some ways. A small farm can sell premium-priced products directly to consumers. A 2,000-acre row crop farm sells into commodity markets that don't pay premiums for regenerative practices. So the financial incentive to change doesn't exist for most large operations. Unless someone creates premium markets for regenerative commodities — which is happening slowly with carbon markets and some sustainability programs from buyers — there's no economic reason to change at scale.

Interviewer 1: Three?

Farmer: Three — equipment and infrastructure are mismatched. The equipment that exists for large-scale agriculture is optimized for conventional practices. The equipment for regenerative practices is often custom or small-scale. Scaling up means either retrofitting equipment, which is expensive, or waiting for manufacturers to develop new equipment, which is slow. So there's an infrastructure gap.

Interviewer 2: Are there things that do scale?

Farmer: Yes. Cover cropping scales. No-till scales — there's a substantial no-till acreage in the US already. Reduced tillage scales. Variable rate application of inputs scales — that's not technically regenerative but it reduces unnecessary input use. Precision agriculture in general can support more efficient input use. So some practices scale. The deeper integrated practices — building diverse polycultures, integrating livestock, complex rotations — those don't scale as easily.

Interviewer 1: What's your honest assessment of what's achievable?

Farmer: I think we can substantially reduce the input intensity of large-scale row crop agriculture over the next 20 years. That's achievable. I think we can build soil health back up across millions of acres of currently degraded farmland. That's achievable. I think we can reduce off-site impacts — water pollution, soil erosion, greenhouse gas emissions — significantly. All of that is achievable with existing techniques scaled up appropriately. What I'm more skeptical about is the transformation of industrial agriculture into something fundamentally different. I don't think we're going to turn the Midwest into agroforestry over my lifetime. The commodity system is too entrenched, the equipment too sunk-cost, the financial structures too rigid. So I think we can make industrial agriculture significantly less destructive without transforming it into something else. And that incremental improvement is worth pursuing. It's not the revolutionary change some advocates want. But it's real.

Interviewer 2: Some regen ag advocates would push back on that view.

Farmer: They do. And I respect their position, even when I disagree. The aspirational vision matters. Without people pushing for the bigger transformation, the incremental improvements wouldn't happen either. So there's a healthy ecosystem of voices in the movement. The dreamers, the pragmatists, the practitioners, the researchers, the policymakers. We need all of them. My role is the pragmatist. Other people fill other roles. The movement is bigger than any one perspective.

Interviewer 1: How does that play out at the policy level?

Farmer: It's complicated. Policy makers respond to evidence and to political pressure. The evidence base for incremental regenerative practices is solid. The evidence for revolutionary transformation is weaker, mostly because we haven't tested it at scale. So policy tends to support the incremental stuff — cover crop cost-share, no-till incentives, soil health programs. The bigger transformation requires policy infrastructure that doesn't exist — different commodity markets, different procurement systems, different incentive structures. Building that is a multi-decade project. And right now there's not the political support for it.

Interviewer 2: What about climate-smart agriculture specifically?

Farmer: That's been an interesting development. The investments through the Inflation Reduction Act were substantial — but many of those funds have been delayed, redirected, or restructured under the current administration. Some farmers who had been counting on that funding for cover crop and soil health work have had projects stalled. The label "climate-smart" itself has become politically toxic in some circles, which is unfortunate because the underlying practices are valuable regardless of how you label them. So we've seen some programs continue under different names, some get cut, some operate in limbo. It's been disruptive. And again — I have to be careful here — but from my position in the state office, I see how that disruption affects real farmers who were trying to do the right thing. They made plans based on commitments that aren't being honored. That damages trust in the program structure itself, which will be hard to rebuild.

Interviewer 1: Let me ask you a different kind of question — what would success look like, for you personally, in your remaining career?

Farmer: I'm 51. I've got probably 15 active years left in me if my health holds, maybe more if I take care of myself. So success looks like — continuing to refine my management and learn from my fields. Continuing to share what I learn through whatever channels make sense — research collaboration, talks at conferences, mentorship of younger farmers in my orbit. Holding the line on the state job long enough to retire with the pension. Setting up the farm so it could transition smoothly to my daughter if she wants it, or to a careful renter if she doesn't. And living a good life. I want — I want quiet evenings with my wife. I want to keep being able to physically work the farm even as I age. I want to see grandchildren if my daughter has kids. I want to be in good shape to enjoy retirement when it comes. These are not unique ambitions. They're pretty ordinary. But that's success for me.

Interviewer 2: That's a really grounded answer.

Farmer: I've come to value being grounded. The bigger ambitions of younger me — there was a time I thought I was going to be a public figure in the agricultural conservation world. Speaking everywhere, writing books, that kind of thing. And I've come to see that as somewhat empty. The people who matter to me are the ones I see regularly. The work that matters is the work I do here on the land. So the grandiose vision has receded and the local has gotten more important. Which I think is — that's middle age. You realize what actually matters.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything you regret about how you've done things?

Farmer: A few things, yeah. I regret some of the experimental decisions I made in the early years. I lost money on some things that I should have been more careful about. I regret being too aggressive in arguing with conventional neighbors when I was younger. I came at them with too much certainty and probably damaged some relationships that could have been salvaged. I regret not taking better care of my body — I have back issues now that come from years of doing things I shouldn't have done unaided. And I regret that I wasn't a more present father when my daughter was young. I was so consumed with the farm that I missed things. My wife covered for me, beautifully. But I should have been there more.

Interviewer 2: Those are honest regrets.

Farmer: They're real. And — I try to do better now. I try to be more present. I try to be more humble with neighbors. I try to take care of myself. The regrets shape me. They don't destroy me. That's the goal.

Interviewer 1: What's something most people don't understand about commercial row crop farming?

Farmer: How thin the margins are. People hear that a farmer grossed $400,000 last year and they think that's a lot of money. They don't realize that the input costs were $350,000 and the equipment costs spread over years are another $30,000 and the land costs and taxes are another $15,000 and the family pulls maybe $40,000 out of that operation in a good year. Tens of thousands of dollars of effort and risk for a household income that's middle class at best. And in a bad year, you lose money. You can do this work for decades and end up with less than you started with. The romance about farming is so thick that it obscures the financial reality. People assume farmers are wealthy because they own land. The land is the only thing keeping us going — and many farmers don't even own theirs.

Interviewer 2: What about what gets understood as a positive — like the lifestyle benefits?

Farmer: The lifestyle is real. I love this life. I love being outside. I love working with my hands when I can. I love the rhythms of the seasons. I love the quiet. I love watching things grow. I love the independence. So the lifestyle benefits are not a myth. They're genuine and they're what keeps a lot of farmers in it through the rough patches. But they don't pay the mortgage. Or in my case the property taxes. So you can't substitute lifestyle for income. People sometimes try to. They take a financial loss every year because they love the lifestyle. And eventually they go broke. So I'd say — the lifestyle benefits are real but they're not a substitute for a viable financial model. You need both. And finding both is rare.

Interviewer 1: Last few questions. What would you tell a young farmer who's interested in incorporating regenerative practices but is intimidated by where to start?

Farmer: Start small. Pick one practice. Maybe cover cropping on a single field. Maybe reducing tillage on a single field. Try it for three years. Measure what happens. Compare it to your other fields. Learn what's different about your specific land. Don't change everything at once. Don't try to follow some popular guru's prescription wholesale. Build your own knowledge through trial in small portions of your operation. And keep good records. Without records you can't tell what's working. Most farmers don't keep records detailed enough to evaluate change. That's the first thing to fix. Then experiment. Then expand what works. Slow and incremental. That's the path that doesn't bankrupt you.

Interviewer 2: And to a young person thinking about getting into farming with no background?

Farmer: Don't romanticize it. Work on a farm for at least a full season before you commit to anything. See what the work actually is. See what the finances actually look like. Talk to multiple farmers — not just the ones with charismatic social media, but the quiet operators who've been doing it for decades. They have more wisdom than the influencers. Build relationships. Don't try to buy land in year one. Lease, intern, work for someone. The barriers to becoming a farmer are mostly capital and skill. Get the skill first. The capital will be a lifelong project anyway. And be prepared for the loneliness. Farming is isolating in ways that aren't visible from outside. You can be wealthy in time outside and poor in human company. So plan for the social side of your life intentionally.

Interviewer 1: Last one — anything we haven't asked that you'd want to say?

Farmer: I'd say something about how I think about this work in the context of the broader story. I'm a small character in a long story. My grandfather started this farm. My father expanded it. I've refined it. Maybe my daughter takes it next. Maybe someone else does. The land will outlive all of us. The decisions I make on it will affect what kind of land it is for whoever comes next. That's the time horizon I try to keep in mind. And it's not just about my family's land — it's about the broader landscape, the watershed, the ecosystem, the rural community. We're all stewards of something that's bigger than us. And I think when I started farming I didn't fully feel that. I felt like I was trying to maximize my own income or build my own legacy. Now I feel more like I'm passing through. Holding the land carefully for the next people. Which is, I think, a healthier way to do this work. And I'd want anyone reading what you write to understand that — that the best farmers I know aren't focused on themselves. They're focused on the land and the future.

Interviewer 2: That's a beautiful place to end.

Farmer: Yeah. And — please — remember the anonymity. Don't put me in the public record on this stuff. I've been honest with you because I trust that. So just — be careful with how you use it.

Interviewer 1: We absolutely will. Thank you so much.

Farmer: You're welcome. Y'all drive safe getting back.

[The interview continued after a brief break for water. The farmer suggested they walk out to one of the fields where the cover crop was establishing. The recording resumed in the field.]

Farmer: So this is what I wanted to show you. This is the field that's in soybean residue with the cover crop just coming up. Can you see the green just starting? That's cereal rye, crimson clover, hairy vetch, daikon radish, all coming up at once. In another six weeks this field is going to be a green carpet. By spring it'll be knee-high. And then I'll roll it down or terminate it and plant cotton straight into the residue. The cotton seed goes through the residue into the soil underneath. No tillage. No bare soil. No erosion. The residue acts as mulch, holds moisture, suppresses weeds, feeds the soil biology. It's a system.

Interviewer 1: How long did it take you to get this dialed in?

Farmer: Eight, ten years to really feel like I had it figured out. Lots of failures along the way. I had years where the cover crop didn't establish well because of weather, years where I terminated too late and the residue was too thick to plant through, years where I had unexpected pest issues from the new habitat the residue created. Each problem taught me something. And gradually the system stabilized. Now in most years it works the way it's supposed to. But I tell people — don't expect this to look like this in year one. Year one is going to be messy. Year three is going to be tolerable. Year ten is when you start to feel like you know what you're doing.

Interviewer 2: That's a long timeline.

Farmer: It is. And most farmers don't have that timeline. They need to be profitable next year, not in ten years. Which is one of the structural barriers. The financial pressure makes long-term transitions difficult. You'd need bridge financing or alternative income streams to survive the transition period. Which is part of what programs like NRCS cost-share are supposed to do, when they're working well.

Interviewer 1: Let me ask a more technical question. When you reduced your nitrogen by 40%, did you do that gradually or all at once?

Farmer: Gradually. Over years. And with careful monitoring. So the first year I cut by maybe 10%. I monitored yields, monitored corn tissue tests, monitored visual signs in the field. The yields held. So the next year I cut by another 10%. Held again. And I kept stepping down until I started to see yield pressure. Then I held at that level. And then in subsequent years, as the cover crop system matured and the soil biology built up, I was able to step down further. So the cut wasn't a one-time decision. It was a gradual calibration over a decade. And the right level for me probably wouldn't be the right level for someone else with different soils, different rotations, different cover crop mixes. The general principle is — you can cut substantially if your soil is improving. The specific number requires field-level data.

Interviewer 2: How are you measuring soil improvement?

Farmer: A few ways. Standard soil tests every two or three years. We do those. They show organic matter, pH, nutrient levels, that kind of thing. My organic matter has gone from about 1.8% when I started cover cropping to over 4% now in the best fields. That's significant. I also do less formal observations — how the soil looks and smells when I dig in it, how water moves through it, how easily roots go through it. The slime when you crush a clump in your hand — that's a sign of fungal activity. The crumb structure — that comes from good biology. Earthworms — we have way more earthworms than we used to. So both formal and informal observation, and they tell a consistent story.

Interviewer 1: What about the research collaborations — what specifically have they measured?

Farmer: They've done more sophisticated stuff. Soil carbon at multiple depths. Microbial biomass through phospholipid fatty acid analysis. Nematode community composition. Soil enzyme activity. Greenhouse gas flux measurements. Aggregate stability tests. So they've been able to verify what I was seeing informally. The carbon increases are real. The microbial communities are more diverse and abundant. The soil structure is genuinely improving. Which is satisfying because — confirmation bias is real. I want to believe what I'm doing is working. So having outside researchers come in and measure with sophisticated tools and confirm — that gives me more confidence than my own observations alone could.

Interviewer 2: Have they ever found something that surprised you?

Farmer: A few things. They found that my soil biology was responding faster than the published literature would have suggested. Some of the changes that researchers thought would take 20 years were happening in 10 in my system. They speculated that this might be because of the diversity of the cover crop mixes I was using — that single-species cover cropping might be slower than multi-species. That observation led to one of the papers we co-authored. They also found that certain microbial groups were responding in unexpected ways to specific cover crop combinations. The science around cover crop mixes is still emerging and there's a lot we don't know about which combinations do what. I get to be part of that learning process, which is a privilege.

Interviewer 1: Has the financial benefit been substantial?

Farmer: Yeah. Let me give you some real numbers, with the caveat that I want you to be vague about them in any final product. Roughly speaking — my nitrogen costs are about $30,000 less per year than they would be at conventional rates for my acreage. My fuel costs are maybe $8,000 less because of reduced tillage. My pest control costs are probably $5,000 less because the system has more natural pest suppression. So we're talking about $40,000 to $45,000 a year in reduced costs. The cover crop seed costs me about $15,000 a year, depending on the mix. So net savings of $25,000 to $30,000 a year. On a 300-acre operation, that's significant. That's meaningful household income.

Interviewer 2: That's a real number.

Farmer: It is. And it compounds over time because the savings get reinvested. So the financial argument for what I do is not just feel-good. It's substantial. And yet — yet — most farmers don't see it. Because they don't run the math. They see the cost of the cover crop seed as an expense and they see the input reduction as risk. They don't see the net savings as a benefit because they don't track it that carefully. So part of my advocacy is just trying to get farmers to do the math. Show them the actual numbers. Force them to confront what the data actually says about their operations.

Interviewer 1: Do you ever do that with neighbors?

Farmer: Carefully. I don't want to lecture. I don't want to come across as preachy. So when someone asks me — and they do ask, sometimes — I'll show them. I'll pull out my records and walk them through what I track and what I've learned. And occasionally that lands. Most of the time it doesn't. Because it requires the farmer to admit that their existing approach is suboptimal. Which is a hard admission for anyone, especially in a culture that values consistency and tradition. So I share when I'm asked, and I try not to push when I'm not.

Interviewer 2: Let's talk about cotton specifically. That's a crop that's less commonly discussed in regenerative ag circles.

Farmer: Right, cotton is — cotton is interesting. It's a very intensive crop traditionally. High pesticide use, lots of cultivation, picky about weather. And it's been the slowest part of my operation to convert. Some of the regenerative practices I use in corn and soybean don't translate as well to cotton. The crop is more sensitive to early season stress, the equipment is different, the planting and harvesting windows are tighter. So I do less aggressive cover cropping ahead of cotton. I do reduced tillage but not full no-till on the cotton fields. I've reduced my pesticide use but I haven't been able to get it down as much as on the other crops. So cotton is the part of my operation where I'm still doing more conventional practices. And I'm honest about that. I haven't figured out how to do cotton fully in a regenerative system. Other people are working on it — there are some interesting cotton trials in the Carolinas — but I haven't cracked it yet.

Interviewer 1: Why keep growing it if it's the hardest one?

Farmer: It's profitable. The cotton price has been decent the last few years. The infrastructure exists locally for cotton — gins, equipment, marketing. And it's part of the rotation that makes the other crops work. Cotton has different pest and disease pressures than corn and soybean, so rotating it in breaks up some of the pest cycles. So cotton plays a role in the system even though it's the most conventional crop in my operation. I've thought about phasing it out. But the economics keep arguing for keeping it. So it stays for now.

Interviewer 2: How does your operation compare in profitability to your conventional neighbors?

Farmer: That's hard to know precisely because my neighbors don't share their numbers with me. But based on what I can infer from input usage, yields, prices, and equipment expenditures — I'm doing better than most of them, financially. Not dramatically better. But meaningfully better. My costs are lower. My yields are competitive. So my net margin is higher. And in years that are tough for everyone — drought years, low price years — I'm more resilient because my margin has cushion. That's been the real test. In bad years, conventional farmers go under at higher rates than I do. The system I've built has more shock absorption.

Interviewer 1: Has anyone tried to follow your lead?

Farmer: A few, slowly. There's a younger farmer about 15 miles from here who's been gradually shifting his operation in similar directions. He's been visiting my farm for years and asking questions. He's not converted to everything I do but he's adopted the cover cropping and most of the reduced tillage. His numbers are improving. He'll probably end up where I am in 10 or 15 years if he keeps going. There's another guy — older, my age — who started cover cropping after seeing what I was doing. Less aggressive than me but he's stuck with it for five years now. So there's incremental adoption happening locally. Not a revolution. But not nothing.

Interviewer 2: How do you think about the political moment we're in for this work?

Farmer: With concern. The conditions for the kind of farming I do are getting harder, not easier. The federal funding picture is worse. The political environment around climate-related agriculture is more hostile. The market signals for regenerative practices are inconsistent. And the cultural pressure on farmers to either conform fully to industrial practices or commit fully to organic certification — neither of which I do — has gotten more polarized. So the middle ground I occupy is a less comfortable place to be than it was five years ago.

Interviewer 1: How do you respond to that?

Farmer: I keep doing what I'm doing. I'm not going to change my practices based on political winds. The data supports what I'm doing. The economics support what I'm doing. The land tells me it's working. So I continue. But I'm more aware now of the broader landscape and how it might affect other farmers trying to do similar things. The pipeline of farmers experimenting with these practices is going to slow if the support structures keep getting weaker. And that's a long-term problem for American agriculture and rural communities, even if it's invisible day to day.

Interviewer 2: Let me ask you something I've been wondering — what role do you think your part-time state job plays in how you think about all this?

Farmer: A big one, I think. It exposes me to perspectives I wouldn't get otherwise. I visit farms across the state through that work. I see practices that work and practices that fail. I see the diversity of approaches and the diversity of farmers. I see the policy dimension up close. And I'm in conversation with state employees who are thinking about agriculture from administrative and analytical angles I wouldn't naturally think from. That broader exposure shapes my farming decisions. It's also probably why I'm more philosophical about this work than some of my neighbors. I see it as part of a larger system. Most farmers see their own farm and that's it. Which is fair — that's the immediate reality. But I've been forced to think bigger, and I think that's been useful.

Interviewer 1: Do you ever consider quitting the state job?

Farmer: Sometimes. The administrative side gets tiring. The political pressures get tiring. There are days when I think — I could just farm full-time, keep things simpler, focus only on what I can control. But then I think about the benefits and the pension and the broader knowledge I get from it, and I stay. And honestly the income matters. Even with what I save through my practices, the farm by itself wouldn't fully support my household at the standard of living we have. The state job income is genuinely important to our finances. So practical reasons keep me in it.

Interviewer 2: What would you say to other farmers considering taking on supplementary employment?

Farmer: I'd say — yes. Find supplementary income. It's one of the most underappreciated strategies for farm viability. Whether it's a part-time job, contract work, a spouse with off-farm income, agritourism, content creation, a side business — having income that doesn't depend on the farm itself takes pressure off the farm operation. It lets you make better long-term decisions. It buffers you through bad years. It opens up experimental possibilities. The mythology of the full-time farmer making a living entirely from production is rarely true and usually unhealthy when it is. Most successful farmers I know have multiple income streams. They just don't talk about it.

Interviewer 1: That's a striking observation. Why don't farmers talk about it?

Farmer: Pride, partly. The cultural ideal is the farmer who lives off the land entirely. Having off-farm income feels like a failure of that ideal. So farmers don't volunteer that information. Even though almost everyone does it. Which means new farmers entering the field have unrealistic expectations because they don't see the support structures that exist behind successful operations. The truth is more honest and more accessible — farming is one part of a household economic strategy that usually involves multiple components. Owning that truth helps new farmers plan better.

Interviewer 2: How does your wife feel about the farm becoming more public — like, you doing research collaborations and conference talks?

Farmer: She's been supportive but cautious. She doesn't want our private life intruded on. She's the reason I'm so careful about anonymity in interviews like this. She doesn't want our name attached to public controversies if she can help it. And I respect that. So my public participation has been calibrated to keep our private life private. I don't post on social media. I don't appear in promotional materials. I don't do TV. I do research talks where the audience is professional. I do panels at conferences. I do this kind of research interview when I trust the researchers. But I draw the line at anything that would bring attention to our home or our family. She and I have agreed on that pretty firmly.

Interviewer 1: Has it worked? Like, has the privacy held?

Farmer: Mostly. There's been a few times when journalists got to me and didn't honor the agreement we made. One major outlet wrote about my farm and used my full name and town despite a clear understanding that they wouldn't. That was bad. We had some unwanted attention for a while after that. I learned to be more careful about who I talk to. Researchers and academics have generally been better about anonymity than journalists. Farmer: Don't make me regret it. [Laughs] I'm joking but not entirely. Just — be careful.

Farmer: Yes. It's given me a different kind of confidence in the methods. When you've co-authored peer-reviewed papers on something, you know the data has been scrutinized by other experts. Farmer: I try to engage with respect. When I disagree with someone publicly — at a panel, in a journal — I focus on the substance, not the person. Farmer: I'd rather not name names. There are some prominent regenerative ag voices whose specific claims I'm skeptical of. They overstate the certainty of their findings. Interviewer 1: What about the universities — are they doing right by farmers?

Farmer: Mixed. The land grant university system has been historically the engine of agricultural research and extension in this country. Some of that work has been excellent and has genuinely helped farmers. Some of it has been captured by industry interests and has produced research that supports the agricultural-industrial complex rather than questioning it. The cover crop and soil health research has been really strong over the last 20 years. The work on integrated pest management has been valuable. The work on water conservation has been important. But there's also a lot of university research that's effectively industry promotion. So I distinguish between specific research programs and individual researchers, not the system as a whole. The Virginia Tech folks I work with are exemplary. There are others elsewhere who I'm more skeptical of.

Interviewer 2: What about extension specifically?

Farmer: Extension has been weakened over the years through funding cuts. The county-level extension agents who used to be the workhorses of agricultural support are fewer in number and stretched thin. When I was a kid we had a full-time corn specialist in this region. Now we have one specialist covering multiple counties. So farmers have less direct support than they used to. Which has created opportunities for private consultants and chemical company representatives to fill the gap. Which has shifted the information landscape toward industry-aligned advice. So the decline of extension has had downstream consequences that aren't well understood.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything that would restore that?

Farmer: Funding. Mostly. And cultural restoration of the role — making extension careers attractive again. We've been training fewer agricultural professionals at universities partly because the career pathways have been less viable. That feeds the decline. The whole pipeline needs investment. And that requires sustained political will, which isn't currently present. So I'm not optimistic about restoration in the short term.

Interviewer 2: Let's wrap up with — what would you want a researcher writing about your kind of farming to capture about it that often gets missed?

Farmer: A few things. One — that it's not a moral position. I'm not virtuous for farming this way. I'm rational. The economics, the data, the long-term value to the land and family — they all point this direction for me. So please don't write about me as if I'm some kind of crusader. I'm a businessperson making decisions based on evidence and incentives. Two — that the path requires conditions most farmers don't have. Paid-off land, off-farm income, family stability, time horizon to absorb losses. Without those conditions, the path doesn't work. So please don't write as if any farmer can do what I do. Three — that the conventional system has logic of its own. The farmers I describe as making suboptimal decisions are responding to real pressures and real information environments. They're not stupid. They're operating in a system that gives them bad incentives and incomplete information. So please don't write about them with contempt. Their decisions make sense within the constraints they face. Changing those constraints is the bigger project.

Farmer: It's what I've come to believe. And — I'd also say — please be careful with the political stuff. I've talked about funding cuts and policy direction. That's not partisan from my point of view. It's observation. But it can read as political. So just — be thoughtful in how you contextualize that. I'm trying to describe what I see, not to advocate for any particular political outcome.

Farmer: Thank you. Okay — we should probably wrap. I've got some equipment maintenance I need to get to before dark. But I appreciate you both coming out. And — one last reminder — anonymity. Please. Use the substance, leave my name and identifying details out.
