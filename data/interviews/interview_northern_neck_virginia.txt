Farmer: [Laughs] Yes. It is. So I grew up in a city called Ra'anana, which is a suburb north of Tel Aviv. Very urban, very modern. Nothing agricultural about it at all. Interviewer 2: And then you met your wife?

Farmer: And then I met my wife. [Laughs] Which — yes. So I stayed in Vermont for a while after graduating, worked at a research farm connected to the university for two years, and then a friend brought me to a conference in Washington DC and I met Renata at this conference. She's from a family that's had land in the Northern Neck for four generations. Her grandfather and great-grandfather farmed it, her father was a farmer until he shifted into county government, and when we got serious and started talking about our future, she said — we have land. And for me, hearing that there was land and it had this history and it needed someone to bring it back into production — that was like, yes. Obviously yes. Interviewer 1: What was the land like when you took it over?

Farmer: Neglected, mostly. Not abused, which was good. Renata's father had leased some of the crop fields to a neighboring conventional operation for about ten years — soybeans, mostly — but the lease had ended a few years before we arrived and the land was just resting. Which meant the soil biology was not in great shape from the soy years but it had had some time to begin recovering. And then there was a large portion that had always been in woods and had never been touched, which is an extraordinary asset. Old growth trees, a creek running through the lower portion of the property, real wildlife habitat. And some old pasture that was overgrown. We have about 85 acres total. About 20 in active orchard and horticultural production now, maybe 10 in pasture that we use for the chickens, 40-something in woods that we manage but don't produce from, and the rest is the homestead — house, outbuildings, the farm stand, the studio.

Interviewer 2: Let's come back to all of those. I want to ask first about the Israeli background specifically — do you feel like that shapes how you farm or how you think about land?

Farmer: Very much. In ways that are both obvious and less obvious. The obvious one is water. I grew up in a country where water scarcity is existential. You don't waste water. You think about water constantly. Where it goes, how to capture it, how to use it efficiently. And when I got here and saw how people use water in American agriculture — and honestly in American life in general — it was shocking to me. Irrigation systems running in the rain. Drainage systems designed to get water off the land as fast as possible. And so my whole orientation to this property was: how do we keep water in the system? How do we slow it down? We've done extensive swale work on the hillside portions, we've created water retention features, we mulch everything heavily. Water management has been a foundational principle for us in a way that I think surprises some people who come to regenerative farming from a different starting point.

Interviewer 1: What's the less obvious way it shapes things?

Farmer: The sense of impermanence, maybe. Israelis have a very particular relationship with land — it's deeply contested, deeply meaningful, deeply political — and growing up in that context I think you develop an understanding that your relationship with a piece of land is not permanent or guaranteed. You have to earn it every day. You have to be a good steward because the land is not simply yours. And I bring that here, to land that is my wife's family's land in a very literal, multigenerational sense. There's a humility that comes with it. I'm a caretaker. I'm trying to do something worthy of what came before me and what might come after me. And I think that orientation — toward the land as something entrusted to you rather than owned by you — is actually very aligned with regenerative philosophy. The land is not an input. It's a participant.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the orchard and horticultural side — that's the main production enterprise?

Farmer: The main one, yes. So we have about 12 acres in tree fruit — apples, pears, persimmons, pawpaws, which have been a real surprise hit actually, chestnuts, and some quince which is something I brought in because it's used a lot in Middle Eastern cooking and I thought we could find a market for it and it turns out I was right. And then we have about 8 acres in what I call the market garden and berry area — strawberries, blueberries, elderberries, blackberries, and then vegetables through the spring and summer and fall. The trees are at various stages. The older apple and pear trees were already on the property — there had been a small orchard here years ago that had been abandoned — and we've done a lot of restoration work on those. Pruning, removing dead wood, inoculating the soil around them with beneficial fungi. Some of them have responded beautifully. Others were too far gone. The newer trees we've planted ourselves over the past four years, so many of them are still in early production.

Interviewer 1: What does the orchard management look like from a regenerative standpoint?

Farmer: So the foundational practice is the understory. A conventional orchard is basically a monoculture — you have the tree and you have mowed grass or bare ground underneath it. We've planted diverse understory guilds under every tree — species that have different roles. Nitrogen fixers like comfrey, clover, goumi berry. Dynamic accumulators that bring up minerals from deep in the soil — comfrey does this too, it's a remarkable plant. Pollinator habitat — we have flowering plants timed to bloom throughout the whole season so there's always something for the bees. We have four hives on the property and the tree health is directly tied to the pollination quality, so caring for the understory and caring for the bees is the same action. And then mulching. We mulch very heavily, wood chip mulch, which builds organic matter, retains moisture, suppresses weeds, and feeds the fungal networks that the trees depend on. It's a lot of work upfront to establish but it becomes more self-maintaining over time.

Interviewer 2: What about pest management in the orchard — what are you dealing with there?

Farmer: Codling moth in the apples is the big one. Spotted wing Drosophila in the berries later in the season. Brown marmorated stink bug, which is everywhere in this part of Virginia. And fire blight, which is a bacterial disease that can devastate pears and apples in a bad year. We don't use any synthetic pesticides. For codling moth we use kaolin clay and we've been experimenting with mating disruption — pheromone dispensers that confuse the males so they can't find females. It reduces population pressure without any inputs that affect other insects. For spotted wing Drosophila the control options are more limited and I'll be honest, we lose some berries. We harvest frequently and try to stay ahead of it. For fire blight — we prune aggressively to remove infected wood, we spray copper during bloom which is the vulnerable period, and we've made varietal selections that favor fire blight resistance. A resistant variety is worth a hundred spray programs.

Interviewer 1: The chickens — how do those fit in?

Farmer: The chickens are what I call our integration engine. We run about 300 laying hens in a moveable coop system — we have a large tractor-style shelter on wheels that we move every three to five days. Through the pasture areas, through the orchard aisles when the timing is right, through the market garden after a crop comes out. They eat insects, they scratch and aerate the soil, they deposit fertility. And we get eggs, which we sell through the farm stand. The key thing about using chickens in an orchard system is the timing has to be right. You don't want them in there when fruit is on the ground and fermenting — they'll eat it and it can make them sick. And you don't want them in the vegetable garden when anything is in an early growth stage because they will destroy it. But in the right windows — after the apple harvest, before the spring planting — they're doing enormous work. Moving them is the labor intensive part. My son has decided this is his job and he takes it very seriously. He designed a new system for the waterers himself. I told him he should patent it. He is nine years old and he thought about this for a long time and then said, I don't think nine-year-olds can get patents. Which is unfortunately correct.

Interviewer 2: [Laughs] What's the farm stand like — how did that come together?

Farmer: So the farm stand was Renata's project initially. She built it out of reclaimed wood from an old outbuilding we tore down — she's very handy, much handier than me — and we opened it in our second spring here. And it started as just what we were producing, which at that point was not very much. Some herbs, some early vegetables, eggs from the first small flock we had. And then we started reaching out to neighbors and other small farms in the county and asking if they wanted to sell through us, and that's when it became something more interesting. We have now about eight other farms that sell through the stand. Honey from a beekeeper about four miles away. Goat cheese from a small dairy operation a little further north. Pastured pork from a family about twenty minutes from us. Preserves and ferments from a woman down the road who makes the most incredible things. Herbal teas and dried herbs from another neighbor. And our own produce, our own eggs, our own fruit in season.

Interviewer 1: How does the economics of that work — are you taking a cut?

Farmer: We take a percentage — 20% on consignment, which is low for retail, but we're not trying to make money on the stand alone. The stand serves multiple purposes for us. It drives traffic to the property, which supports the yoga studio. It builds relationships with other farmers in the area, which has been enormously valuable beyond just the commercial piece. And it creates a reason for people to come back every week, which builds community loyalty that eventually converts to more of our own product sales too. If we were running it purely as a retail operation we'd take a bigger cut. But it's really an anchor for the whole ecosystem we're trying to build here.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the yoga studio — that's an interesting addition to a farm.

Farmer: It was Renata's idea and I was skeptical at first, I'll admit that. My thinking was: we're a farm, let's be a farm. But she was right and I was wrong, which she reminds me of occasionally. [Laughs] So there's an old tobacco curing barn on the property that was structurally sound but unused. We restored it — again, mostly Renata and her father doing the work — and created a studio space inside it. Very beautiful, actually. Stone floor, original wood beams, we put in big windows on the south wall that look out over the orchard. Renata is a certified yoga instructor. She teaches five classes a week, and we have two other instructors who rent the space and teach their own classes. It runs most of the year except the deep winter when it gets very cold in there and the heating costs become prohibitive.

Interviewer 1: And the clientele — you mentioned the community around you is receptive?

Farmer: Very. The Northern Neck has changed a lot in the past decade. There's been an influx of people from DC and Northern Virginia — retirees, remote workers, people who made money elsewhere and wanted to come somewhere slower and more beautiful. And within that population there is a significant contingent that is very values-aligned with what we're doing. They care about where food comes from, they care about environmental practices, they're interested in mindfulness and physical wellbeing. The yoga studio serves them and so does the farm stand and so does the educational programming we're starting to develop. And then there's a longer-standing local population — some of whom are more traditional, some of whom have been pleasantly surprised by what we're doing. We've made real efforts to be part of the existing community and not just the new community, and I think that's paying off.

Interviewer 2: What does that look like practically — connecting with the older local community?

Farmer: A few things. We sell at the county farmers market in Kilmarnock, which is very much a local institution. That's where you see the longtime families, the older farmers, the people whose families have been in Lancaster County for generations. Showing up there consistently — and being genuine about it, not performing — matters. We've helped some neighboring conventional farmers with cover cropping — just offered to supply seed and help with the seeding if they wanted to try it on some of their acreage. A couple of them have taken us up on it. One of them, a man whose family has been growing corn and soybeans here for sixty years, came out last spring and walked our orchard with me for two hours and asked the most thoughtful questions. He's not going to overhaul his operation. But he's curious, and that's something.

And we've hosted a harvest festival the last two years that's open to everyone — not just the yoga crowd, not just the farm-to-table crowd. We have music, we have food, we have activities for kids. People bring their whole families. And that event has been really important for building a sense that this place belongs to the community, not just to us.

Interviewer 1: Let's talk about the grant side of things because you mentioned being pretty active in that space.

Farmer: Yes. This is something I feel strongly about and spend a lot of time on. My view is that grants are an underutilized resource for small farms that are doing genuinely innovative work, and most farmers don't pursue them either because they don't know they exist or because the application process is intimidating. And I understand both of those barriers. But I think if you approach grant writing as a skill you can develop — and I do think it's a learnable skill — it opens up real opportunities.

Interviewer 2: What's your success rate been?

Farmer: [Pause] Let me think. We've submitted I think fourteen applications over four years. We've been funded seven times. So fifty percent, which I'm told is quite good for this kind of work. The funded grants have ranged from a small farmer-rancher grant through SARE — Sustainable Agriculture Research and Education — that was about $14,000, up to a USDA NRCS program payment for our conservation practices that was more significant. We've also gotten support through the National Young Farmers Coalition, through a regional foundation that funds agricultural innovation in the Chesapeake watershed region, and a smaller state-level grant for the orchard restoration work specifically.

Interviewer 1: What makes your applications work, do you think?

Farmer: A few things. First — I try to read every grant program's stated goals very carefully and understand what they're actually trying to accomplish. Grants are not charity. They're investments by the funder in outcomes they care about. So your job as an applicant is to make the clearest possible case that your project advances those outcomes. If a program is focused on soil health, I lead with soil health data. If it's focused on farmer economic viability, I lead with the financial case. You have to write for the reviewer who is sitting there asking: does this project do what we're trying to fund?

Second — specificity. Vague proposals don't get funded. I had a mentor at Vermont who said: if your project could be anywhere, it won't be funded anywhere. The proposal has to be rooted in this specific land, this specific community, this specific set of circumstances. Here is our soil test baseline. Here is the measurable outcome we expect in three years. Here is how we will document it. Reviewers can tell the difference between a genuine project and a generic template with names filled in.

And third — I always, when possible, call someone at the granting organization before I submit. Introduce myself, explain the project briefly, ask if there's anything about the program priorities I should make sure I'm addressing. It signals that you're serious. And occasionally someone will tell you something that changes how you frame the application.

Interviewer 2: Do you help other farmers with this?

Farmer: Informally, yes. I've helped three or four neighbors put together applications over the last couple of years. And I'm starting to think there's something more formal here — maybe a workshop, maybe a resource guide specific to the region. Because the information is so scattered and the language of these programs is often impenetrable if you haven't spent time in academic or government contexts. I was lucky that I came through a graduate program that trained me to write for this kind of audience. Most farmers haven't had that. And it's a real equity issue — the farmers who most need financial support are often the least equipped to navigate these systems. That bothers me.

Interviewer 1: The farm isn't profitable yet — you mentioned that. How do you think about the path to profitability?

Farmer: Yes, and I want to be honest about this because I think there's a tendency in this space to project more success than actually exists, which does a disservice to people considering this path. We are not profitable. We are covering our operating costs and generating some income, but we are not covering our full cost of living from the farm. Renata works part time as a landscape architect, remotely, which covers a significant portion of our household expenses. And we live modestly. Very modestly. This is a choice we've made consciously, but it's a choice, and it requires that we're both fully committed to it, which we are.

The path to profitability — I think about it in three phases. We are in what I consider the establishment phase, which is the phase where you're investing in infrastructure that doesn't pay back immediately. The trees, the soil biology, the water management features — these are investments that compound over time. A five-year-old apple tree produces a fraction of what a fifteen-year-old apple tree produces. So every year we stay the course, the productive capacity of this land increases. That's the foundation.

The second phase is revenue diversification. We have the farm stand, the eggs, the produce, the orchard fruit. We need to add processed products — we're working on getting our kitchen certified so we can produce jams, dried fruit, cider vinegar, maybe some fermented products. Value-added is where the margins are in small-scale agriculture. You cannot compete on raw commodity price. You have to do something with the product.

Interviewer 2: And the third phase?

Farmer: The third phase is the educational and experiential economy, which I think is the most interesting and potentially the most significant for us long term. The yoga studio is a piece of this. But I'm thinking bigger than that. Farm immersion programs, workshops, school visits, an apprenticeship program, potentially hosting a small number of volunteer workers through WWOOF or similar networks. There's a real and growing appetite for connection to land, for learning how food is produced, for spending time in a working agricultural environment. We're positioned well for that. Beautiful land, an articulate story, diverse operations. I think we can build something meaningful in that space. And the revenue model is better — time and knowledge have better margins than raw produce.

Interviewer 1: That's something we've heard from multiple farmers actually — the experiential piece as economic strategy. Do you see risks with it?

Farmer: Yes. The main risk is that you stop being a farm and start being a tourist attraction. I've seen it happen. A farm starts doing agritourism, the income is better than the farming income, and gradually the farming becomes set decoration for the experience business. The animals are there to be photographed, the vegetables are there to be picked by visitors, and the actual serious agricultural work gets squeezed out because it's not scenic or accessible enough to build an experience around. And I have no interest in that. The farming has to stay primary. The educational and experiential work should illuminate the farming, not replace it.

Interviewer 2: How do you hold that line?

Farmer: Intentionality about what we invite people into. We're not building a petting zoo. We're building learning experiences around real working practices. When we do a composting workshop, people are doing real composting work, not watching a demonstration. When we host a school group, they're in the orchard learning to identify pest damage and think about what to do about it. The experience has to be rooted in something genuine or it becomes meaningless and eventually people can tell the difference. I've seen farms that have the aesthetics of regenerative agriculture without the substance, and the community eventually notices.

Interviewer 1: Tell us more about your relationships with the other farmers in the area — you mentioned some good connections.

Farmer: The Northern Neck has a surprisingly active small farm community. There's a loose network — not formal, no organization, just people who know each other — of farms doing interesting things. We share equipment sometimes. We've done informal labor exchanges during harvest. There's a woman named Claire about six miles from us who does market gardening and we share a cold storage unit that we built jointly, which cut the cost in half for both of us and has been a really good arrangement. And there's a farm family the Hendersons who've been in this county for four generations and know everything about this land — the soils, the weather patterns, where the frost pockets are, which fields drain slow in spring — and they've been incredibly generous with that knowledge. I feel a real obligation to that relationship. You know, I come here as an outsider, literally from another country, and there are people who've been in this soil for a hundred years. Listening to them is not just courtesy. It's practically essential.

Interviewer 2: And there's no tension there — around being an outsider, or being Israeli specifically?

Farmer: [Pause] I want to answer this carefully. The Northern Neck is a complicated place socially. It's very Southern in its culture in a way that coastal Northern Virginia is not. There are Confederate monuments in the county. There's a history here that you feel. And I am — I'm a Brown-skinned man from the Middle East with an accent, married to a local white woman whose family has roots here. That is not uncomplicated. I would be dishonest if I said I've never felt it. There have been moments at the farmers market, early on, where I could feel — not hostility exactly, but scrutiny. And I dealt with it the way I've dealt with most social challenges in my life, which is just — be competent, be generous, be genuinely interested in people, and let time do the rest. And time has largely done that. There are people in this county who I think were not sure about me initially and who are now, I believe, genuine friends. But I don't want to erase that complexity by saying it was never there.

Interviewer 1: What about the Israeli background in terms of agricultural knowledge — do you draw on Israeli agricultural innovation at all?

Farmer: Yes and no. Israeli agricultural technology is remarkable — precision irrigation, drip systems, desalination, advanced greenhouse systems. And some of that technical knowledge is in my background. But the Israeli agricultural model is predominantly intensive and technological. It's about producing maximum output from minimum input in a challenging environment, and it does that extraordinarily well. Regenerative agriculture is asking different questions — not how do we maximize output, but how do we build a system that sustains and even improves itself over time. Those are compatible in some ways and in tension in others. The water efficiency thinking transfers directly. The high-input, high-yield mindset has to be unlearned. And I think my graduate training at Vermont did a lot of that work for me. I came out of that program thinking very differently about what optimization means in an agricultural context.

Interviewer 2: How did your family in Israel respond to this life you've built?

Farmer: My mother came to visit two years ago — first time she'd ever been to rural America — and I watched her walk through the orchard and I couldn't tell what she was thinking. She's a very composed person. And then at the end of the day she sat down and she said, in Hebrew: this is a real thing. Which from her is about the highest praise you can get. My father thinks I'm slightly crazy but he respects that I have a clear vision and I'm executing it. He's an engineer, so he wants to know that the system works. I keep sending him soil test results and water retention data and he seems to find that reassuring. [Laughs] And my grandmother, who is in her eighties and still grows things in her courtyard in Ra'anana, she just wants photos. She wants to see the trees. Every time I send her a photo of the orchard she sends back a voice message that's like three minutes long about when the persimmons will be ripe and whether the soil looks right and could she taste the quince when it comes in. She is my biggest fan.

Interviewer 1: You have two kids you mentioned — how do they experience the farm?

Farmer: It's their whole world in the best sense. They've grown up with their hands in the soil. My daughter can identify probably thirty species of plants on this property, knows which ones are edible, knows which ones the insects like. She's the one who manages our pollinator observation notebook — we've been tracking what species visit which plants and when, and she takes this completely seriously. My son has his chicken responsibilities and he's also become very interested in the trees — he asks questions about grafting that I have to look up to answer, which is both humbling and wonderful. They don't experience the farm as work, or not primarily as work. They experience it as the place they live, which I think is the healthiest relationship you can have with land. I hope that stays with them.

Interviewer 2: What's the hardest part of this life that people don't see from the outside?

Farmer: The financial anxiety is real and I don't think there's any use pretending otherwise. When you're not profitable and you have two children and you're in the establishment phase of a long-term project, there are months where the math is uncomfortable. And I have enough self-awareness to know that my training, my English, my education, my wife's income, our family's land — these are enormous privileges that let me sit in financial discomfort with more security than many people have. Someone without those resources who tried this path would be in a much harder position. I carry that awareness.

And then the physical demands are significant. I'm thirty-nine years old and I feel it in ways I didn't feel at thirty-two. The heavy mulching work, the pruning — pruning season my hands and shoulders are just destroyed. We've been getting smarter about tools and ergonomics, but it's fundamentally physical work and the body keeps score.

Interviewer 1: Are you thinking about bringing in employees or help beyond the family?

Farmer: Yes. We have one part-time worker now — a young man from the county who helps with the heavy labor a few days a week. And I want to formalize something more like an apprenticeship program, ideally someone who wants to learn and is willing to work for below market wages in exchange for housing — we have a small cottage on the property — and genuine education. And there are programs that support this kind of arrangement. WWOOF is one model. There are also J-1 visa programs for agricultural training. I've been looking into what makes the most sense legally and practically.

Interviewer 2: Where does the farm need to get to for you to feel like it's viable long term?

Farmer: I think about this a lot. I've built out projections in some detail, which probably reflects the grant writer in me. In five years I want the orchard in full production — the trees we've planted are four to seven years old right now, and in five years they should be producing at their potential. That alone should roughly double our fruit output. Combined with the value-added kitchen products and continued growth in the farm stand revenue and the studio income, I think we reach breakeven on the farm operations independent of Renata's work. And then the educational programs on top of that is what takes us to genuine profitability. That's the model.

The variable I can't fully control is the market. The customer base we have now is loyal and growing, but we're dependent on this area's demographic composition staying favorable. If the influx of values-aligned people from DC and Northern Virginia slows, that affects us. I try not to build a plan that only works under optimistic assumptions, but some assumptions about the customer base have to be in there.

Interviewer 1: The farm stand as a community hub — do you see that evolving?

Farmer: Very much. I'd like to add a small café component eventually — nothing elaborate, but the ability to serve coffee and simple food using our products and our neighbors' products. There are permitting and infrastructure requirements for that which make it a longer-term project. But the stand is already functioning as a gathering place on weekend mornings in a way that feels important. People come for the eggs and the honey and end up standing in the driveway talking to each other for an hour. That's something. That's the beginning of something.

Interviewer 2: What's your philosophy on the regenerative label — how do you talk about your practices to customers?

Farmer: I'm probably more comfortable using the term than some farmers I know, partly because I've done a lot of reading and thinking about what it means and I feel confident I can defend it substantively. But I try to always use it as an opening for explanation rather than a closing. So if someone asks, are you organic, I say: we go beyond organic, here's what that means. And then I talk about the soil biology, the water management, the habitat, the closed loops we're trying to create. And I find that people — especially the customers we're attracting — are hungry for that detail. They want to understand, not just feel good about the label. And that kind of customer becomes a real ally. They tell other people. They come back. They bring their kids. They become part of what you're building.

Interviewer 1: What do you see for agriculture in this region over the next generation?

Farmer: The Northern Neck and the broader Chesapeake watershed region have enormous agricultural potential that I think is underutilized. The soils are good, the water access is generally good, the climate is favorable for a wide range of production. And there's a regulatory framework around the Chesapeake Bay that, whatever you think of regulations, has actually pushed farms in this region toward practices that align with regenerative goals — cover cropping, buffer zones, reduced erosion. So there's a foundation there.

What I want to see is more of what we're trying to do and what a handful of other operations in this region are doing — farms that are economically viable and ecologically restorative at the same time. And I think it's possible. But it requires support infrastructure that doesn't fully exist yet. It requires processing facilities — there's almost no local meat processing in this region, which is a real constraint for livestock producers. It requires aggregation and distribution — some mechanism for small farms to combine product and reach the DC and Northern Virginia markets that are very interested in local food. Some of that is being built but slowly. I try to be a node in that network wherever I can, rather than just a standalone operation.

Interviewer 2: If you could change one thing about the support system for farms like yours, what would it be?

Farmer: The timeline mismatch between how agriculture actually works and how funding programs are structured. I do a three-year SARE grant and they want to see outcomes in three years. But I'm working with trees that take ten years to reach their potential. I'm building soil biology that responds over decades. The meaningful results of what I'm doing won't be fully visible for twenty years. And almost no funding program is built around that reality. It's not a criticism of the programs individually — I understand why funders want defined timelines and measurable outcomes in a reasonable window. But it creates a structural mismatch where the work that most needs support is also the work that's hardest to fund because the evidence of success is slow to accumulate.

If I could change one thing it would be long-term stewardship grants — multi-decade commitments to farms demonstrating genuine ecological improvement — with flexibility in the metrics and real patience about when outcomes materialize. Think of it like an endowment for land stewardship rather than a project grant. That model would transform what's possible.

Interviewer 1: That's a really interesting idea. Is anyone doing something like that?

Farmer: There are a few conservation easement programs that have some of that flavor. And some land trust arrangements. But not at the scale or with the flexibility that I'm describing. It's a policy and philanthropy gap that I think is real and solvable. Maybe someday I'll be in a position to advocate for it more formally. For now I'm trying to do the work, document it carefully, and build the evidence base that might eventually make that case.

Interviewer 2: Last question — what do you tell people who want to do something like this and are wondering if they can?

Farmer: I tell them: go slow and learn the land before you make big decisions. The most expensive mistakes I've seen people make in regenerative farming are the ones they made in the first year before they understood the specific piece of ground they were working with. Every property has its own logic — where the water moves, what the soil is doing, what wild species are already there. Spend a full year observing before you start imposing. Walk every inch of the property in every season. Take notes. Take photos. And read the history — what was here before, what grew here, what animals used it. The land will tell you what it wants to do if you listen before you push.

And then — don't underestimate the community piece. The neighbors, the local knowledge, the relationships with other farmers. This work is hard enough when you're embedded in a community that knows you and supports you. Trying to do it as an island is much harder. The social infrastructure is as important as the physical infrastructure. Maybe more.
