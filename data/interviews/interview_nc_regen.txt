Interviewer 1: What breed are you running?

Farmer: Cornish Cross. And I know — I know. Some people in the regenerative space judge that. There are real partisans for heritage breeds. But here's my reasoning. Farmer: Yeah, and taste is part of it. But the economics is the real reason. On pasture, with constant fresh ground and access to forage and bugs and sun, they live a great chicken life. Short, but great. I sleep okay with it.

Interviewer 1: What about the cattle — do they follow the chickens?

And the manure cycle continues — cattle drop their patties, then the chickens follow later in the rotation and break the patties apart, spreading the fertility and reducing parasite loads. It's a closed loop. The two species are working together.

Interviewer 2: How do you decide paddock size?

Interviewer 1: Are they all the same breed?

Farmer: Mostly. We're running primarily South Poll. They're a relatively new breed developed for the South — heat tolerant, easy fleshing on grass, gentle disposition. Interviewer 2: Why South Poll over more common breeds?

Farmer: For us, it came down to grass-finishing performance in heat. We're in the South. Summer is brutal. Most beef breeds were developed in northern climates and they struggle here. South Polls were bred specifically for southern grass finishing. They put on weight on poor forage, they handle the heat, they have low maintenance needs. The trade-off is they're smaller — a finished South Poll might be 1,000 pounds versus 1,300 for a finished Angus. So you get less beef per animal. Farmer: Yeah, this is something I'm pretty worked up about, honestly. So when we started, there was actually a really good ecosystem of grants and programs for regenerative practices. Farmer: It's all about layered uses. That's actually how I think about everything now. The cattle aren't just for beef — they're for soil building too. Interviewer 1: Do you connect with the broader MAHA movement?

Farmer: I do. With caveats. I think there's a lot of real concern there about food quality, about chronic disease, about chemicals in our food, that I share. Farmer: A few reasons. Regenerative ag has a longer track record and includes a lot of people who don't identify politically with MAHA at all. So there's tribal stuff that prevents real conversation. I think the future for both movements depends on getting past that. The actual content overlaps a lot. Soil health, food quality, less industrial inputs, more local food. Those should be common ground.

Interviewer 1: Do you talk about these things on your content?

Farmer: Carefully. I'll talk about practices — like, here's why we use no synthetic inputs — without getting into the political framing. Interviewer 2: Here's the cabin?

Farmer: Yeah, this is it. Cute, right? We did this whole renovation ourselves. It was a mess when we started — old hunting cabin, sagging porch, no real bathroom. Now it's — it's a nice place. People love staying here.

Farmer: That's a good question. I think about it a lot. The simple answer is — sustainability. Interviewer 2: Let's go back to the regenerative practices for a minute. Can you walk us through your full rotation cycle?

Farmer: Yeah. So roughly speaking, here's the annual cycle. Spring — the cattle come off winter feeding and we start grass-based grazing in March or April depending on growth. Daily moves through the paddocks. We're trying to time the first grazing of each paddock when the grass is at the right vegetative stage — not too short, not too tall. That stage is when the grass is most nutritious for the animals and when grazing actually stimulates further growth. Grazing too early stunts the grass.
