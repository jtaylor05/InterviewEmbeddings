Interviewer 1: Mr. — is it okay if we just call you by your first name, or would you prefer —

Farmer: First name is fine. First name's fine. And — y'all said you're not filming, right? You said just audio.

Interviewer 1: Just audio. The camera's not on. Just the recorder.

Farmer: Okay. Okay. Because I don't — I don't do well in front of cameras. I just — I'd rather be a voice. Y'all can write down what I say but I don't need to be on anything.

Interviewer 2: Understood. The recorder is just for transcription, and we can stop it at any time if you want.

Farmer: Alright. Alright then. Where do you want me to start?

Interviewer 1: Maybe just — tell us where we are and what y'all do here.

Farmer: Alright. Well, we're in Marlboro County, South Carolina. About — oh, I'd say maybe an hour from the coast if you go the right roads. This is — this land here, we're on about 35 acres. I've got the muscadine vineyard out behind the house there, you can see the rows from here if you look past that shed. And then north of that I've got the orchard. Mixed fruit trees. Some pears, some figs, some persimmons, some apples — though apples don't love it here as much as I'd like. Plums. Peaches when I can keep them alive. And then I've got a few experimental things I'm working on. Mulberries, pawpaws, some Asian persimmon varieties I'm trying out. Bunch of stuff.

Interviewer 2: That's a lot of variety.

Farmer: That's because I can't help myself. [Laughs] Every time I go somewhere, I come back with another tree. My wife — she passed about six years ago now — she used to say I had a problem. And she was right. I'd be driving down the road and see a tree growing wild on the shoulder, and I'd stop, and I'd take a cutting, and I'd bring it home and try to root it. I've got trees on this property that came from all over. There's a fig over there came from a abandoned house up in Chesterfield County. There's a pear back behind the shop that came from a old churchyard in North Carolina that nobody was tending anymore. So this orchard is kind of a — it's a collection. It's a museum, almost. Of trees that other folks forgot about.

Interviewer 1: That's beautiful. How long have you been doing this?

Farmer: I planted the first muscadine vines in — let me think. 1986? '87? Right around in there. So we're coming up on 40 years now. The orchard came later. I started adding fruit trees in the early '90s and just kept going. So a long time. A long time.

Interviewer 2: And you grew up around here?

Farmer: Born and raised right here. My grandfather had land just down the road. My father had land just down the road. My brothers and sisters — what's left of them — they all live within a quarter mile of where we're sitting right now. There's a whole little neighborhood of us. We were here when there wasn't anything else here. Just woods and tobacco fields back then. Tobacco was the thing when I was coming up. Everybody grew it. We grew it. My daddy grew it. I worked tobacco when I was a boy — bent over those rows from dawn til dark, taking the worms off the leaves with my fingers. I knew real young I didn't want to do tobacco for the rest of my life. So when I came back from the service I knew I was going to do something different on this land. Took me a while to figure out what.

Interviewer 1: You served in Korea, is that right?

Farmer: I did. '52 to '54. I was — let me see — I was 18 when I went in. 20 when I came home. Came home and didn't talk about it for a long time. Still don't talk about it much. But you asked, so — yeah. I was there. I saw things. I did things. And I came back here and I had to figure out how to be a person again. Took years. Honestly, took decades, some of it.

Interviewer 2: We don't have to go there if you don't want to.

Farmer: No, it's — it's alright. I just don't go into the details. I don't think they help anyone. What I'll say is — coming back from a war, the world don't fit you the same anymore. You see colors different. You hear sounds different. A loud noise can drop me to the floor still, even now. 70-plus years later. So you carry it. And you have to find a way to carry it. And for me, the carrying — the way I figured out how to carry it — was working in the dirt. Working with growing things. Something about that work just — it slows the mind down. It puts you in the moment. You can't be in 1953 if you're pruning a muscadine vine, because the vine needs your full attention right then. So I worked. I worked hard for a lot of years.

Interviewer 1: And the farming has been part of that healing for you.

Farmer: It's been everything for it. I don't know who I'd be without it. I really don't. There was a long stretch in my 30s where things were bad. Bad with drinking, bad with sleep, bad with — bad with everything. And the land brought me back. The land and my wife. My wife brought me back. And then the land kept me back. So that's why — when people ask me when I'm going to retire — and they ask me that, a lot of them ask me, what are you 92 years old, when are you going to stop? And I tell them — I'm going to stop when they put me in the ground. Not before. Because if I stop, I don't know what happens. I really don't.

Interviewer 2: How are you still doing this physically at your age? That's incredible.

Farmer: [Laughs] Slowly. Real slow. I can't do what I did at 50. I can't do what I did at 70, even. I've got a grandson helping me — I'll tell you about him in a minute — and I hire some help during the picking season and when I need pruning done. But the truth is I get up every morning and I go out there and I do as much as my body lets me do. And then I sit down, and I drink some water, and I get up and do some more. And by the end of the day I'm tired. But I'm not — I'm not the kind of tired that makes you sad. It's the good kind. The kind where you know you did something. Most people my age don't have that no more. They're sitting in front of the TV being tired for no reason. That's the worst kind of tired.

Interviewer 1: Can we see the vineyard?

Farmer: Yeah, yeah, let's walk. Just — take my arm if I need it on the steps, would you. Sometimes the steps off the porch are tricky.

[The group makes its way outside.]

Farmer: Alright, so — this whole stretch right here, all the way back to where you see that tree line, that's the muscadines. I've got about three acres in vines. Used to be five but I pulled out two acres about ten years ago because I couldn't keep up with all of it. Three is what I can manage. Just barely. With help.

Interviewer 2: What varieties are you growing?

Farmer: Mostly Carlos and Magnolia for the white wine. Noble and Ison for the red and the dark wines. And I've got some specialty ones — I've got a row of Supreme over there that's just for fresh eating, those are the big ones, big as a quarter when they get going. And then I've got some old varieties I keep just because nobody else does. I've got a row of Scuppernong — the original, the true Scuppernong — that came from a vine my grandfather had on his property. So that vine is — those vines are descendants of vines that have been in my family for over a hundred years. And I keep them going because if I don't, nobody will.

Interviewer 1: That's — that's a lot of history in those vines.

Farmer: It's all history. Everything growing here is history of some kind. The Scuppernong is family history. The vines I planted in '86 are my history. The trees I brought back from the side of the road are somebody's history that I'm just borrowing. It's all stories. Every plant out here has a story.

Interviewer 2: How do muscadines do as a crop? Like, are they easy compared to other grapes?

Farmer: Easier than European wine grapes, for sure. Muscadines are native. They evolved here. So they handle the heat, they handle the humidity, they handle the disease pressure better than vinifera would. You can't grow Cabernet Sauvignon in South Carolina, basically. The fungus will eat it. But you can grow muscadines because muscadines were here before we were. They know what they're doing. The skin is thick — that's part of why they're disease-resistant. And they ripen one cluster at a time, not the whole bunch at once like European grapes, so you've got a longer window to harvest. They drop. You go out and pick what's ripe and you come back and pick more later. Some people don't like that — they want everything to ripen at once for efficiency. I like it. I like having a long harvest. It feels more — it feels like nature working with you instead of against you.

Interviewer 1: And you make wine from these?

Farmer: I do. I've got a small permit and a small operation. I'm not — I'm not Robert Mondavi. [Laughs] I make maybe — oh, 600 bottles a year. Maybe 800 in a good year. All under my own label, which is just — it's just the name of this place and a little drawing my granddaughter did of the vineyard when she was about ten years old. That's my brand. I sell it out of the shop here. People come pick muscadines fresh in season and then they buy a bottle on the way out. That's the model.

Interviewer 2: Do you make different styles?

Farmer: I make a dry white, a semi-sweet white, a sweet red, and I make a dessert wine that I'm proud of. It takes me about three years to make the dessert wine — I age it in glass demijohns out in the shop. People who know wine, they understand what that is. People who don't, they just say it's good and they buy two bottles. Either way is fine with me.

Interviewer 1: Are you a wine drinker yourself?

Farmer: I don't drink anymore. I quit drinking — must be 35 years now. After the bad stretch I told you about. I make wine but I don't drink it. I sell it. Other folks enjoy it. And there's something — there's something nice about that, actually. Making something I don't partake of. It's just for other people. It's a gift.

Interviewer 2: That's a really beautiful way to think about it.

Farmer: Well, you find your peace where you find it. Let's keep walking. I want to show you the orchard.

[They walk further onto the property.]

Farmer: See that big tree right there — that one with the wide canopy? That's a mulberry. That tree right there is my favorite thing on this whole property and I'll tell you why. It's not for sale. The fruit, I mean. I don't sell mulberries because the season's short and the berries are too soft and they crush in baskets. So the fruit is for the birds. And the pollinators. And the deer and the foxes and whatever else comes through here. That tree drops fruit for about three weeks every spring and it is a — it is a banquet. You stand out here at dusk in May and the whole thing is alive. Bees, wasps — the good kind — butterflies, moths, every kind of bird you've ever heard of. I sit out here with a cup of coffee in the morning sometimes and just watch. That tree feeds more living things than anything else on this property.

Interviewer 1: Do you think a lot about pollinators?

Farmer: I think about them constantly. Because without them, I don't have a crop. The muscadines are self-pollinating mostly but the orchard isn't. The peaches, the plums, the persimmons — they all need pollinators. And it's been concerning. I've been here on this land for 92 years and I've watched the bug populations change in my lifetime. I remember being a boy and you'd drive down a country road in summer and your windshield would be covered in bugs. Now you can drive 50 miles and not have to clean the windshield. So they're going somewhere. And if they go far enough — we're in trouble. Everybody's in trouble.

Interviewer 2: Do you do anything specifically to support them?

Farmer: I plant for them. I leave wild spots. I don't mow everywhere — there are corners of this property I leave alone, let the wildflowers come up, let the brambles grow. The brambles are great pollinator habitat. Most people clean them out because they look messy. I leave them. I plant native shrubs along the edges — buttonbush, elderberry, sumac. And I do not spray anything. I haven't sprayed an insecticide on this property in — forever. Decades. And the orchard suffers some for it. I lose fruit to bugs. But I won't poison the pollinators to save a few peaches. That math doesn't add up to me.

Interviewer 1: Were you ever certified organic?

Farmer: I was. For about — oh, maybe eight years. Got out of it around 2010 I think. And I'll tell you the honest truth — the certification was a pain. The paperwork, the inspections, the rules — some of it made sense and some of it didn't. And the thing was, I was doing it the right way already. I wasn't suddenly going to start spraying just because the certificate ran out. So I let it lapse. And nothing changed about how I farm. Just the label changed. And my customers know me. They come here. They see how it's done. They don't need a sticker to trust me.

Interviewer 2: Do you still describe yourself as organic to customers?

Farmer: I tell them I farm chemical-free. I don't use the word organic because that has a legal meaning and I don't have the certificate anymore. But I tell them everything I do. I don't spray. I don't fertilize with anything synthetic. I use compost from my own chickens and from the leaves I rake up in fall. I'm doing what organic was meant to mean before the lawyers got hold of it.

Interviewer 1: How do you feel about the organic label now in general?

Farmer: Mixed. Mixed feelings. I think it's been good for getting some practices into the mainstream that wouldn't have got there otherwise. But I also think it's become a big business and a lot of what's certified organic these days is industrial farming with different inputs. It's not the small farmer model that the people who started the movement had in mind. So I have respect for what it tried to do and skepticism about what it became. Like a lot of things.

Interviewer 2: That tracks with a lot of what we've heard from other farmers.

Farmer: I'd imagine so. We've all been thinking about it.

[They continue walking toward the orchard.]

Farmer: Now this row right here is all stone fruit. Peaches, plums, a couple nectarines. Stone fruit is the hardest thing I grow. Just — peach trees especially. They get every disease, they break under their own weight when the fruit gets heavy, they're short-lived. A peach tree in this climate, you're lucky if it gives you 12 productive years. So I'm constantly replanting. Constantly. There's a little nursery section back behind the shop where I'm always rooting cuttings or starting from seed. Trees come in, trees go out. It's a cycle.

Interviewer 1: Do you graft your own?

Farmer: I do. I learned grafting from a old man named Mr. Wilkins who lived just south of here, must be — oh, 40 years ago he taught me. He's long dead now. But he showed me chip budding and cleft grafting and whip and tongue. And I've been grafting ever since. The good thing about grafting is — say I find a wild plum on the side of the road that's got real good fruit, but it's not a variety anybody knows. I can take cuttings, graft them onto rootstock I've got going, and now I've got that wild plum growing in my orchard. That's how a lot of these trees got here. They're grafted onto rootstock from somewhere else with scion wood from wild trees or old homesteads. So it's a kind of preservation. Trees that would have been lost.

Interviewer 2: That's such a cool way to do it.

Farmer: It's how it used to be done before the seed catalogs took over. Everybody had their own varieties. Every community had their special apple or special pear that grew on Mrs. So-and-so's place. And then the catalogs came in and standardized everything and a lot of those varieties got lost. I'm just trying to hold onto a few.

Interviewer 1: Do you sell scion wood to other people?

Farmer: I give it away. If somebody asks me for a cutting of something I've got, I'll give it to them. I don't sell it. Selling scion wood feels wrong to me. It's like selling a story. You can pass it on but you shouldn't make money off it. That's just how I feel about it.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the pick-your-own side of it.

Farmer: That's most of my business, especially in the orchard. I open up — depends on what's ripening — usually starts in May with mulberries if I'm letting people pick them, which I sometimes do and sometimes don't. Then through summer it's plums and figs and peaches. Late summer and fall is muscadine season — that's the big one. Persimmons in November. And the customers come from all over. I've got people who drive from Charlotte. From Columbia. From Wilmington sometimes, if you can believe it. People who plan their fall around coming out here and filling up some bags.

Interviewer 1: Why do they drive so far?

Farmer: Because what they get at the grocery store doesn't taste like anything. That's it. That's the whole story. A muscadine off a vine, ripe, picked in the moment — there's nothing else like it. It pops in your mouth, the inside is sweet like honey, the skin has that little bite to it. You can't ship that. You can't store that. You have to come get it. And once people have had it, they keep coming back. Some of my customers have been coming for 20, 25 years. I watched their kids grow up. They bring their grandkids now.

Interviewer 2: That's a really lovely community.

Farmer: It is. It's one of the best things about this work. Pick-your-own brings you into people's lives in a way that selling at a market doesn't. They come out here, they spend an afternoon, the kids are running around eating off the vines, the parents are talking to each other. It's an event. And I get to be part of that. They're not just buying fruit. They're making a memory. So I take it seriously.

Interviewer 1: Do you have specific things people come for?

Farmer: The muscadines are the biggest draw, no question. Especially Supreme and the bigger varieties. People love picking them because they're easy — they fall right into your hand when they're ripe. Figs are a big draw too. I have some really old fig varieties that are hard to find anywhere else. And then I've got a few real specialty things — like I've got a pawpaw patch back there that I'm trying to develop. Pawpaw is the largest native fruit in North America, did you know that?

Interviewer 2: I didn't.

Farmer: It's bigger than a pear when it's ripe. It tastes like — folks say like a cross between a mango and a banana. Custard texture. And it grows wild from here all the way up to Michigan. Most people in this country have never tasted one because they don't ship and they don't last but a few days off the tree. So I've got a patch I'm growing as a curiosity. Sometimes when people come pick muscadines I'll cut one open and let them try. And their face — every time, their face. They didn't know fruit could taste like that. It changes how they think about what they're eating.

Interviewer 1: You mentioned you've got a grandson helping you?

Farmer: I do. His name's DeAndre. He's 21. He's at Clemson — he's studying horticulture, actually. Which I had nothing to do with, I want to be clear. He chose that on his own. He grew up around here, he's been coming out to this farm his whole life, and I think — I'd like to think — that being out here has shaped him. He's the only one of the grandkids who's shown a real interest in this. The others are good kids, they got their own paths. But DeAndre, he comes out on weekends when he's not at school, and he works with me, and we talk. He's been a big help especially these last few years as I've slowed down. He does a lot of the heavy work now. The bending, the lifting, the climbing. I do the thinking.

Interviewer 2: Are you grooming him to take over?

Farmer: I am hoping. I am hoping. I haven't pressed him on it because I don't want him to feel obligated. He's young. He's got his whole life. He might decide he wants to work for a big nursery operation or get into research or do something else entirely. And that would be okay. But I've been showing him everything. I've been telling him the stories. The Scuppernong came from my grandfather's land — he knows that. The fig from the abandoned house — he knows that. So whatever he ends up doing, he carries the stories. And if one day he decides he wants to come back and take this on — it's ready for him. The land is here. The trees are here. The vines are here. I'd hand it over in a minute.

Interviewer 1: That must be hard — not knowing for sure.

Farmer: It is and it isn't. It's hard because I don't want to see this all go after I'm gone. I really don't. The thought of someone buying this place and bulldozing the vineyard — I can't stand it. But also — I've been on this land 92 years. I've done my work here. Whatever happens after, happens. I've made my peace with it as best I can. Hoping for DeAndre. Not depending on it.

Interviewer 2: What does he think when y'all are working out here together?

Farmer: We talk a lot. About everything. About the farm, about school, about — about life. He asks me about Korea sometimes. He's one of the only people I'll talk to about it. Because he's curious and he listens and he doesn't push. He just lets me talk when I want to. And I appreciate that. He's a thoughtful young man. I'm real proud of him.

Interviewer 1: Can we ask — what made you start farming after the war? Like, what was the path from coming home to the first vines?

Farmer: There was a long path. I didn't go right into farming. When I first came home I worked at a — at a sawmill up in Bennettsville for a few years. Then I worked construction. Then I worked at a textile mill for about 15 years until it closed down, which a lot of folks around here can tell you about. And the whole time I was doing those other jobs, I had a garden. I had a kitchen garden going at whatever house I was renting or owning at the time. Always something growing. And I think — I think the garden was keeping me sane. It was the constant. Whatever else was going on, I'd come home and dig in the dirt for a couple hours and feel a little better. So when the mill closed and I had to figure out what to do next, I'd already built up the land here that I'd inherited from my daddy, and I was — I was 50-something at that point, and I thought, this is my time. If I'm going to do this, I have to do it now. So I planted the first vines. And it grew from there.

Interviewer 2: Did you ever think it would become what it is?

Farmer: No. Lord, no. I thought I'd grow some grapes for myself and maybe sell some at a roadside stand. I didn't have any plan. I just planted vines because I wanted to. Everything else happened slow, by accident. Somebody asked if they could buy grapes — sure. Somebody asked if I could make wine from them — well, let me try. Somebody asked if they could come pick — okay. Each thing came in its own time and I said yes when it felt right. There was never a business plan. I'd be a poor businessman if you sat me down and asked me to write one. I just did what was in front of me and it grew.

Interviewer 1: That's how a lot of the best things happen, I think.

Farmer: I think so too. I don't trust people with big plans, honestly. I've seen too many big plans crash. The folks who do the thing slow and small and just keep going — those are the folks who build something that lasts.

Interviewer 2: Let's go back to some of the practical stuff for a second. The fruit trees — how do you decide what to add?

Farmer: Mostly by curiosity. I'll read about something or see something at another farm and I'll think — I wonder if that would work here. And then I'll try a tree or two. If it does well, I add more. If it doesn't, I learn from it. I've got — I'll tell you something funny. I had a fascination with paw paws for years before I finally got a patch going. And I tried twice before this current patch and failed both times. Paw paws are picky about their start. They need shade when they're young and they don't transplant well. So my first two tries I bought trees that had been potted up and they just languished and died. The patch that's going now — I grew them from seed, in place, where they were going to live. And that worked. Three years to get there. So that's how I learn. By failing. By trying again.

Interviewer 1: How do you keep up with all the different species and their different needs?

Farmer: I keep a notebook. I've got — I've got notebooks going back probably 35 years now. Stacks of them. With notes on every tree. When it was planted, where it came from, what variety, when it bloomed, when it fruited, what the yield was, what pests hit it, what I did about it. And I go back to those notebooks. I'll have a question — say I'm wondering when a particular variety usually bloomed — and I'll dig through and find an entry from 2003 with the answer. So that's my system. Pen and paper. I tried doing it on a computer one year and I gave up by April.

Interviewer 2: Has the climate changed in the years you've been doing this?

Farmer: It has. I can tell you it has. I look at my notebooks and I can see it. The bloom dates have shifted earlier. The first frost is later. The summers are hotter and more humid than they used to be, which is saying something because South Carolina summers have always been hot and humid. We get rain events now that we didn't used to get — these big dumps of rain in short periods that flood things out. Used to be we got steadier rain, more often. Now it's drier in between and then we get these — these episodes. And it affects the fruit. Stone fruit is hard hit by it. The peaches and plums don't like the swing from wet to dry. They like steady. So yeah, climate has definitely changed. Anybody who's been on the land 50 years can tell you that.

Interviewer 1: How are you adapting to it?

Farmer: Slowly. I've added some heat-tolerant varieties that I wouldn't have bothered with 30 years ago. Some Asian persimmons that handle heat real well. I'm phasing out some of the apples because they need more chill hours than we reliably get anymore. I've added some Mediterranean things — pomegranates have done well for me, which they wouldn't have here in 1985. So I'm shifting. I have to. The land tells you what wants to grow on it and you listen or you fail.

Interviewer 2: Have you talked to other farmers about climate?

Farmer: Some. Not as much as I should, maybe. I'm not — I'm not a big joiner. I don't go to many of the meetings. I should but I don't. I'm getting too old to want to drive out to things in the evening. So I read. I read a lot. I've got a stack of newsletters from the extension and from some of the heritage fruit groups and I read those. And I talk to my grandson. He's bringing me information from school. He'll tell me about research being done at Clemson on something and we'll talk it through. So my information channels are quieter than they used to be but they're there.

Interviewer 1: What's your relationship with the extension office like?

Farmer: It's been good. Mostly. I've got mixed feelings about some extension stuff over the years — there's been times when extension agents came in here trying to tell me how to do things that I'd been doing fine for 40 years, and I had to politely send them on their way. But the current agent is a younger woman who knows what she doesn't know, which is the best quality you can have in an extension agent. She asks questions instead of telling me things. And she's been a help. She connected me with some grant money for the deer fencing a few years back, which was important because the deer here are bad.

Interviewer 2: How bad?

Farmer: Bad. They'll eat a young fruit tree to the ground in a single night if you let them. I've lost trees. I've lost a lot of trees over the years to deer before I got the perimeter fence up. And I've got electric inside too in some areas. They're smart animals. They figure things out. I respect them but I don't want them in my orchard.

Interviewer 1: Let me ask you something different — and you can pass on this if you want — but how do you think about being a Black farmer in South Carolina? Like, does that come up for you?

Farmer: [Long pause] How honest do you want me to be.

Interviewer 1: As honest as you're comfortable being.

Farmer: [Pause] It comes up. It comes up. I've been on this land my whole life and so has my family and we've never had a serious problem here — I want to be clear about that. This community is my community. The folks around me, white and Black, know me, respect me, do business with me. So I'm not going to tell you I've had a hard time on my own land. That would be a lie. But the bigger picture — yeah. The bigger picture is something else.

Interviewer 2: What do you mean?

Farmer: I mean — Black farmers in this country — there used to be hundreds of thousands of us. Now there's a fraction of that. The land we used to own — most of it's gone. There's a whole history of how Black families lost their land in the 20th century. Discriminatory lending. The USDA's record on Black farmers is a record I don't want to get into in detail but you can look it up. Deeds being lost. Heirs property situations where one family member could force a sale and the rest of the family lost everything. I had cousins lose land in that exact way. So yes — I think about that. I think about that a lot. And I think about how lucky I am that my daddy held onto this land. He held onto it through a time when a lot of Black folks couldn't.

Interviewer 1: Was there pressure on him to sell?

Farmer: There was pressure on everybody to sell. The whole 20th century was pressure on Black farmers to sell. And he didn't. And I don't take that for granted. I'm here because of what he did. And — and you know what — could y'all just, could y'all not — can you just take what I said next out of the recording? I'm going to say something and then I want you to take it out.

Interviewer 2: Of course. We'll cut it.

Farmer: [Section removed at the farmer's request.]

Farmer: Okay. Okay. Thank you. I just — that's between me and you. Not for any project.

Interviewer 1: Absolutely.

Farmer: [Pause] Where were we.

Interviewer 2: We were talking about — about your father holding onto the land.

Farmer: Right. Yeah. So. He held on. And my brothers and sisters and I held on after him. And now my kids and grandkids — my daughter lives in that house right there across the road. My son and his family are about a half mile up. My sister is two houses down from her. So we are still here. The whole family is still here. Which is rare. In a country that's been built on Black families being displaced and scattered, we are still on our land. I'm proud of that. I don't say it loud but I'm proud of it.

Interviewer 1: It's a beautiful thing.

Farmer: It is. It's the thing I'm most proud of. More than the wine, more than any award the wine ever won, more than anything. The family is still here.

Interviewer 2: Can we talk about what your day looks like? Just to give a sense of what your life is actually like now?

Farmer: Sure. Yeah. So I get up early. Earlier than I used to even. Old men don't sleep much. I'm up usually before 6. I'll make myself some coffee. Sit on the porch for a while. Watch the light come up. That's important to me. That's a quiet time. Then I'll head out and walk the property. I do that every morning — just walk. Look at things. Look at every section of the orchard, look at the vines, see what's changed since yesterday. You'd be surprised how much changes in a day. New growth, new pests, new ripening. The walk is my inspection. Then I'll start the work — whatever needs doing. Pruning, tying vines, fixing fence, working in the nursery, whatever. I work until lunch. Then I sit down. I eat. I take a real nap, like an hour, hour and a half. That's new. I didn't used to nap. Now I have to. And then I'm up and back out for the afternoon. Slower. By 5 or 6 I'm done. And I sit. I read. I might call my daughter, or DeAndre might come by. And then bed early. Because tomorrow I'm doing it again.

Interviewer 1: That's a beautiful rhythm.

Farmer: It's a rhythm that suits me. I couldn't live in a city anymore. I haven't been to a city in years, honestly. I went to Columbia maybe — when was the last time? Maybe four years ago, for a wine event. And I couldn't wait to get home. The noise. The pace. I'm too old for it.

Interviewer 2: Do you ever travel?

Farmer: Used to. Used to travel a fair amount. We — my wife and I — we did some trips when the kids were grown. Went to California once, to wine country. That was a big trip for us. We went to New York one time, took the train up. And we went down to the Caribbean a few times in winter. But I haven't been on a plane since she passed. And I don't think I'll go again. My world is small now. It's this land. And that's okay. The land is big enough.

Interviewer 1: You miss her.

Farmer: Every day. Every single day. We were married 51 years. You don't recover from losing somebody you were married to that long. You just learn to live with the missing.

Interviewer 2: I'm so sorry.

Farmer: Don't be sorry. It's the natural order. I'm 92. I've lost more people than most people meet. My parents, my older brother, my best friend from the service, a daughter — my middle daughter passed in 2003 of cancer. So I know loss. And my wife was the hardest one. But you go on. You go on because there are still things to do and people who need you. My kids needed me to be okay. My grandkids needed me to be okay. The land needed me. So I figured out how to be okay. Mostly.

Interviewer 1: How do you handle the grief part of it?

Farmer: With the work. Same as the war. The work is where I put the things I can't carry any other way. You pour grief into a peach tree and you prune it slow and careful and you cry a little and you keep moving. The work absorbs it. The trees don't mind. They just want what they want — water, sun, attention. And you give it to them. And the act of giving it is the medicine. That's the truth I've found.

Interviewer 2: That's profound.

Farmer: It's not profound. It's just true. The simple things are usually true. The fancy explanations are the ones that fall apart.

Interviewer 1: What do you want people to understand about what you do — about this kind of farming?

Farmer: I want people to understand that this is a relationship. Between me and the land. Between me and the trees. Between me and the people who come here. It's not a business model. It's not a brand. It's a relationship that's been built over decades and that takes care of itself if you take care of it. And I think modern people have forgotten how to be in relationships like that. With anything. With places, with traditions, with each other. Everybody wants a quick transaction. And that's not — that's not what I'm doing. I'm doing something that takes a lifetime. And that's the only kind of farming I know how to do.

Interviewer 2: Would you ever want to scale it up? Like, sell to grocery stores?

Farmer: No. No. Never. The minute you put my muscadines in a grocery store, they're not my muscadines anymore. They're just produce. The experience of coming out here and picking them, of seeing the vines, of meeting me — that's what they are. Without that, they're just grapes. So no. I'm where I'm meant to be. And it's plenty.

Interviewer 1: What about advice — for a young person who wanted to do something like this?

Farmer: First — start small. Smaller than you think you should. Master a little patch before you spread out. Second — pick something you actually love. If you don't love what you're growing, you're going to hate the bad days. And there's a lot of bad days. Third — be patient. This is not work for people in a hurry. Fourth — listen to your land. Don't try to make the land do what you want. Find out what the land wants to do and help it do that. And fifth — invest in people. The relationships you build, the customers, the neighbors, the folks who help you when things break — those people are your real wealth. Not the money. The people.

Interviewer 2: That's a beautiful list.

Farmer: I've had a lot of time to think about it. [Laughs] If you're 92 and you can't put together a list, you've been wasting your time.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything we haven't asked about that you'd like to talk about?

Farmer: [Pause] I'd like to say one more thing about pollinators. Because I worry about them. I worry about the bees especially. We've lost so many. And if young farmers are going to do anything for the future, the biggest thing they can do is plant for the pollinators. Don't just plant your crop. Plant the things that feed the bees and the butterflies and the moths and the little wasps that nobody pays attention to. Because without them, no crop. And the way most agriculture is going — big monocultures, lots of chemicals, no margins — it's killing them. So if a young person is listening to this and they're thinking about how to make a difference, that's what I'd say. Plant for the pollinators. Even if you can't afford the whole big regenerative system or whatever they're calling it now — just plant a few things for the bees. That alone is a contribution.

Interviewer 2: Thank you so much for sharing all of this with us.

Farmer: You're welcome. You're welcome. I don't usually talk this much. I think I went on more than I meant to. But you asked good questions and I — I just talked. So thank y'all for listening.

Interviewer 1: It was an honor.

Farmer: [Pause] Y'all going to come back? Maybe in season, you should come back. Pick some muscadines. Take a bottle of wine to your families.

Interviewer 2: We'd love to.

Farmer: Alright. Alright then. Y'all drive safe. And remember to take out what I asked you to take out.

Interviewer 1: We will. We promise.

Farmer: Alright. God bless. God bless.
