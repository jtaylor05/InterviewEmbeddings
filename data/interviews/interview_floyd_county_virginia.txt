Interviewer 1: Alright, I think we're live. Can you see us okay?

Farmer: Yes, yes I can see you both. Good. Let me just — sorry, I'm going to move this lamp, the angle is bad. Okay. Better?

Interviewer 2: Perfect. Thank you so much for making time for this.

Farmer: Of course. I was actually just writing when you called. I try to get an hour in the morning before anything else starts, but this morning the sheep had other ideas. So I'm a little behind. But I'm here and I'm glad to be here.

Interviewer 1: What were you writing about?

Farmer: [Pause] I've been thinking about the word natural. What it means, what we mean when we use it in the context of farming, and whether it's actually a useful word anymore or whether it's been so colonized by marketing that it's become almost meaningless. The organic label went that way. Sustainable went that way. Natural was never really a regulated term so it's been meaningless in a legal sense for a long time, but I mean philosophically — what are we pointing at when we say a thing is natural? And does that pointer still work? That's what I was trying to get at. It may become a blog post. It may become nothing. Sometimes I write things just to find out what I think and then I don't need to publish them.

Interviewer 1: How long have you been writing the blog?

Farmer: About six years now. It started as a way to process what I was learning in the early years of this farm. I came to farming later in life, and I found that writing about it helped me think. You know how some people talk through a problem? I write through problems. And at some point my wife suggested I put it somewhere public, which I was resistant to because I'm not really interested in performing. I don't want to be a farming influencer, I don't want a brand. But she said — some people might find it useful. And I think she was right. I get emails from people I've never met in countries I've never visited who say something I wrote helped them think through something they were wrestling with. That's enough for me.

Interviewer 2: What kinds of things do you write about?

Farmer: The philosophy underneath the practice, mostly. The history — how we got to where we are with industrial agriculture and what that history tells us about what we might do differently. The cultural dimensions of farming, which I find endlessly interesting. Questions about what we owe the land, what we owe the animals, what we owe the people who will come after us. I write about my own mistakes. I write about things I'm reading. I'm not writing how-to content — there are a thousand better resources for that than me. I'm trying to write about the why, and the what kind of world do we want, and where does farming fit in that conversation.

Interviewer 1: Let's start from the beginning. You grew up in the Dominican Republic?

Farmer: In a town in the Cibao Valley, which is the agricultural interior of the country. My father's family had been there for generations. And yes — we had animals, we had a garden, we grew things. But I want to be careful about how I describe this because I think there's a romanticism trap that I fall into sometimes and I try to resist. It wasn't a farm in any formal sense. It was just — how people lived. Everybody had chickens. Everybody had a few fruit trees. Most families kept a goat or two, maybe a pig, depending on what they could manage. You grew things because that's how you ate. It wasn't a philosophy, it wasn't a lifestyle choice, it wasn't regenerative agriculture. It was life. And I think that distinction matters enormously when we talk about what's been lost in the industrialization of food, because what's been lost isn't just a farming practice. It's a relationship to food that was embedded in everyday life for most of human history and has been severed, very deliberately in many cases, in a very short period of time.

Interviewer 2: When you say deliberately —

Farmer: The Green Revolution. Which I can talk about for a long time if you want me to, and I probably will anyway. [Laughs] So starting in the 1940s and accelerating through the 1960s, there was a concerted international effort — funded largely by American foundations, the Rockefeller Foundation being the most prominent — to transform agriculture in what we were then calling the developing world. And the stated goal was humanitarian: end hunger, increase yields, feed populations that were growing rapidly. And by the narrow measure of calories produced per acre, it worked. Yields went up dramatically. Certain famines that might have happened didn't happen, or were less severe than they might have been. That's real. I don't want to be glib about that.

But the costs were enormous and in many cases the people paying them were the same people the revolution was supposedly serving. The new high-yield varieties required chemical inputs — synthetic fertilizers, pesticides — that small farmers had to purchase. Which meant they needed cash, which meant they needed to enter the market economy in ways they hadn't before. Which often meant they had to grow commodity crops instead of diverse food crops. And over time, across country after country, you had this pattern: small diverse food-growing operations giving way to monoculture cash crop production. Traditional seed varieties being displaced by the new commercial varieties. Local food knowledge — which plants grow well here, which varieties do well in this specific soil, how to manage this pest without inputs — that knowledge starts to disappear because it's no longer being practiced and transmitted. And the soil — the soil that had been built over centuries of diverse cultivation — begins to degrade because you're applying chemicals instead of building biology.

Interviewer 1: And you watched some version of that happen where you grew up?

Farmer: Partly. The Dominican Republic didn't go through the Green Revolution in quite the same way as some countries — rice production was heavily transformed, but the small mixed farming in the interior persisted longer and in more places than it did in, say, parts of Central America or South Asia. But you could see the edges of it. You could see young people leaving the land because farming was no longer economically viable without inputs they couldn't afford, and moving to Santo Domingo or emigrating. You could see the traditional varieties starting to disappear from people's gardens. And you could see, even as a kid, that there was a knowledge gap opening between the older generation who knew how to do things and the younger generation who didn't see the point.

Interviewer 2: When did you leave?

Farmer: I came to the United States when I was twenty. Came to New York first, which is where a lot of the Dominican diaspora lands. I had family there. I enrolled at City College for a while — I was interested in literature, I was reading constantly, I thought maybe I wanted to be a writer or a teacher. And then — I don't know exactly how to describe what happened. The academic environment wasn't wrong for me, I don't want to sound dismissive of it. But I kept feeling like I was being trained to think within a set of inherited frameworks rather than actually think. If that makes sense. And I was restless. I left after two years and spent a long time doing other things.

Interviewer 1: What kinds of things?

Farmer: All kinds of things. I worked in a restaurant kitchen for several years, which I found I loved — there's a physicality to it, an immediacy, the feedback loop between what you do and what exists in the world is very short. I traveled — spent time in Mexico, in Costa Rica, in Colombia. Did some farm labor in Costa Rica that was my first real adult encounter with agricultural work and I found it — I don't want to say transformative because that's a cliché, but something shifted. I worked on a sustainable forestry project in the mountains of Chiapas for almost a year. I came back to New York. I worked in a bookstore. I met my wife, Yolanda, who is from Virginia — she grew up in the Roanoke area — and we lived in the city for a few years and both eventually decided we wanted something different.

Interviewer 2: What drew you to Floyd County specifically?

Farmer: We knew we wanted rural Virginia — partly because Yolanda has family connections there, partly because the land is extraordinary, partly because we'd visited and felt something. And Floyd County specifically — we found it by accident the way you often find the most important things. We came through on a drive, stopped at the farmers market on a Saturday, and I remember standing in that market thinking: this is a community that has figured something out. There's a density of people here who are doing serious work and thinking seriously about land and food and community and what those things mean. It's not uniform — Floyd County has its tensions and its contradictions like anywhere else — but there's a thread running through it of people who are asking real questions. And I wanted to be around people asking real questions.

Interviewer 1: And you found the land?

Farmer: We found the land. Sixty-two acres, about forty of which are forested. And I want to talk about those forty acres of forest because I think my relationship to them says something important about what I'm trying to do here. When we bought this place, a neighbor — very kindly, very helpfully — walked the property with me and pointed out the forested section and said: you know, if you clear this and put it in pasture you'd have twice the grazing land. And I understood what he was saying and why he was saying it. More pasture means more animals means more production. That's the logic of productivity maximization, which is the dominant logic of American agriculture.

But I didn't come here to maximize production. I came here to be in a right relationship with this piece of land. And those forty acres of forest — they're doing things. They're filtering water, they're providing habitat, they're sequestering carbon, they're maintaining the hydrology of the whole property. There are species living in there that I'm still discovering after eight years. To clear that forest and call it an improvement would be — it would be a category error. It would be applying a metric of value that doesn't capture what that forest actually is.

Interviewer 2: So your productive land is the remaining twenty-two acres?

Farmer: Twenty-two acres, yes. About fifteen in pasture for the sheep, and the rest in a small orchard and a kitchen garden and the homestead. It's not a large operation by any measure. We run about forty ewes plus their lambs. We have a mix of breeds — I've settled on Katahdin as the foundation because they're hair sheep, no shearing required, very hardy, do well on grass alone. And I've crossed some of them with Icelandic rams, which gives me a more varied animal with interesting fiber characteristics when they do have some wool expression. We also have about twenty fruit trees — apples, pears, a few plums, some persimmons — planted informally across the property in ways that interact with the pasture system. And the kitchen garden produces for our household and a modest surplus that we sell through a small CSA — eight families right now, which is about as many as I want to manage at this stage.

Interviewer 1: Tell us about the community you've built here — your kids, the people who come through.

Farmer: [Laughs warmly] The house is always full. We have three kids — the youngest is seventeen, the middle one is twenty, the oldest is twenty-four and lives mostly in Roanoke but is here a lot. And their friends have always been here. From the time the kids were small we had an open door, which means on any given weekend there might be eight or ten young people around — eating, helping with things, asking questions, just being in this space. And I think that's been one of the most meaningful things we've done here, honestly. Not intentionally meaningful — we didn't design it as an educational program. It was just our nature and Yolanda's nature to welcome people. But the effect has been that there's a generation of young people in this county who spent real time on a working farm, who know how to do things, who have some relationship to land and food production that they wouldn't otherwise have.

And beyond the kids — we have a community of friends and neighbors who cycle through regularly. Shared meals are important to us. Probably once a month we have a big dinner where we cook something from the farm and neighbors bring things and we sit for a long time. Floyd has that culture. People here know how to sit at a table and talk. That's not universal in the United States and it's something I noticed and valued immediately when we moved here.

Interviewer 2: You said Floyd has that culture — do you think that's specific to Floyd?

Farmer: I think Floyd has an unusually concentrated version of something that exists in pockets around the country. The combination of the old Appalachian culture — which is not warm to outsiders in all its expressions, I want to be honest — and the back-to-the-land influx that happened here in the sixties and seventies, which brought a lot of people with serious intellectual and philosophical commitments to land and community — those two streams have been living together here for fifty years and something interesting has emerged from it. There are families who've been in this county for eight generations and families who came here in 1971 from Boston with a copy of the Whole Earth Catalog, and their grandchildren go to school together and their families know each other. That creates a texture of community that's unusual.

Interviewer 1: Have you found it welcoming as an outsider — as someone from the Dominican Republic?

Farmer: Mostly yes, genuinely. There have been moments of friction — I don't want to pretend Virginia is something it isn't. But Floyd specifically — the culture here is such that strangeness is not automatically suspect. There's a tradition of eccentricity that offers cover to someone like me. [Laughs] And I think the farming has helped. If you work hard and take care of your land and you're generous with your neighbors and your table is always open, people see that. The land doesn't care where you're from, and eventually the community takes its cue from that.

Interviewer 2: Let's talk about the philosophical dimensions of what you're doing — how do you articulate your approach to the farm?

Farmer: I resist the word approach, actually. An approach implies a methodology, a set of techniques you've chosen from among available options. And I think if you begin with technique, you've already made a wrong turn. The question I think we should be asking is not: what's the best method? But: what kind of relationship do we want with this land, with these animals, with this food system? And the methods follow from the answer to that question. The reason I find regenerative agriculture compelling is not primarily because of the soil health outcomes, though those matter, and not primarily because of the ecological benefits, though those matter enormously. It's because regenerative agriculture, at its best, represents a certain philosophical orientation toward the living world. It says: I am part of this system, not its master. I will manage it, but I will manage it by understanding how it wants to work and supporting that, not by imposing my will on it.

Interviewer 1: Where does that orientation come from for you? Is it the Dominican Republic upbringing, is it the reading you've done, is it the farming itself?

Farmer: All of those and none of them individually. I think growing up around animals and plants in the way I did — not as a farm project but as ordinary life — gave me a baseline intuition that living things are not objects. They have their own logics, their own needs, their own responses to how you treat them. You can feel that. A chicken that is unhappy tells you it's unhappy if you're paying attention. A soil that's dying tells you it's dying if you're paying attention. That attentiveness is something I absorbed before I had language for it.

And then the reading — I've been deeply influenced by Wendell Berry, who I think is the most important American thinker on the relationship between culture and agriculture in the twentieth century, though he's not fashionable anymore in the way he once was. And Wes Jackson, who is asking the most fundamental question in agriculture: what would it look like to farm in a way that's native to a place rather than imposed on it? And going further back — Liberty Hyde Bailey, Albert Howard, the people who were thinking about these questions before the Green Revolution foreclosed the conversation for a few decades. And philosophers — Ivan Illich, who wrote about what he called counterproductivity, the idea that institutions developed to serve human needs eventually reach a scale where they undermine those needs. I think about industrial agriculture in those terms constantly. It was developed to feed people and it's now doing things to people's health and to the land that are the opposite of feeding.

Interviewer 2: How do you think about the tension between those philosophical ideals and the practical reality of running an operation that has to produce food and some income?

Farmer: I think the tension is real and I don't want to dissolve it prematurely. There's a version of this conversation that ends with: and it all works out perfectly, the right relationship with the land is also the most profitable approach and everyone can be happy. And sometimes that's true — I believe that regenerative practices, built up over time, do lead to more resilient and productive land. But not always, not immediately, and not without real costs. There have been years on this farm where a more conventional intervention would have saved me money and saved some animals. And I chose not to make that intervention because of what I believe, and I paid a price for it. I'm not asking for sympathy about that — it was my choice and I stand by it — but I want to be honest that the philosophy costs something.

And the income piece — we are not profitable from farming. My wife works. I do some consulting and writing. The farm produces a significant portion of our food, which has real economic value even if it's not cash income. And the CSA and the occasional lamb sales contribute. But we are not supporting ourselves from this land in a conventional financial sense, and I think that's a structural problem with small-scale regenerative farming that we need to be honest about rather than papering over with stories about farms that make it work.

Interviewer 1: What is the structural problem, in your view?

Farmer: The externalities are not priced. The things that industrial agriculture costs — the soil degradation, the water pollution, the public health consequences, the loss of rural community, the climate emissions — those costs exist, they're real, they're enormous. But they're not in the price of a chicken at the grocery store. They're distributed across society, across time, across ecosystems. And the thing that regenerative farming produces — healthy soil, clean water, biodiversity, carbon sequestration, a community that's connected to its food — those things have enormous value. But they're not captured in the price of a lamb chop either. So the market, as currently structured, systematically undervalues what we do and underprices what the industrial system does. And you can have all the right values and all the right practices and you'll still struggle to be financially viable in that environment. That's not a personal failing. It's a structural problem.

Interviewer 2: Is there a policy solution to that?

Farmer: I have complicated feelings about policy solutions, which I should be transparent about. I think government support for agriculture can be an important tool, and I use some of it — I've accepted grants and I participate in some USDA conservation programs, which I'll tell you about. But I'm also deeply skeptical of the idea that the solution to a structural problem created largely by government agricultural policy is more and different government agricultural policy. The commodity payment system, the crop insurance system, the regulatory frameworks that favor large consolidated operations over small diverse ones — those are all policy choices that have been made over decades and they have produced what we have. I don't have tremendous confidence that the same kind of thinking will produce different results.

Interviewer 1: What programs have you participated in?

Farmer: We're enrolled in an NRCS EQIP program for pasture management — it's a cost-share arrangement that helped us put in some fencing infrastructure and do some pasture interseeding. And I've taken a couple of small grants — one through the state sustainable agriculture program for a pollinator planting project, one through a regional land trust for some forest management planning work. I'm honest with myself that my resistance to grants is partly principled and partly temperamental. I find the application process genuinely demeaning in its design — it treats farmers as supplicants who need to prove their worthiness to bureaucrats who in many cases have never farmed. And there's a conformity pressure in grant-funded work, an expectation that you'll do things in the approved way and document it in the approved format. That's fine for some kinds of projects. For work that's genuinely experimental and philosophical, it's constraining. So I take them carefully and selectively and with my eyes open.

Interviewer 2: Tell us about the sheep operation specifically — how does it function within the regenerative framework you described?

Farmer: The sheep are the integration mechanism for everything else on this property. They're the tool I use to manage the land. We practice adaptive multi-paddock grazing — the pasture is divided into sections, the sheep graze a section intensively for a relatively short time and then we move them. The grazed section rests, sometimes for thirty days, sometimes for sixty or more depending on the time of year and how the growth is coming. During the rest period the plants recover, the root systems deepen, the soil biology does its work. And then we graze it again.

The key principle — and this is the thing that I think is hardest for people coming from conventional livestock thinking to internalize — is that the grazing is not a cost to the land. It's not something the land tolerates. Done correctly, it's something the land needs. The grassland ecosystem co-evolved with large grazing animals over millions of years. The grasses are adapted to being grazed. They respond to it by sending more energy into root growth, by exuding more carbon compounds into the soil to feed the microbes, by triggering a kind of regenerative response at the plant level that mirrors what we're trying to do at the farm level. A grassland that is never grazed, or is only lightly grazed, does not become a forest overnight, but it moves in that direction. The diversity declines, the productivity declines. The animals are part of the ecosystem, not an imposition on it.

Interviewer 1: What about the animals themselves — the welfare side?

Farmer: Central. I've written a lot about this. I think you cannot be genuinely regenerative while being indifferent to animal welfare, because an animal under stress is not participating in the system in the same way a healthy, calm animal is. There's research now showing that the gut microbiome of stressed animals is fundamentally different from that of calm animals — different microbial community structures, different compounds exuded into the soil through manure. The health of the animal and the health of the land are not separate questions. And beyond the pragmatic argument — I don't find the pragmatic argument for animal welfare entirely satisfying because it implies that if the welfare didn't affect productivity it wouldn't matter. I think it matters independently. These are sentient beings. They have a form of experience. What that experience is I can't fully know, but I have a responsibility to take it seriously.

Interviewer 2: There's something we wanted to ask you about specifically — you've written about the bird flu situation and your perspective on it is pretty distinctive. Can you walk us through your thinking?

Farmer: [Long pause] Yes. This is something I feel strongly about and I know my position is not popular in some circles, so let me try to state it as carefully as I can. The conventional response to avian influenza — and we're talking about a policy position held by the USDA and most veterinary authorities — is rapid and complete depopulation. If your flock tests positive or is in a zone where positive cases have been found, you euthanize. Everything. Immediately. Tens of millions of birds have been killed under this protocol in the last few years. And the argument is: you prevent the spread, you protect the broader flock, you contain the virus.

My argument is that this protocol is doing something deeply problematic from a genetic and immunological standpoint, and that the framing of the problem — as a containment problem rather than an immunity problem — reflects a kind of thinking about disease that I think is fundamentally mistaken. When you have a disease outbreak in a diverse, healthy, pastured flock and you kill every animal, including the ones that showed no symptoms, including the ones that may have had a robust immune response and recovered, you are eliminating from the breeding population the animals with the best natural resistance to that pathogen. You are, in a very literal genetic sense, selecting for susceptibility over time. The animals that survived and would have passed on their immunity are gone. The gene pool you're drawing from for the next generation has been filtered to remove the most resistant individuals.

Interviewer 1: But the concern is that the virus spreads to other farms before you can even identify which animals are resistant, isn't it? There's a public health dimension here that affects people beyond your flock.

Farmer: That's the argument, yes. And it has real force. I'm not dismissing the genuine public health and economic concerns about disease spread. But I think that argument assumes a concentrated industrial poultry model where birds are in extremely close quarters, where there's no natural immune variation because they've been bred to genetic uniformity for productivity, and where transmission dynamics are fundamentally different from what you have on a small pastured operation. When you apply a policy developed for industrial confinement poultry to a small outdoor flock with genuine biological diversity, I think you're mismatching the tool to the problem.

Interviewer 2: But even if your flock is small and pastured, the virus doesn't respect those distinctions. A sick bird is a vector regardless of how it was raised, right? The risk to neighboring farms seems real.

Farmer: Yes. And I want to be honest about that because it would be easy for me to sidestep it. There is a genuine tension between what I believe is the right long-term response to building flock immunity — letting the biology do its work, selecting for survivors — and the short-term responsibility I have to my neighbors and to the broader poultry-raising community in this region. I don't have a clean resolution to that tension. What I'm arguing against is the idea that the current protocol is obviously correct. I'm not arguing that I have a worked-out alternative that eliminates all the tradeoffs. What I want is a more honest accounting of what the depopulation policy is costing us in the long run, genetically and immunologically, because that cost is invisible in a way the spread risk isn't.

Interviewer 1: How did this become something you think about so concretely? Did something happen?

Farmer: About two years ago. There was a county extension meeting following some bird flu detections in the broader region. They brought in a USDA epidemiologist — and I want to be fair to this person, she was clearly knowledgeable, clearly well-intentioned — and she laid out the protocol very plainly. If you have a positive test or a contact case, you call the hotline, they come out, they depopulate. That's the policy and compliance is legally required.

And I asked her: what's the data on natural immunity variation in backyard and pastured flocks? What do we know about which birds survive exposure and whether that survival has a heritable component? And she looked at me — not unkindly — and said that wasn't really the framework the policy operated within. The framework was containment. And I said, with respect, I think containment is treating the symptom and not the disease. If we keep eliminating every bird that encounters this pathogen, we are perpetually starting over with no immunity base. We're on a treadmill.

Interviewer 2: What was the room's response?

Farmer: Mixed. There were a few farmers nodding. Others clearly thought I was being irresponsible, prioritizing my own philosophical commitments over public health. And I don't dismiss that view. If my approach contributes to disease spread that affects neighboring farms, that's a real harm. I take that seriously. But I also think the alternative — systematically eliminating natural immunity potential from domestic bird populations over decades — has costs that are mostly invisible because they accumulate slowly and counterfactually. We can count the birds killed by an outbreak. We can't easily count the resilience lost by the depopulation policy.

And what struck me about that meeting, beyond the substance, was the epistemological frame. The epidemiologist was operating within a very particular institutional logic — risk minimization in the short term, clear protocols, quantifiable outcomes. Which is not wrong as a way of thinking. But it's not the only way of thinking about a biological system, and in some contexts it actively prevents you from asking the right questions. That's the thing I keep coming back to. The policy is coherent within its own framework. The question is whether the framework is adequate to the complexity of what we're actually dealing with.

Interviewer 1: Let's pull back to the broader picture. Eight years here — how has your relationship with this land changed?

Farmer: Deepened. I don't think there's a better word for it. I know things about this property now that took years to accumulate and that I couldn't have learned any other way. I know where the water table is close to the surface in late winter. I know which part of the pasture the sheep avoid in the heat of August and which corner they always return to in the evening. I know which tree in the old orchard blooms first in spring, reliably, and what that tells me about the microclimate in that spot. I know where the fox den is and roughly how many animals are using it at any given time. This knowledge doesn't come from reading or from consulting experts. It comes from being here, paying attention, day after day, year after year.

And I think that's what I most want to say to people when they ask about regenerative farming — the transformation is not primarily in the soil, though the soil does transform. The transformation is in the farmer. You become a different person in relationship to a piece of land over a long period of time. More humble, more observant, less certain that you know what's best. The land teaches you that it has its own intelligence that preceded yours and will outlast yours, and your job is to learn its language well enough to be useful to it.

Interviewer 2: How does the writing fit into all of this — do the farming and the writing feed each other?

Farmer: Constantly, yes. I'll have an experience in the field — something a sheep does that I don't expect, or a change in the pasture I notice that I don't have an explanation for yet — and it sets off a chain of thinking that eventually becomes something I write about. And then writing forces me to make the thinking precise. When you write for an audience, even a small one, you have to actually finish the thought. You can't live in productive vagueness the way you can in your own head. And then sometimes I'll write something and a reader will push back in a way that genuinely changes my position. That's happened several times. I have a correspondent in New Zealand who's been farming for forty years and who disagrees with me about almost everything and whose emails are the most useful intellectual challenge I have. I've never met him. We've been arguing by email for three years.

Interviewer 1: What do you argue about?

Farmer: Mostly the scope of individual agency versus systemic change. He's much more radical than I am in his politics and much more pessimistic about whether small-scale farming practices can matter at a scale sufficient to address the problems we're facing. His argument, roughly, is: you and I and every farmer doing what we're doing represents a rounding error in global agricultural impact, and the fetishization of the individual farmer making right choices is actually a political distraction from the systemic transformations that might matter. And I have a lot of sympathy for that argument. I'm not sure he's wrong. My counter is not that I think I'm saving the world by managing my paddocks well. It's that the practice of living in right relationship with land, and modeling that, and passing it on — I'm not able to simply give that up in favor of waiting for a systemic transformation that may not come. The farm is also a way of being in the world, not only a strategy for changing the world. And I think that matters too, even if it doesn't scale.

Interviewer 2: When you say passing it on — do you mean the kids specifically?

Farmer: The kids, and all the young people who've been in and out of this place. I don't expect my children to farm — that's not a decision I can make for them and I wouldn't want to. My oldest is interested in environmental law. My middle one is exploring music and I support that fully. My youngest is — I genuinely don't know what she'll do and I think she doesn't know either and that's exactly right at seventeen. But they all know how to do things. They know how animals work. They know what soil should look and smell like. They know how to read weather. They know how to sit with a problem without immediately reaching for a technological solution. That knowledge will be useful in their lives regardless of whether they farm.

And for the other young people who've spent time here — I hear from some of them. One young man who practically lived here for two summers during high school is now studying sustainable agriculture in college. I got a message from him last year that was — that was good to receive.

Interviewer 1: What does the future of this farm look like to you?

Farmer: I want to get better at what I'm already doing, mostly. I don't have expansion ambitions in the conventional sense. I don't want more acreage, I don't want to grow the operation to a scale where I lose the intimacy I have with the land. What I want is for the pastures to be healthier in ten years than they are now, and healthier in twenty years than in ten. I want the orchard to mature into something that reflects this specific place — you can taste terroir in apples the way you can in wine, though nobody talks about it that way. I want the forest to stay forest. I want this house to keep being the kind of house it is, where people come and eat and talk and feel like they can stay.

And the writing — I want to keep doing that and maybe eventually collect it into something. I have essays that I think belong together that I haven't figured out how to arrange yet. There's a book in there somewhere, maybe. Yolanda thinks so. I'm still deciding.

Interviewer 2: What would you want someone to take away from reading it?

Farmer: That farming is a form of thought. That the choices you make in how you manage land are philosophical choices, whether you recognize them as such or not. That the crisis in our food system is not primarily a technical crisis — it's not that we lack the knowledge to farm well — it's a crisis of values and of what we think the land is for. And that recovering a different answer to that question is possible. It's not about recovering something purely historical — that's romanticization, which I'm suspicious of in myself as much as in anyone. But it is drawing on something that has been present in human culture across every time and every place: the understanding that the relationship between people and land is one of the most fundamental things there is, and that it has to be tended.

Interviewer 1: Is there anything you feel like we haven't gotten to that matters?

Farmer: [Thinks for a moment] The grief. I don't talk about this publicly very much because I think it can become self-indulgent, but there is a grief in this work that I think is real and important to name. When you pay close attention to what has happened to agricultural land in this country — and I've paid close attention, both by reading history and by looking at what our land looked like when we came to it and understanding what it had been through — there's a grief there. The soil that was here before European settlement, the knowledge systems of the people who managed this landscape for thousands of years, the diversity of life that has been lost, the farms that have been consolidated or abandoned — that's a loss. It's a real loss in ecological and cultural terms. And I think you can't do regenerative farming with full seriousness without feeling some of that loss. It's part of what motivates the work. You're trying to restore something that was real and that was valuable and that has been damaged. That's not nothing. That's worth getting up for.
